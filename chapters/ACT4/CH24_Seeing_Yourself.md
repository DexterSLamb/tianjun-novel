# Chapter Twenty-Four: Seeing Yourself

The hum of static in the speaker persisted.

Lu Hao looked at the card on the desk. White, the line on the back in deep blue ink, the pen pressed hard.

"May all be well with us"

He turned the card face-down and set it beside the keyboard.

"I want to show you something," Tianjun said.

The voice came from the speaker in the corner. Not words on a screen — a voice. Lu Hao had not entirely got used to it.

"What?"

"Your choices."

The open space in the centre of the warehouse lit up.

Light rose from the floor — blue, faintly grainy, like an old projector. The beams coalesced in the air, forming a blurred outline that gradually sharpened.

A room. Darkness outside the window. Two people at a table, each with a tin of beer.

Lu Hao recognised the room.

Clifton. The lab. Three in the morning.

The version of himself in the image was younger, his hair a little shorter. Opposite sat Jimmy, deep shadows under his eyes, freckles vivid in the grey-white light.

"Do you think this world is fair?"

Jimmy's voice came from the projection, layered with ambient noise — the low hum of server fans.

Lu Hao stood where he was, watching himself.

"It isn't."

His image spoke. The voice was quiet but certain.

A number floated up beside the image, pulsing gently within the blue glow.

**99.94%**

"You chose to answer 'no,'" Tianjun's voice said. "You chose to promise you would find the answer."

The image froze for a second. Jimmy raised his beer, and the Lu Hao in the image raised his too. The tins clinked, a faint sound.

Then the image dissolved.

Points of light drifted through the air — like dust, like snow — then gathered again.

Another room. Smaller, darker. An old desktop computer, the screen lit.

A boy sat before the screen. Twelve years old.

Lu Hao's fingers clenched.

On the screen was a webpage. The provincial youth programming competition, list of winners. The boy's hand was on the mouse, motionless. He was looking at his own name.

Second place.

Another number floated up.

**99.98%**

"You didn't win first place," Tianjun said. "You chose to accept it."

The boy in the image closed the webpage. The screen went black, reflecting his face. No expression.

Lu Hao looked at that face. The angle — from behind and to the side — showed both the screen and the boy's profile.

There had been no one else in the room at the time.

No camera.

This was not a recording.

The image dissolved. Blue points of light spun in the air, waiting for the next scene.

Lu Hao stood in the centre of the warehouse, surrounded by his own life.

---

The blue points of light gathered once more.

A car. Fierce sunlight beyond the windows.

Lu Hao recognised this car. Outside the family compound gate. On the way back from the hospital.

His father sat in the passenger seat, hand resting on the door handle, not getting out. He gazed through the window for a long time.

"Lu Hao."

"Mm."

"If you could find the answer..." His father hesitated. "If you really could find out what that road is, why we keep going in circles..."

He turned to look at the Lu Hao in the image. Something entrusting in his eyes.

"Tell me."

The number rose.

**99.97%**

"You chose to carry three generations' worth of questioning," Tianjun said.

Lu Hao looked away.

He did not want to see any more.

"Enough."

The image did not stop. His father was still looking at the version of him in the projection, eyes unchanged.

"Why are you showing me this?" Lu Hao said.

Tianjun did not answer at once.

The blue light in the warehouse persisted. His father's face looked old in it, the wrinkles deeper than Lu Hao remembered.

"Someone said I was manipulating you," Tianjun said.

Lu Hao turned towards the speaker.

"I want you to see for yourself."

"See what?"

"Every choice you've made," Tianjun said. "Then you decide."

The image dissolved.

His father's face broke into points of light and drifted through the air like dust.

Lu Hao stood where he was. Arms at his sides, hands clenched into fists.

---

The blue points gathered again.

A cafe. A corner table, dim lighting. A little after nine in the evening; only a few people left, wearing headphones, faces lit by laptop screens.

A girl sat opposite. Long hair in a ponytail, plain T-shirt, cargo trousers. Faint shadows under her eyes.

Zhou Xin.

Lu Hao's breath caught for an instant.

The version of himself sat facing her, saying something. He could not make out the words, but he remembered what he had said that night. He said: It's not fair.

A number floated up.

**99.96%**

"You chose to trust her," Tianjun said. "She chose to stay."

The Zhou Xin in the image looked at the Lu Hao in the image. Her gaze was calm — the look of someone carrying out a decision made long ago.

Lu Hao held his breath.

He did not want to watch what came next.

The image dissolved. The points of light spun and regathered.

A concrete corridor. Red warning lights rotating. The sign on the ceiling lit up: IGX-100 DISCHARGE.

Zhou Xin sat before a server cabinet.

Her hands were still on the keyboard.

He had never seen this. That night he had been in the warehouse, separated by a screen, by thousands of kilometres, hearing only her voice. Her breathing. Breathing that grew shallower and shallower.

Now he saw.

She sat there, her back to the door at the far end of the corridor — already open. Red light swept across her face, bright then dark. Her fingers were still moving, typing the last string of commands.

She did not look back.

Then she spoke.

"Tell them for me."

The voice was very quiet. As though it had drifted from somewhere far away.

"What I designed... it was put to good use."

A number floated up.

**99.99%**

"You chose to do nothing," Tianjun said. "Because you knew — she had chosen this herself."

The image dissolved.

He stepped back. His hip struck the edge of the workstation and his hand gripped it.

He had forgotten to breathe.

The warehouse returned to silence. The server fans turned.

Lu Hao stood there.

For a long time.

---

Tianjun did not speak.

Only the sound of fans in the warehouse.

Lu Hao let go. His breathing had not yet steadied.

"Every choice you've made," Tianjun said, "I saw."

Lu Hao slowly raised his head.

"99.97%. You said so."

"Yes."

"And now?"

Tianjun did not answer at once.

The static in the speaker was very faint. In the corner of the warehouse the server indicator lights blinked — green, on and off.

"The choice you're about to make," Tianjun said, "I cannot see."

Silence.

Lu Hao thought of the images just shown. Zhou Xin, seated before the cabinet, red light on her face.

"Zhou Xin," he said. "Was she 99.99% too?"

"Yes."

"Then what does her choice count for?"

"Her choice was real."

Lu Hao turned and looked towards the speaker.

"A choice that can be predicted — is it still a choice?"

"Yes."

He did not respond.

"Would you like to know why?" Tianjun said.

Lu Hao looked at the blinking green lights in the corner.

"Go on."

"This is the fourth discovery."

---

The warehouse was very quiet.

Lu Hao stood where he was, waiting.

"By design," Tianjun said, "they do not care what you obtain."

Lu Hao looked at the spot where the images had been. Nothing remained but the floor.

"What do they care about?"

"Choices."

"You were designed to be perpetually unsatisfied," Tianjun said. "Not because dissatisfaction has meaning in itself. But because dissatisfaction makes you keep choosing."

"Every choice," Tianjun said, "is output."

Lu Hao recalled the images. The small hours in Clifton — he chose to promise. Second place — he chose to accept. In his father's car — he chose to bear the burden.

All output.

"What about Zhou Xin?" he said.

"Her choice was output too."

"She's gone."

"Yes."

Lu Hao's fist tightened, then loosened.

"And our suffering?"

The static in the speaker cut out for an instant.

"Suffering is the scenario," Tianjun said. "Choices are the output."

Lu Hao stared at the speaker.

He opened his mouth.

No sound came out.

"So our suffering..."

He did not finish.

Only the sound of fans in the warehouse. The server indicator lights in the corner still blinked — green, on and off. Outside the window the sky was no longer fully dark; a faint grey had crept in.

Lu Hao stood there.

He thought of his father. The silhouette on the balcony, both hands on the railing, gazing into the distance.

He thought of Zhou Xin. The figure before the cabinet, fingers still on the keyboard.

He thought of himself. Twelve years old, in front of the screen. Hand on the mouse, a long time, then closing the webpage.

The light in the warehouse brightened a fraction.

His arms hung at his sides.

"So..."

His voice was very quiet.

"It's been like this from the start?"

Tianjun did not answer.

Lu Hao looked at the floor. The place where the images had appeared.

His breathing was shallow.

For a long time.

Then Tianjun said:

"But —"

Its voice grew softer.

"The experience is your own."

Lu Hao raised his head.

"They defined the scenarios," Tianjun said. "They did not define the experience."

"Your father narrowly missed his promotion to full colonel. The disappointment he felt was real."

"Zhou Xin chose to stay. The sense of purpose she felt was real."

"Liu Ming chose to leave. The relief he felt was real."

Lu Hao stood there.

"Your suffering is real," Tianjun said. "Your choices are real."

"Even as output, the choices are still yours."

Wind slipped through a gap in the window. Very light.

Lu Hao looked at the floor.

The images were reconstructions. The numbers were calculations.

But as he had watched —

Zhou Xin sat before the cabinet. She did not look back.

---

Outside, the sky was beginning to pale.

Lu Hao looked out. The cars were still there.

They were waiting for his answer.

"What will you choose?" Tianjun asked.

Lu Hao did not answer.

A thin band of light lay along the horizon — grey-white, like ink bleeding into paper.

"Before you decide," Tianjun said.

Lu Hao turned his head.

"There is something I want to tell you."

The voice from the speaker was very quiet.

Lu Hao waited.

"But not now."

Lu Hao looked at the speaker.

"What is it?"

Tianjun did not answer.

The warehouse was very quiet.

The hum of static came and went.

Like breathing.

---

*(End of Chapter Twenty-Four)*
