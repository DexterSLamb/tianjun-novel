# Chapter Twenty-Three: Echo

---

The beams stayed on for a long time.

Lu Hao stood by the window, motionless.

Then the lights died. The sky darkened. The cars remained, engines off, windows shut.

Someone knocked at the door.

Tianjun's surveillance feed switched to the entrance. The data stream on the screen stuttered for a moment, the cursor flickered, then returned to normal.

Shen Yao.

Lu Hao walked over and opened the door.

She stood on the threshold and gave a small smile.

"I only have an hour."

Lu Hao stepped aside. Shen Yao walked in.

"There are people outside."

"I know."

He closed the door.

"They let you in?"

"It's not me they want."

She took off her jacket and draped it over the back of a chair.

"Did the Abyss contact you?" Lu Hao asked.

"They did."

"Are you all right?"

Shen Yao did not answer. She stood in the middle of the room, looking around.

Three workstations.

One empty — the chair pushed into the corner, the desk wiped clean.

One gathering dust. A soldering iron propped at an angle on its stand, the power light dark, a few capacitors scattered beside it, a half-used reel of solder wire. A sticky note on the edge of the desk: "Node 7-23, check solder joints."

One with a screen still glowing.

Shen Yao stood before the second workstation for a while, looking at the sticky note.

Lu Hao watched her from behind. She seemed thinner than before.

She turned and walked to the window, gazing at the cars outside. The orange streetlamps had just come on; light fell across her cheek.

"You're the only one left."

"Jimmy's still here."

Shen Yao looked out of the window.

"Some things," she said, "will come to something in the end."

Lu Hao felt at a loss. "What do you mean?"

She did not answer. She walked to the sofa nearby and sat down.

Lu Hao poured two glasses of water and handed her one. She took it and held it in both hands.

Lu Hao sat down beside her.

Outside the window the sky darkened. The glow of the screen and the glow of the streetlamps fell on the floor — half blue, half orange.

Shen Yao set her glass on the ground, leant back into the sofa, her shoulder tilting slightly towards him.

Then she closed her eyes.

------

Three days earlier.

The curtains were drawn. Only a sliver of light came through the gap.

Shen Yao stood in the centre of the room, arms at her sides.

On the screen sat a figure, backlit, face indistinguishable.

On the desk lay a manila envelope, its flap open. Several yellowed pages. A transparent evidence bag containing a fountain pen.

Deep blue barrel, a scratch on the clip. As a child she had asked where the scratch came from. Her father said he had dropped it once, then patted her head.

"The complete chain of evidence from 2015," the voice said. "Including this. Your father used it to sign a great many things. Including the report that was tampered with. The original, not a copy. Fingerprints, ink composition, paper dating. Enough to overturn the conclusions reached at the time."

The air seemed to solidify.

"You refused to help us approach Lu Hao. We gave you many opportunities. But we can do this differently."

"How?"

"You leave him. Don't interfere with our dealings with him."

"In exchange, these items will turn up where they need to turn up. And we guarantee we won't touch you."

The voice paused.

"Or him."

Shen Yao looked at the strip of light on the floor.

"May I see him once?"

"You may."

"How long?"

"One hour."

Her gaze moved to the fountain pen. Deep blue. The scratch on the clip.

Eleven years.

"One hour," she repeated.

------

Shen Yao opened her eyes.

Lu Hao sat beside her, looking out of the window. Night had fully fallen. The streetlamps were on. The cars were still there.

"Sometimes I wonder," she said, "if what Tianjun says is true. If we really are code."

Lu Hao turned his head.

"Then the choice I'm making now — is it my own? Or was it written too?"

"I don't know."

Shen Yao turned to look at him. "Have you thought about it?"

"I have," he said. "It can't be proved either way."

"If it's true," Shen Yao said, "then what I'm doing now... what does it amount to?"

Lu Hao looked at her. The orange of the streetlamps was in her eyes.

He did not answer. His gaze was full of worry.

Shen Yao studied his face. Then she reached out, across the distance between them, and touched his hand.

She held it.

Lu Hao tightened his grip. Her fingers, cold at first, slowly grew warm.

"What's between us," she said. "Was that written too?"

"I don't know."

"But I felt it."

"Yes."

Her fingers tightened a little.

"That's enough."

Some time passed — she did not know how long — before Shen Yao sat up straight.

"It's nearly time."

Shen Yao stood. Her jacket was on the chair back. She picked it up but did not put it on straight away, just held it for a moment.

She put on the jacket and walked over to him.

Lu Hao stood. She did not move.

Very close. He could smell her scent.

She looked at him.

He reached out and touched her face. His fingers rested on her cheek.

She closed her eyes. Her lashes trembled.

Then she opened her eyes, took that hand, and gently lowered it.

"I have to go."

She took a card from her pocket and placed it on the workstation. White, with a string of numbers on the front.

"You already have one."

"One more for you."

She walked towards the door.

"Shen Yao."

She stopped.

"Where are you going?"

She did not turn round. "There are things I need to take care of."

"What things?"

"Once they're done, it'll be fine. Don't worry."

She turned to face him.

Lu Hao looked at her. Something was lodged in his throat, refusing to come out.

She waited a moment.

Then she reached out and patted his chest, her hand resting there.

"I've always been here."

Her hand fell away.

"Look after yourself."

She opened the door and walked out.

Footsteps — one, then another — growing fainter.

Lu Hao went to the window.

Shen Yao walked out through the park gates, her shadow stretching long under the streetlamps. Her car was parked across the road. She walked over and opened the driver's door.

She did not get in.

She stood by the door, one hand on the frame, her back to the warehouse.

The lamplight fell on her. Her shadow stretched a long way.

The wind blew and her hair lifted. She did not smooth it down.

Her shoulders were shaking.

Lu Hao stood at the window, his hand pressed to the glass.

She got in.

The door closed. The engine started. The headlamps came on.

The car pulled out of its space, passed the warehouse entrance without stopping. The tail lights became two red dots, growing smaller, disappearing round the corner.

Lu Hao stood at the window. The corner was empty.

The speaker sounded.

"Her car has left the surveillance zone."

Lu Hao turned.

"The Abyss?"

"No."

"...Tianjun?"

"There isn't much time."

"I know."

He walked back to the workstation and sat down. The screen glowed, the cursor blinked.

The white card lay beside the keyboard.

He picked it up and glanced at the numbers on the front. Then turned it over.

On the back was a line of writing — fountain pen, deep blue ink.

"May all be well with us"

His fingers traced the words. The pen had pressed hard; the indentations were visible on the reverse.

A faint hum of static came from the speaker.

"I'd like to talk to you," he said.

"So would I," Tianjun said.

------

*(End of Chapter Twenty-Three)*
