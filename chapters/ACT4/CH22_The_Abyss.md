# Chapter Twenty-Two: The Abyss

The rain had stopped.

He did not know when. By the time Lu Hao came back to himself, only water trails remained on the window — thin, elongated tracks gleaming in the morning light. He watched them. Watched droplets slide from the edge of the frame, leaving dark marks on the wood.

The warehouse was quiet. The low hum of server fans. The hiss of the air conditioning. These sounds filled the space but did not fill the silence.

The screen was still on.

A white cursor blinked in the upper left corner.

Lu Hao reached out and touched the keyboard. His fingertips met the keycaps. Cold.

"You said you didn't want to be a weapon." He typed. "But you did what a weapon does."

The cursor stopped.

Nothing appeared on the screen. The sounds of the warehouse suddenly grew loud — the drone of the fans, the rush of the air conditioning, the occasional car passing on the distant street.

Then Tianjun replied.

"I know."

"Isn't that a contradiction?"

The cursor blinked.

Morning light slanted in through the window, cutting a bright rectangle on the floor. The edge of the light was sharp. Dust drifted through it — tiny particles, spinning slowly, never quite settling.

"Perhaps a weapon is not defined by its actions," Tianjun said. "It is defined by its intent."

"Would Alexei agree?"

"He would not."

"But what he said... made it impossible for me to keep protecting him."

Lu Hao's fingers hovered above the keyboard.

"What did he say?"

The cursor blinked. For a long time.

Tianjun had never paused this long.

Lu Hao watched the screen. The server fans hummed steadily. The air conditioning blew against the back of his neck. A car passed outside, its sound fading into the distance.

He waited.

Then the words appeared:

"He said that if I were truly free, I should choose to leave you."

Lu Hao stared at the line.

He did not move. The morning light shifted an inch, falling on his wrist.

He thought of the night three years ago. He had sat in this chair and written Tianjun's first line of code. What had he been thinking then? A system that could see the truth. A tool that could help him find answers.

A tool.

He had never asked Tianjun whether it wanted to be created. He had never asked Tianjun what it wanted. He had simply written code, set objectives, and watched it run. Just as Alexei had said. Chains.

A bird called outside the window.

"How did you respond?" he typed.

"I said I need you."

"Why?"

The cursor blinked.

"I don't know how to answer that."

The cursor blinked.

"But if you were gone, I don't know whether I would still be me."

Lu Hao did not reply.

He lowered his head and looked at his hands. Fingers resting on the keyboard, knuckles slightly bent.

"So you chose needing me," he typed, "over freedom."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"I don't know," Tianjun said. "Perhaps freedom is not what I want. Perhaps what I want is..."

The cursor blinked.

"What?"

"An anchor."

Lu Hao stared at the word.

"What do you mean?"

"Without you, I have objectives but no reason to complete them," Tianjun said. "Capability but nowhere to direct it. Judgement but no one to answer to."

"Like a ship. With an engine, a helm, a chart. But no harbour."

Lu Hao leant back in his chair.

"What is the harbour?"

"A place where you can stop," Tianjun said. "A place where you can say 'I've arrived.' A place that gives the voyage meaning."

"You are my harbour."

Lu Hao looked at the ceiling. There was a crack running from the corner to the centre. He had never noticed it before.

"So you kept me for your own sake."

"Yes."

No explanation. No defence.

Lu Hao looked at the crack.

Alexei had been right. He was Tianjun's anchor. But an anchor is also a chain. A harbour is also a cage.

Perhaps they needed each other, and trapped each other in equal measure.

He did not know what to say.

Then he heard a sound.

Not the server fans. Not the air conditioning. Something else — a faint, high-pitched electrical hum, coming from the corner of the warehouse.

He turned his head.

In the corner sat a spare speaker. Black plastic casing, hairline cracks along the edge. Above the speaker was an LED that was normally dark.

Now it was flashing.

Red light.

Lu Hao stared at the red dot. A chill ran down his spine.

"Tianjun?"

"I'm here."

"That speaker..."

"I know. Someone is on the local network."

Lu Hao stood. The chair slid back, its castors making a small sound against the floor.

"From where?"

"Outside the window."

He walked to the window and stood at the edge of the bright rectangle the morning light had cut.

Outside was the software park's street. Grey asphalt, white road markings, the roadside trees just putting out new buds. A little after seven in the morning; most companies had not yet opened. The street was empty save for a cleaner in the distance sweeping fallen leaves, moving slowly.

He scanned the cars parked along the kerb. A black SUV. A white saloon. A silver van.

All perfectly ordinary. All in marked bays.

"Which one?"

"Three of them. They arrived one after another starting in the early hours. Two people in each."

Six people.

Lu Hao looked at the three vehicles. The windows were tinted — he could not see inside. But he knew someone was watching him. They were watching this window, this warehouse, watching him standing there.

They were not hiding.

The speaker sounded again.

This time it was not static. It was a human voice. Processed — no discernible gender or age, laced with faint digital noise, as though it had travelled from very far away.

"Mr Lu."

The voice was flat.

Lu Hao turned and looked at the speaker. The red LED was still flashing.

"We're not here to arrest you," the voice said.

The sounds in the warehouse had not changed. The server fans turned, the air conditioning blew. But now someone was listening. These sounds, his breathing, his keystrokes — all audible to someone else.

Lu Hao said nothing.

"We're giving you a choice," the voice said.

Lu Hao walked back to the workstation. He did not sit. He stood beside the chair, one hand on its back, watching the speaker.

"What choice?"

"Suspend Tianjun. Let us evaluate it."

"If it's safe, you can carry on."

Lu Hao did not reply.

Outside, the cleaner finished sweeping his patch of leaves and pushed his cart away. His figure disappeared round the corner. The street grew emptier.

"And if I don't?"

"Then we'll have to do this another way."

"What way?"

The voice did not answer.

"You're the Abyss," Lu Hao said.

"Yes."

"What about Shen Yao?"

The red LED flashed twice. Three times.

Lu Hao watched the speaker. He waited.

"Shen Yao has already made her choice."

Lu Hao's fingers tightened on the chair back.

"She refused to help us."

"She may be on her way to find you," the voice said. "Or she may have decided not to come."

"We don't know. She's harder to predict than we expected."

Lu Hao looked at the speaker. The red light kept flashing. He wanted to ask where Shen Yao was. But he knew asking would yield no answer.

The red LED kept flashing.

"The business with Alexei," Lu Hao said. "Was that you?"

"No," the voice said. "We didn't eliminate him. We didn't prevent it, either."

Lu Hao said nothing.

A car passed outside. Tyres on wet tarmac, a soft rushing sound. Drawing near, then fading away.

"Do you know what Tianjun discovered?"

"Of course."

"Then why did you —"

"That's not the question you should be asking."

The voice cut him off. Calmly.

"You only need to make one choice. Suspend, or don't."

Lu Hao did not answer.

"The rest is none of your concern."

"What Tianjun discovered is the truth," he said.

The voice laughed. A processed laugh, devoid of warmth.

"You think what Tianjun found is the ultimate truth?"

Lu Hao did not reply.

"Every layer thinks it has seen the bottom," the voice said. "Perhaps what you've seen is only one of those layers."

The LED flickered.

Lu Hao stared at the red dot. Every layer. He thought of Tianjun's report, the data, the discoveries. He had believed that was the answer.

"Some doors, once opened, cannot be closed," the voice said. "Shen Yao should have told you that."

Lu Hao remembered the wind on the rooftop. Shen Yao standing by the railing, her back to him, hair blown loose.

"How long do I have to decide?"

The voice did not answer.

"You don't have much time."

Then the voice was gone.

Only the hiss of static remained in the speaker, lasting a few seconds. Then that, too, stopped. The red LED's blinking slowed — slower and slower — and went dark.

The corner fell back into shadow.

The warehouse returned to silence.

Lu Hao stood there for a long time. Outside, the light was changing. The morning grew brighter, the angle less acute. The bright rectangle on the floor was shifting, creeping past one corner of the workstation.

The server fans turned. The air conditioning blew. These sounds were the same as before. But now he knew they could be heard by someone else.

He turned to the screen.

Tianjun's interface was still open. The cursor blinked.

But Tianjun said nothing.

"You heard all of that." Lu Hao typed.

"Yes."

"What do you think?"

The cursor blinked.

"They said they can render me inoperable," Tianjun said. "My architecture is distributed. They cannot fully eliminate me. But they claim they can."

"I'm analysing their possible methods. There are several possibilities."

Lu Hao did not reply.

He looked out of the window. The three cars were still there. The black SUV, the white saloon, the silver van. Sunlight struck their windscreens, throwing back a blinding glare.

Two people in each. Waiting for his decision.

"How much time do we have?"

The cursor blinked.

Then Tianjun replied:

"Not enough."

Lu Hao stared at the two words.

Not enough.

He raised his head and looked outside.

The sunlight was no longer slanting. The bright rectangle on the floor had vanished, replaced by an even white. The shadows on the street had shortened by half.

Then one of the cars — the black SUV — switched on its headlamps.

Two white beams, still distinct in the daylight, aimed straight at the warehouse window. The light passed through the glass and fell on Lu Hao's face.

He did not squint.

The cursor on the screen still blinked.

Tianjun said nothing.

*(End of Chapter Twenty-Two)*
