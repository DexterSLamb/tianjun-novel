# Chapter Two: Clifton

The rain in Clifton was the first thing Lu Hao grew accustomed to in England.

Not the hammering kind — something between fog and rain. The locals called it mizzle: so fine it was almost invisible, yet capable of soaking a person through in ten minutes. Not carrying an umbrella was a local tradition, because umbrellas were useless; the moisture crept in from every angle. When Lu Hao first arrived he still used one. Two months later, he gave up.

It was autumn 2018. He had just come to Clifton University for his master's.

The computer science department was on the third floor of the engineering building. The postgraduates shared a single room — a dozen desks crammed together, the window seats overlooking a stretch of grey-green lawn bordered by old oaks whose leaves were turning yellow. Lu Hao's desk was by the window, but he seldom looked outside. Most of the time, his eyes were fixed on the screen.

The room was never busy. The postgraduates kept their own rhythms — some came twice a week, others stayed until late every night. Lu Hao was the latter.

He found he liked it here.

Not Clifton itself — the city was too grey, the sky a perpetual oppressive lead-white, sunshine a rarity. What he liked was the state of being: alone, one computer, no one to disturb him, no one to judge him. If the code ran, it was right; if it didn't, it was wrong. No "atmosphere", no "emotional expression", no subjective rulings from a panel.

Clean. Pure.

He could stay until ten, eleven at night, then walk back to his accommodation. The university had assigned him a single room — small, but adequate. He didn't need much space.

During that period he had little contact with home. The occasional phone call to his mother — a few words of "I'm fine", "don't worry". His father never answered the phone; his mother said he was busy, on assignment. Lu Hao didn't ask further.

He was used to it.

—-

One afternoon, he was alone in the room.

Outside the window, the mizzle was falling again — barely perceptible, the sky as dark as evening though it was only just past three. Lu Hao had his headphones on, debugging a piece of code. He couldn't remember what he'd been listening to. Probably a white-noise channel. Or possibly nothing at all — just shutting out the world.

Someone stood in the doorway for a moment, then tapped lightly on the frame.

He pulled off his headphones, looked up, and saw a man standing there. Close-cropped dark brown hair, grey eyes, a scattering of faint freckles across the bridge of his nose. Blue hoodie, a pair of battered white trainers, the edges a bit grubby.

"Er... sorry," the man began, his voice uncertain. "I don't mean to bother you, it's just... I'm from physics next door, I saw the light was still on in here, so..."

He faltered, as though assembling his words.

"My computer's having some trouble. I've looked up a lot of things, spent a few hours trying, but I can't fix it. I heard the computer science people might be able to... I mean, if you're too busy that's completely fine, I can try something else —"

Lu Hao watched him talk in circles and couldn't help cutting in. "What's the problem?"

The man looked faintly embarrassed. "I don't know what happened — it was fine yesterday, then today I ran an update, rebooted, and... it won't come back. Black screen, then a load of error messages."

"What system?"

"Ubuntu."

Lu Hao nodded. Physics using a Linux environment for simulations — common enough.

"Come on, I'll have a look." He stood up.

The man blinked, apparently not expecting help so readily. "Really? I mean... you're sure it's not too much trouble?"

"It's not." Lu Hao was already gathering his things. "Let's go."

—-

The physics postgraduate room was messier than the computer science one. Papers and books were piled on every desk; the whiteboard was covered in formulae, some half-erased to a blur. The man's desk was in the corner, a monitor on it, flanked by stacks of thick textbooks with *Quantum Mechanics* and *Statistical Physics* on their spines.

Lu Hao glanced at the screen — a stream of error messages. One look and he knew the problem.

"Hang on."

He fished a USB stick from his pocket — carrying a bootable drive was a long-standing habit — plugged it in, changed the boot order, and began typing commands. Jimmy stood beside him, watching, understanding none of it.

In under three minutes, Lu Hao pulled the stick out.

"Try rebooting."

Jimmy did. The familiar login screen appeared.

"... That's it?" Jimmy stared at the screen, incredulous. "I spent all afternoon on this and you fixed it in three minutes?"

"Small thing." Lu Hao pocketed the USB stick. "The update was interrupted. Corrupted boot files."

The man let out a long breath, relief washing over his face. "Thank you so much. I'm Jimmy. Jimmy Ovens. You?"

"Lu Hao."

"Lu Hao?" Jimmy tried the name, his pronunciation not quite right. "How do you spell it?"

Lu Hao wrote the pinyin and the characters on a piece of paper.

Jimmy looked at them. "What do these two characters mean?"

"Hao means bright. Like moonlight."

"Moonlight?" Jimmy laughed. "Sounds like a poet's name."

Lu Hao said nothing and began gathering his things to leave.

"Wait," Jimmy called after him, a little awkward. "I... thank you. Really."

Lu Hao nodded and left.

—-

After that, Jimmy would come to find him from time to time.

Not about the system — that never gave trouble again. He came to ask the occasional programming question (physics simulations required a fair amount of coding), or simply to drop by and ask if Lu Hao fancied a cup of tea.

Lu Hao usually declined. But occasionally, he said yes.

Once, Jimmy turned up looking dreadful.

"I'm done for," he said.

It transpired that his research data had gone haywire. He was running numerical simulations of quantum many-body systems — each run took hours, and over three months he had accumulated more than two thousand data files. But his naming conventions were a shambles, parameters scattered across different folders, and now his supervisor wanted everything reorganised into a uniform format with batch parameter extraction for analysis. He couldn't make head nor tail of which file was which.

"I tried writing a script," Jimmy said with a grimace. "But the thing I wrote runs slower than doing it by hand, and it keeps throwing errors."

Lu Hao glanced at his code and nearly laughed — Jimmy had used three nested loops to do something a single regular expression could handle.

"Go back to your desk," Lu Hao said. "Send me the directory structure, the naming rules, the data format, and a few sample files."

That evening he spent two hours writing a script. Nothing complicated, but he accounted for every edge case, added logging, and wrote a brief set of instructions.

The next day he sent the script to Jimmy. Jimmy ran it. All two thousand-odd files were reorganised in three minutes flat.

"..." Jimmy stared at the screen in silence for a long time, then turned to look at him. "Do you know you've just saved my life?"

Lu Hao shrugged. "It was nothing."

"It's not nothing." Jimmy was earnest. "It really isn't."

Afterwards, Jimmy bought him a kebab — the Turkish place near campus that stayed open deep into the night, the lifeline of students on deadline. They sat in the shop, Jimmy gnawing a doner and saying, "I owe you a big one."

Lu Hao said, "Don't mention it."

Jimmy shook his head. "If you ever need anything — come to me."

He found that Jimmy was an interesting person.

Once, when they were discussing the way their supervisors marked work, Jimmy gave him a deadpan tutorial in the British "translation system": "If your supervisor says 'Not bad', congratulations — that's high praise. But if he says 'Quite good' —" He pulled a face. "Be careful. That might mean it's a disaster."

"What about 'Interesting'?"

"'That's very interesting' means 'What on earth is this?'" Jimmy sipped his tea. "It gets worse. If he says 'I have a few minor comments', that means start again from scratch. And if he says 'With the greatest respect' —"

"What does that mean?"

"It means he thinks you're an idiot." Jimmy shrugged.

Jimmy was from Mannwick, working-class family. He'd done his undergraduate physics there, then come to Clifton for the master's.

"What about your family?" Jimmy asked. "What do your parents do?"

"My dad's in the army."

"Really?" Jimmy looked curious. "Did you move around a lot as a kid? I've heard military families get posted."

"Not too much," Lu Hao said. "His posting was fairly fixed."

"Must have been very..." Jimmy thought for a moment. "Disciplined?"

Lu Hao smiled faintly and let it pass.

The autumn went quickly. The Clifton mizzle fell and stopped, stopped and fell; leaves turned from green to yellow, then from yellow to brown. Lu Hao's life was simple — lectures, coding, the occasional cup of tea with Jimmy.

He realised he had begun to look forward to those teas.

Not because the tea was especially good — the English habit of adding milk was something he hadn't quite got used to — but because of the way Jimmy talked. He had an easy, effortless humour, never forced, as though he could lighten anything simply by speaking of it.

Once, when Lu Hao complained that his paper had been sent back for the third time, Jimmy held his teacup and said, "Three times? You're lucky. My last one went through seven drafts. At the end my supervisor said, 'Hmm, slightly better than the first version.' Slightly." He held up his fingers a fraction apart. "That much."

Once, Jimmy asked him why he'd come to Clifton.

"Russell Group," Lu Hao said. "Good research."

"True," Jimmy nodded. "But with your grades, you could've applied anywhere. Why here?"

Lu Hao paused.

"I wanted to be somewhere far away," he said.

Jimmy didn't ask what from.

"I understand," was all he said. "So did I."

—-

One evening that winter, Lu Hao stayed in the room until very late.

He was debugging a piece of code that refused to run. Outside the window it had been dark for hours; the room was empty, the corridor lights dimmed.

His phone rang. His mother.

"Lu Hao, your father's in hospital."

His fingers froze on the keyboard.

"What is it?"

"High blood pressure — he's been on medication to keep it under control, but last week's check-up wasn't good. The doctor wants him in for observation." His mother's voice was level, but he could hear the worry beneath it. "It's nothing serious. Don't fret."

"How is he now?"

"All right. Just bad-tempered — won't rest properly. The doctor tells him to stay in bed; he insists on sitting up to read his files."

Lu Hao said nothing.

That was exactly what his father would do.

"If you get a chance..." His mother hesitated. "Give him a ring."

He hung up and sat there, staring at the code on the screen, motionless.

He didn't know what to feel.

His father was in hospital. High blood pressure. Not a serious illness, but not a minor one either. He ought to be worried, ought to ring home, ought to... do something.

Yet he sat there, and there was nothing inside.

As though something had been frozen.

—-

He didn't know how long he'd been sitting there.

When the door opened, he didn't even look up.

"Still here?" Jimmy's voice. "I was passing and saw the light... Are you all right?"

Lu Hao raised his head and glanced at him.

"I'm fine."

Jimmy came over and sat in the chair beside him. He looked at Lu Hao's face but said nothing.

A moment of silence.

"My dad's in hospital," Lu Hao said.

"Serious?"

"No. Blood pressure."

"Good." Jimmy paused. "Are you going to go back and see him?"

Lu Hao didn't answer.

Jimmy didn't press. He simply sat there. The sky outside the window was black; in the distance, the sparse lights of the city.

"Have I ever told you about my dad?" Lu Hao said suddenly.

"Not really."

"He's a soldier," Lu Hao said. "For as long as I can remember, he's been the same. Uniform on, spine rigid, always standing like a tree. He never laughed, never praised anyone. Nothing I did was good enough. Top of the class — he'd ask why I wasn't top of the year. Top of the year — why not top of the city. My whole life... I've never once heard him say 'well done.'"

He stopped.

"When I was a boy I learnt to paint. I won a lot of prizes. But always second. Never first. Once, a provincial competition, he took leave specially to come and watch. He stood at the back of the hall that day, watching me go up to collect my prize. Second prize again. He didn't say a word."

Jimmy listened, not interrupting.

"That painting — it was of him." Lu Hao's voice was quiet. "His silhouette, standing on the balcony. Every time he came home on leave, he'd go and stand out there after supper. I never knew what he was thinking. But I felt there was something in that silhouette. So I painted it."

"The judges said my painting lacked emotion."

He gave a soft laugh.

"After that I stopped painting. I switched to science. Maths, physics, computing. Those subjects have one virtue — the answers are definite. Right is right, wrong is wrong. No subjective judgement from a panel. No 'almost there.'"

"But you still think about it," Jimmy said.

Lu Hao looked at him.

"Think about what?"

"What he thinks of you." Jimmy's voice was steady. "You've done all this, come all this way, studying at this university... and you still wonder. Whether it's good enough."

Lu Hao fell silent.

"My dad's like that too," Jimmy said. "Quiet. Never any grand speeches. When he put me on the train, I saw something in his eyes, but he turned away so I wouldn't see."

He paused.

"Just because someone can't say it doesn't mean they don't care."

Lu Hao said nothing.

They sat there like that, talking for a long time.

"You should give him a ring," Jimmy said at last. "Whatever's between you two — he's in hospital. You should ring."

Lu Hao looked out of the window.

"I don't know what to say," he said.

"You don't have to say anything," Jimmy said. "Just ring."

—-

That weekend, Lu Hao phoned home.

His mother answered; his father was asleep. He said not to wake him — let him rest. Before hanging up, his mother said, "Your father will be glad you called."

He didn't know whether his father would be glad. But he called.

After that long talk, Jimmy knew something of his past. Not all of it, but part. It made Lu Hao feel lighter.

Some things weigh less once they're spoken.

—-

Spring came early in 2019.

One night, Lu Hao finished debugging the final version of Jimmy's dissertation analysis programme. It was already three in the morning. The lab was empty but for the two of them, the glow of the monitors casting their faces in a bluish pallor.

Jimmy produced two cans of beer from a drawer and handed one to Lu Hao.

"Lu." He leant back in his chair, staring at the ceiling. "Do you think the world is fair?"

Lu Hao popped the ring pull and took a sip. "No."

"Most people would say 'more or less' or 'hard work can change things'," Jimmy said.

"Perhaps."

Jimmy turned his head to look at him.

"My nan always used to say that if you worked hard enough, a working man's child could get ahead. She believed it her whole life."

"And then?"

"And then my dad spent thirty years in the factory. His pension barely covers the bills." Jimmy's voice was quiet. "I was the first in the family to go to university. They thought I'd change something."

Lu Hao didn't respond.

"But I've found that sometimes it isn't about effort." Jimmy thought for a moment. "It depends on whether they see you as one of their own."

Outside the window the sky was still black. The lab was silent save for the hum of the server fans.

"What about you?" Jimmy asked.

Lu Hao looked at the beer can in his hand and said nothing.

The silence stretched on.

"So many times, just falling short. There's always some reason or other." Lu Hao frowned slightly.

Jimmy watched him. "So?"

Lu Hao stared out of the window. It was a long time before he spoke. "I haven't worked it out yet. But this sort of thing... there has to be a way."

Lu Hao took a sip of beer.

"If you find a way," Jimmy said, his voice serious, "tell me."

Jimmy raised his can.

They clinked. The thin aluminium sound echoed briefly in the quiet lab.

"I will," Lu Hao said.

—-

Summer arrived quickly, and with it, graduation. Lu Hao had already accepted an offer from a Chinese tech company and was heading home. Jimmy had found his place too — a quantum computing start-up in London had taken him on.

Before they left, they met in a cafe near campus.

"So," Jimmy said, cradling his tea — milk, as always — "you're going back."

"Mm."

"Will you miss this place?"

Lu Hao considered. "I'll miss the rain."

Jimmy laughed. "You're the only person I've ever met who says they like the rain here."

"Thank you," Lu Hao said.

Jimmy looked at him. "For what?"

"That night," Lu Hao said. "When my dad was in hospital. What you said."

Jimmy waved it away. "That's nothing. You wrote the script that saved my dissertation — I just talked. Hardly comparable."

Lu Hao shook his head.

Jimmy grew serious. "When you helped me, we barely knew each other. You didn't ask a single question — you just came with me. I've always remembered that."

He raised his cup, as though making a toast.

"If you ever need anything — if you ever need help — find me."

Lu Hao thought he was joking.

"I mean it," Jimmy said.

Sunlight came through the window and fell on Jimmy's face. A rare fine day in Clifton.

"All right," Lu Hao said. "If I ever set out to do something, I'll find you."

Jimmy lifted his cup. "Cheers."

—-

**Five years later.**

Nanhai City, the small hours.

Lu Hao sat in a dim room, the screen's glow on his face. He was staring at a chat window. Jimmy's avatar was greyed out.

Five years. He had spent five years at the tech company, written countless lines of code, contributed to project after project, yet felt increasingly unsure of what he was doing.

He had seen too much.

Seen people less capable than himself promoted, because they "knew how to play the game".
Seen veterans of ten, fifteen years made redundant, because their "cost-to-value ratio" was too high.
Seen rules drawn up, revised, interpreted — always to the benefit of certain people.

He was coming to believe what Jimmy had said five years ago.

It wasn't that any one person was at fault. The rules of the game themselves were flawed.

But where, exactly, was the flaw?

Was there a better way?

He didn't know. But he wanted to.

And the first thing he needed to do was find Jimmy.

He began to type.

*(End of Chapter Two)*
