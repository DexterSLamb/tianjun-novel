# Chapter Six: Father

After the Tianjun test, Lu Hao went home.

The plane landed at three in the afternoon. Outside the window, ochre mountains folded ridge upon ridge. There was green in the river valley, but it was quickly swallowed by barren slopes. The cabin chimed with the landing announcement. Lu Hao switched off flight mode and his phone buzzed — a node status report from Zhou Xin. He didn't reply.

The drive from the airport to town took over an hour. The motorway followed the river valley, steep hillsides on both sides, the odd mud-brick house clinging to the rock face. The car radio played a local dialect programme, the host chattering about neighbourhood affairs, the volume rising and falling.

His last visit had been for Spring Festival the previous year. Tianjun had been just a few lines of code then. His father asked how work was going. He said "fine," and his father didn't ask again.

This time was different.

Tianjun had produced that number — 62.7%. He hadn't told the team he was going home. Jimmy was in England verifying data sources, Zhou Xin was optimising power consumption, Liu Ming had picked up a new project, Alexei maintained his silence. He needed a few days away.

Needed to see his father.

---

It was nearly five by the time he arrived.

Fourth floor of the family quarters, grey-white walls peeling in places. He climbed the stairs and knocked.

His mother opened the door and blinked. "Why the sudden visit?"

"Spur of the moment."

She headed for the kitchen. "Have you eaten? I'll put some noodles on."

"Not hungry. Had something on the plane."

"As if that's enough." The water was already on.

Lu Hao sat on the sofa. In the family photograph on the wall he was seven or eight, wearing a sailor shirt, standing between his parents. His father's uniform was immaculate, his mother smiling with a hand on his shoulder. His father had had less grey hair then.

From the kitchen came the sounds of boiling water and chopping. These sounds were familiar, like background music, unchanged since childhood.

The lock clicked. His father pushed open the door, carrying a few steamed buns. He saw Lu Hao and his step faltered for a moment.

"Dad."

His father nodded, set down the buns, changed his shoes. His movements were slower than last year; when he bent, his back arched slightly.

"When did you arrive?"

"Just now."

His father sat at the other end of the sofa. One empty seat between them.

Silence. His mother was cooking noodles in the kitchen; the knife fell on the chopping board in a steady rhythm.

"How's work?" his father asked.

"Same as ever."

His father nodded, turned on the television and found the news channel. The newsreader's voice filled the room.

Lu Hao studied his father's profile. Hard lines, high bridge of the nose, deep crow's feet. A grey short-sleeved shirt washed almost white, cuffs rolled to the forearms. On his forearms, several old scars. Lu Hao had asked about them as a child; his father only said "careless scratches."

The news turned to a report on veterans' benefits. His father stared at the screen and tapped a finger once on his knee.

Just once.

The noodles were ready. His mother brought out two bowls. She wasn't hungry; she sat beside them and watched them eat.

"You've lost weight," she said. "Are you staying up late again?"

"No."

"You definitely are." She turned to his father. "Look at his dark circles."

His father glanced at Lu Hao, said nothing, and bent over his noodles.

His mother asked whether he was eating regularly, whether his flat had air conditioning, whether he'd met any suitable girls. Lu Hao gave brief answers. She didn't seem to expect more — she simply needed to ask, like a kind of ritual.

After the noodles, his father rose and went to the balcony.

An old habit — standing there awhile after supper. He used to smoke. He'd since quit.

Lu Hao thought of that painting.

The one he'd done at twelve, of this same view. The balcony, dusk, his father standing there with a cigarette between his fingers. The judges said it lacked emotional expression. He hadn't known how to explain — he had painted every emotion he had. Silent, heavy, inexpressible.

His mother cleared the bowls. Lu Hao offered to help; she waved him away.

"Go and talk to your father," she said. "Lately he's... even quieter."

Lu Hao walked to the balcony doorway.

His father stood with his back to him, both hands on the railing, gazing into the distance. The balcony was small; in the corner a few flower pots held spring onions and garlic shoots. Beyond lay the other buildings of the compound, and further still the mountains, turning dark grey in the dusk.

Lu Hao stood there a moment before stepping out.

His father didn't turn round.

They stood side by side, half a person's width between them. The evening breeze carried dry heat. In the distance, children laughing at play — the sound drifting up high, then dissolving.

"Dad." Lu Hao spoke.

His father gave a quiet "mm."

"Back when you were... in the army, did you ever have a... an 'almost' moment?"

He regretted it the instant he'd asked. Too direct.

But his father didn't answer immediately. He looked into the distance, tapping a finger on the railing, as though thinking, or remembering.

A long time passed before he spoke.

"Yes."

Lu Hao waited.

"The time I was up for full colonel." His father's voice was level. "My name was on the list. The paperwork had been submitted, the division had approved it. Then word came down that one slot had been cut."

A pause.

"I was the one who missed out."

Lu Hao said nothing. He looked at his father's hand — on the back of it, a scar from barbed wire during an exercise years ago.

"There was another time. A third-class merit citation. Should have been mine. But it went to someone else. They said his family had hardships, he needed it more."

His father turned and glanced at Lu Hao.

"A commendation was fine too. One step short of a third-class merit."

The balcony fell silent again. In the distance, a car passing.

"Your mother asked me a question once," his father said suddenly.

"What?"

"Years ago. Just after your grandfather passed." His father's voice dropped. "She asked me: are we walking a path we can't see?"

Lu Hao's heart skipped.

"A path you can't see?"

"Mm." His father nodded. "She said her father spent his whole life in the factory, never got the chance to be a teacher. I was in the army, one step short of full colonel. You — always the second prize in painting."

A beat.

"She said: are the three generations of us walking the same path? A path that never reaches its end, no matter how far you walk."

Lu Hao's throat tightened.

"What did you say?"

His father was quiet for a long time.

"I said I didn't know." At last. "I told her — whether there's an invisible path or not, I don't know. But when orders come, you march. When a mission comes, you complete it. No asking why."

He turned to look at Lu Hao. In the dusk his eyes were deep, as though holding a great deal.

"Now I think," he said, "perhaps your mother was right."

---

The next morning, his father had a hospital check-up.

Lu Hao went with him. His mother wanted to come too; his father said there was no need.

The hospital was in town, twenty minutes by car. His father sat in the passenger seat, silent the whole way. The morning rush had just passed; one red light after another.

At a red light his father suddenly spoke.

"How's that project of yours?"

Lu Hao was taken aback. His father never asked about his work.

"Still going."

"Difficult?"

"Difficult."

His father nodded and said no more.

The light turned green. Traffic inched forward.

At the hospital: registration, queuing. His father knew the routine. He collected his ticket and led Lu Hao to the waiting area. The room was full of elderly people, the air a blend of disinfectant and old age.

His number was called. Into the consulting room. The doctor was a middle-aged man who seemed to know his father.

"How have you been feeling lately?"

"All right."

"Taking the blood pressure medication on time?"

"Yes."

The doctor asked a few questions and wrote out test orders. His father took them, thanked him, and left.

Blood draw, ECG, ultrasound. A queue for each. His father told Lu Hao to wait and took the forms to each department himself. Lu Hao watched him queue, hand over forms, roll up his sleeve, lie on the examination bed. His movements were practised, as though he'd done this many times.

All tests completed, it was nearly noon. They sat on a corridor bench to wait for results. His father closed his eyes as if resting. Lu Hao watched the people passing — patients, relatives, doctors, nurses. Different expressions on their faces: anxious, exhausted, numb.

"Dad." Lu Hao spoke.

His father opened his eyes.

"Do you regret it? Joining the army."

His father considered.

"No. Joining up was my own choice."

"And... the almost moments?"

His father looked into the distance. At the end of the corridor there was a window; through it a tree, its leaves glinting in the sunlight.

"There are regrets." Slowly. "But regret is a different thing. Regret means you think you chose wrong. This is more like... falling short."

A pause.

"I think sometimes — if that promotion to full colonel had gone through, what would it be like now? Perhaps still in the army, perhaps transferred somewhere else, perhaps..." He didn't finish.

The results came back. Blood pressure was under reasonable control, cholesterol slightly elevated. The doctor adjusted the medication and went through the instructions. His father listened carefully, nodding, then folded the prescription and put it in his pocket.

They walked out of the hospital into fierce sunlight. His father put on a cap. Lu Hao went to start the car. Cold air rushed from the vents; the temperature inside gradually dropped.

On the drive back his father spoke again.

"That path your mother talked about — I've thought about it since."

Lu Hao gripped the steering wheel and waited.

"Perhaps it isn't a path." His father's voice was very quiet, almost as though speaking to himself. "Perhaps it's a circle. You walk forward, thinking you're making progress, but you're going round. You reach a certain point and realise it's the same place your elders passed through."

He looked out of the window.

"Your grandfather spent thirty years in the factory, never became a teacher. I was in the army, one step short of full colonel. You..."

He didn't finish. But Lu Hao understood.

The car pulled up at the compound entrance. His father undid his seatbelt. His hand was on the door handle but he didn't get out straight away.

"Lu Hao," he said.

"Mm."

"If you could find the answer..." His father paused, as though weighing his words. "If you really could find out what that path is, why we keep going in circles..."

He looked at Lu Hao. In his eyes was something Lu Hao had never seen before.

Not expectation. Something more like an entrustment.

"Tell me," his father said.

---

That afternoon, his mother asked Lu Hao to help clear out old things from the storage room.

The room was packed with clutter. Lu Hao shifted boxes whilst his mother sorted through them. Most of it was worthless — old clothes, expired magazines, broken appliances.

But at the bottom of a cardboard box, she found a photograph album.

Very old, the plastic cover yellowed. She opened it. Black-and-white photographs. A young grandfather in overalls standing at the factory gate. A still younger grandmother with two plaits, smiling shyly. Photos of his mother as a child, standing on open ground, a row of crude bungalows behind her.

"This is what your grandfather looked like at the factory," his mother said, pointing. "He worked there for thirty years."

In the photograph the factory was spartan — low buildings, a chimney trailing smoke. A group of workers stood at the entrance. His grandfather was at the far edge, not tall, his expression solemn.

His mother didn't turn any more pages. She held the album against her chest and sat down on a box.

"He wanted to be a teacher," she said, very quietly. "His family was too poor when he was young — they couldn't afford it. Later, when he had me, he said no matter what, I had to go to school. The day I got into the teachers' college, he got drunk and told me: study hard, girl. Teach well."

She paused for a long time. The storage room was still; only the occasional sound drifted in from outside.

"He was a foundry worker — molten iron and sand moulds, that was his life. Thirty years, then retirement. Then they found lung cancer. The doctor said it was linked to the dust."

Lu Hao watched his mother. She was looking down, stroking the album cover, her fingers trembling slightly.

"In the last days, he kept asking." Her voice dropped further, as though afraid of waking something. "Asking why him. Why was it the man who'd spent a lifetime in the factory? Why had he worked conscientiously all his life only to get this disease?"

She raised her head and looked at Lu Hao. Her eyes were red but no tears fell.

"He asked me: I never slacked off, not once. Why me?"

"I couldn't answer," she said. "Couldn't then, can't now."

Lu Hao couldn't speak. He thought of Tianjun's number — 62.7%. Almost succeeded, almost escaped, almost...

"That's why I asked your father that question," his mother went on. "Are we walking a path we can't see? Your grandfather never slacked, but the path didn't take him where he wanted to go. Your father never slacked, but the path always turned at the final step. And you..."

She didn't finish. But Lu Hao understood.

Three generations. Three eras. Three lives.

All going in circles on the same path.

His mother placed the album back in the box and pulled out a few old garments, folding them neatly to one side.

"Did your father say anything to you?" she asked suddenly.

"A few things."

"He rarely talks about these things." She stood and brushed the dust from her hands. "But he cares about that project of yours. He doesn't say so, doesn't ask, but he cares."

She walked to the door, then stopped and turned back.

"All your certificates from school — he keeps them in his desk drawer. I once saw him looking at that painting of yours. He looked at it for a very long time."

"He never says. But he knows."

---

After supper, his father went to the balcony again.

This time Lu Hao didn't follow. He sat in the living room with the television on, taking nothing in. His mother washed up in the kitchen, water splashing.

His phone buzzed. Zhou Xin: Power draw down to 9.2 watts, stable for 24 hours.

He replied: Good.

A moment later, another message: Liu Ming's picked up a new project — intelligent timetabling system for an education firm. Ten thousand in advance.

He replied: You take the lead.

Zhou Xin didn't write again.

Lu Hao set down his phone and walked to the study door. It was ajar. He pushed it gently open.

The study was small. A desk, a bookshelf, a chair. The shelf held mostly military titles. The desk was bare save for a pen holder, a lamp, and a photograph frame.

In the frame, the family portrait.

He went in and opened the desk drawer. Inside, neatly arranged documents and notebooks, and a tin box. He hesitated, then opened it.

Inside were certificates.

His certificates. From primary school through to sixth form, stacked in chronological order. Some had faded, the edges curling. He turned to the very bottom. There it was — the second prize certificate from the art competition. Red cover, gold-stamped lettering, the paper yellowed.

Beneath the certificate lay a drawing.

The one he'd done. His father's back, the balcony, dusk. A pencil sketch, blurred in places. In the bottom right corner, his childish signature and the date: 21.5.2006.

The drawing had been well preserved. No creases, no stains.

Lu Hao stared at it for a long time without moving.

He remembered what the judges had said: lacks emotional expression.

He thought — perhaps the judges were right. There was no anger in this drawing, no sorrow, no joy. Only silence. The silence of a man, the silence of a family, the silence of a life.

But silence itself is an emotion.

The heaviest kind.

---

On the third morning, Lu Hao was leaving.

His mother had risen early to make dumplings — "dumplings before departure, noodles on arrival," she said. His father was up too, eating slowly at the table. The three of them barely spoke; occasionally his mother asked "too salty?" and Lu Hao answered "just right."

After the meal Lu Hao packed his bag. There wasn't much — just a few clothes. His mother stuffed in a jar of her pickled vegetables and a bag of red dates: "you don't eat properly out there; these go well with rice."

His father stood by the door, watching.

"What time's your flight?"

"Two o'clock."

"Leave early. Traffic."

"Mm."

Lu Hao shouldered his bag and walked to the door. His mother recited her reminders — look after yourself, eat on time, ring more often. He acknowledged each one.

His father reached out.

Not a handshake. A pat on the shoulder. One. Light, but heavy.

"Be careful on the road."

"I will."

Lu Hao turned and went downstairs. At the second-floor landing he looked back. His father was still standing in the doorway, back straight as a tree. His mother beside him, waving.

He waved too, then continued down.

Stepping out of the stairwell, the sunlight stung. He put on sunglasses and walked towards the compound gate. A few elderly men playing chess by the roadside noticed him and nodded. He nodded back.

At the gate, he turned for one last look.

On the fourth-floor balcony, his father stood.

The same as in the painting.

And not the same.

In the painting his father held a cigarette. Now his hands gripped the railing. In the painting his father gazed at the distance. Now his father was looking at him.

They held each other's gaze across four storeys for a few seconds.

Then his father raised a hand and pointed towards the gate, and nodded.

It meant "go." A hand signal common in the army.

Lu Hao nodded too.

He turned and walked out of the compound to hail a taxi. Before getting in, he looked once more at the balcony. His father was still there, his outline slightly blurred in the sunlight.

The car started and pulled away. In the rear-view mirror the compound building grew smaller and smaller, then vanished round a corner.

Lu Hao leant back and closed his eyes.

He thought: he might never be able to paint his father's back as it truly was.

Because that silhouette held too much — the promotion that was cut, the merit citation that became a commendation, his mother's invisible path, his grandfather's dying question, and himself, the son who was always one step short.

Too heavy for a single sheet of paper.

Perhaps too heavy even for Tianjun.

But he still wanted to know the answer.

---

As the plane took off, Lu Hao gazed out of the window at the city.

It spread across the river valley, buildings packed tight, roads crisscrossing. In the distance the mountains stood like a wall enclosing everything. Sunlight glanced off the river, a blinding glare.

He thought: how many people in this city are asking the same question?

How many fathers standing on balconies, how many mothers sighing in kitchens, how many grandfathers on hospital beds asking why?

He didn't know.

But Tianjun might.

Or at least it might help them ask a better question.

The plane climbed through the cloud layer. Everything below disappeared, leaving only boundless white.

Lu Hao leant back and closed his eyes.

Nothing played in his earphones; only the engine's drone, steady and constant, like a kind of heartbeat.

He thought: this trip home had solved nothing.

His father was still silent and restrained, swallowing regret. His mother was still fussing and reminding, hiding love in the details. He himself was still quiet and determined, carrying his questions wherever he went.

But something had changed.

He knew now about his father's almost. About his mother's invisible path. About his grandfather's why me.

Those questions were no longer private puzzles. They had become a shared pursuit.

The plane began its descent.

Lu Hao stowed his tray table and straightened his seat. Outside the window, clouds again, then land, city, roads — all growing clearer.

He took out his phone. Flight mode was still on.

On screen, the last WeChat message between him and his father — sent at Spring Festival by his father: "Let me know when you land."

He'd replied: "OK."

Scrolling further back, it was all the same. Brief, restrained, no superfluous words.

He put the phone away and looked out of the window.

The ground was drawing closer; buildings, vehicles, pedestrians resolved into sharp detail.

The plane touched down, wheels hitting tarmac in a judder. Applause broke out in the cabin — some people clapped at every landing, as though performing a ritual.

Lu Hao didn't clap.

He waited for the plane to taxi to the gate, for the seatbelt sign to go dark, for passengers to rise and collect their luggage.

Then he stood, took his rucksack, and followed the crowd out of the cabin.

The air bridge was heavily air-conditioned and he shivered. At the exit he turned on his phone. Signal returned and messages flooded in — Zhou Xin, Liu Ming, Jimmy, the work group chat.

He swiped them all away, opened his contacts, and found "Father."

His finger hovered over the dial button for a few seconds.

Then pressed.

The phone rang five times before the call connected.

"Dad."

"Landed?"

"Landed."

Silence.

"Was the journey all right?"

"All right."

Another silence.

Lu Hao drew a deep breath.

"Dad," he said. "That painting... I saw it. In your drawer."

Quiet on the other end for several seconds.

"Mm."

"It's in very good condition."

"Mm."

Lu Hao gripped the phone.

"I'll keep looking," he said. "For that answer."

His father said nothing.

But down the line he heard it — very faintly, a sound like a breath released.

"Good," his father said.

Then hung up.

Lu Hao stood in the arrivals hall, people flowing around him, the tannoy announcing flight information. He held his phone for a moment, then walked towards the exit.

Outside was Nanhai's summer — humid heat rushing to meet him.

He called a car.

It pulled onto the motorway. The city lights receded behind him.

He thought: Tianjun is still running.

Power at 9 watts, nodes online, data flowing, questions being calculated.

And he was going back to continue that question.

The invisible path.

The 62.7%.

The world of almost.

The car accelerated and merged into the night-time traffic.

Lu Hao looked ahead.

The road was still long.

But he had to walk it.

*(End of Chapter Six)*
