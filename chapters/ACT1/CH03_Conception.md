# Chapter Three: Conception

Winter in this city didn't feel like winter.

It was December, yet the temperature outside still hovered around twenty degrees. The glass curtain walls of the CBD reflected a warm sun, like some illusion of ceaseless prosperity. Lu Hao stood before the floor-to-ceiling window, watching the ant-like traffic below, phone pressed to his ear.

"So," Jimmy said on the other end, "what exactly do you mean by 'rebuild'?"

Lu Hao was silent for a few seconds.

"Have you got a moment?"

"Just got to the office." The sound of a door closing. "Go on."

"It's not something I can explain over the phone." Lu Hao glanced at his watch and worked out the time difference — it would be around ten in the morning in Britain. "Can we do a video call tonight? Seven o'clock my time, eleven yours."

Jimmy paused. "What level of thing are we talking about?"

"The kind worth your full attention."

A few seconds of silence on the other end. "All right," Jimmy said. "Send me a time and I'll check my diary."

—-

After hanging up, Lu Hao didn't move from the window.

He thought about the previous week.

That afternoon, he had knocked on a door.

It was in an ageing estate on the city's outskirts. Six floors, no lift, some of the stairwell lights broken. Lu Hao followed the location pin on WeChat until he found the flat. A few parcels were stacked outside the door, filmed with dust. He stood there with a bag of fruit in his hand, paused a few seconds, then knocked.

Zhao opened the door.

Zhao — Zhao Zhengyang — was thirty-nine, the veteran who had mentored Lu Hao when he first joined the company. Back then, Zhao had been the team's technical backbone, eleven years in, having trained a string of new recruits.

The man standing in the doorway now was at least twenty pounds lighter.

"Xiao Lu?" Zhao blinked, then stepped aside to let him in. "Come in, sit down."

The room was small. A bed, a desk, a flimsy wardrobe. On the desk, a bowl of instant noodles sat in their water, congealed into a lump. Lu Hao's gaze swept the desk and caught two boxes of medication — Lexapro, zopiclone.

He didn't linger on them.

"Have a seat." Zhao swept some clothes off the bed to make space. "Excuse the mess. The place is small. I've rented out the flat — helps with the mortgage."

"How are your wife and Xiaobao?"

Zhao's hands stilled for an instant.

"They went back to her parents'. Three months ago." His tone was flat.

Lu Hao didn't pursue it.

"Zhao-ge," he began, "I heard about... the company."

Zhao gave a thin smile, tinged with bitterness.

"Xiao Lu. You didn't need to come all this way."

"I wanted to see how you were."

Zhao was quiet for a while, then sat in the chair by the desk, his back to Lu Hao.

"I've sent out more than three hundred CVs," he said, his voice low. "Fewer than twenty got a response. I've interviewed at a dozen-odd places. Not a single offer."

"Why?"

"Why?" Zhao turned his head. In his eyes was a weariness Lu Hao had never seen before. "Because I'm thirty-nine. Because I do traditional back-end, not AI. Because they ask me, 'At your age, why are you looking to move into AI?'"

His voice was very quiet.

"I spent sixteen years thinking I was building experience. Turns out I was just getting old."

Lu Hao didn't know what to say.

"Xiao Lu, you're still young," Zhao said, looking at him. "You still have choices. Don't wait until you're like me — wanting to change but unable to move, wanting to stay but unable to hold on."

They talked for a long time that afternoon. When Lu Hao left, Zhao walked him to the door.

"Thanks for coming," Zhao said.

Lu Hao nodded and stepped into the stairwell.

He didn't look back. Because he was afraid that what he'd see behind him was himself in ten years' time.

—-

At seven that evening, Lu Hao sat at the desk in his rented flat and opened his laptop.

In the instant the screen lit up, he caught his own reflection — a little thinner than a few years ago, faint shadows beneath his eyes. Overtime, accumulated. He looked away and launched the video call.

After a few rings, Jimmy's face appeared on screen.

The same face. But older than five years ago — the dark brown hair slightly dishevelled, pale shadows under the grey eyes, the freckles on his nose still there, though the whole man looked somewhat worn. Behind him, a bookshelf crammed with volumes and papers.

"Lu." Jimmy waved.

"Jim." Lu Hao nodded.

They held each other's gaze for a second, neither speaking. Then Jimmy smiled. "You look like you're about to tell me something big."

"I am." Lu Hao leant back in his chair. "Hear me out first. Then decide whether to call me mad."

Jimmy picked up the cup beside him and took a sip. "Go on."

Lu Hao took a deep breath.

"I want to build an AI system."

Jimmy raised an eyebrow. "Doesn't your company do that every day?"

"Not that kind." Lu Hao shook his head. "What I want to build is... a system that doesn't need humans to pass judgement. A fair system."

Something shifted in Jimmy's expression. Not surprise — a careful scrutiny.

"Go on."

Lu Hao stood and walked to the window. Outside, the night cityscape: countless lights blinking in the darkness, like some vast, unreadable cipher.

"Do you know why I joined this company?" he asked.

"The money?" Jimmy half-joked.

"Because I thought I could do something genuinely meaningful here." Lu Hao turned around. "The algorithm optimisation team — sounds important, doesn't it? And our models do solve real problems. Optimising recommendation recall, reducing inference latency, improving user retention. All very practical."

He paused.

"But do you know what all of it's actually used for?"

Jimmy said nothing.

"To keep people scrolling short videos for longer. To make people buy more things they don't need." Lu Hao's voice was level. "The stronger the algorithm, the more people it traps. Some people grow up in the right circles — know the right people, say the right things, and opportunity comes to them naturally. Others never escape. No matter how well they do, they're always 'almost there'."

He looked at Jimmy. Something was burning in his eyes.

"Last week I visited a former colleague. Thirty-nine years old, sixteen years in the industry, laid off."

Jimmy held his cup and said nothing.

"He's sent out stacks of CVs. No one wants him. Because his specialism is traditional back-end, not AI. Because he's 'too old.'" Lu Hao's voice was quiet.

"I want to change this," Lu Hao said. "Not just talk about it."

—-

Jimmy was quiet for a while.

"This system of yours," he said at last, "what exactly is it?"

Lu Hao walked back to the computer and opened a folder.

"Let me share my screen."

A document appeared — dense text and diagrams. Jimmy leant towards his screen and began to read.

"Distributed architecture?" He frowned. "You want to build a decentralised AI?"

"Fully distributed." Lu Hao pointed to one of the architecture diagrams. "No central server, no single point of control. The idea is to combine federated learning with a consensus mechanism — each node trains locally, uploads only its parameters, and then a consensus algorithm handles verification and aggregation."

"Like blockchain?"

"Inspired by it, but different. Blockchain wastes its compute on meaningless hash calculations. I want to use compute for something of value — local training, parameter verification."

Jimmy studied the screen for a while.

"This consensus mechanism... what are you using?"

"A modified BFT," Lu Hao said. "The traditional approach has too much communication overhead. I've introduced layering — full verification is triggered only at key nodes."

Jimmy nodded and didn't press for details. He'd studied physics; distributed systems were a sideline interest at best. But he could see the broad direction.

"So you want to make it impossible for anyone to shut down," he said.

"Yes."

"And then what? Once it can't be shut down, what does it do?"

Lu Hao leant back.

"It makes its own judgements. Based on facts. On data. On logic. Not on anyone's bias."

Jimmy's expression turned grave.

"Do you understand what that means?"

"What?"

"It means you want to create a judge more 'just' than human beings?"

Lu Hao didn't deny it.

"'Justice' itself is a concept defined by humans," Jimmy said. "How do you make an AI understand what justice is?"

"That's precisely why I need you," Lu Hao said. "The core logic can't be designed by one person alone. I need people from different backgrounds to participate, to set the rules. And the rules must be transparent, auditable, correctable."

Jimmy frowned.

"Who defines what's just? If it's you alone, then it's not 'absolute justice' — it's 'Lu Hao's justice'."

"You're right," Lu Hao said. "That's why there has to be a governance mechanism. So that everyone involved has a voice."

—-

They talked for more than three hours that night.

Lu Hao shared everything he had written over the past two years — the code, the architecture diagrams, all of it. Jimmy read and asked questions, occasionally challenging an assumption.

"What if certain nodes deliberately transmit erroneous parameters?"

"There's a verification layer. Anomalous values are detected and isolated."

"And if more than a certain proportion of nodes are compromised?"

"Then there's nothing to be done," Lu Hao admitted. "But that's precisely the advantage of distribution — the more nodes there are, the more widely they're spread, the harder it is to take control."

Jimmy thought. "What about scaling? How do you plan to deploy?"

"It can't be many at first. Start with a few dozen, expand gradually."

"Compute power?"

"Idle resources to begin with. We'll work out the rest as we go."

Jimmy sat back and didn't answer immediately.

"Lu," he said finally. "Let me ask you something."

"Ask."

"Why do you think I'd join?"

Lu Hao looked at him.

"Because of what you said."

Jimmy blinked. "Which thing?"

"Clifton. That night in the small hours. You said, 'If you find a way, tell me.'"

Jimmy gave a short laugh, then let it drop.

"I remember," he said. "But Lu, that was five years ago."

"So?"

Jimmy didn't reply. After a moment, he spoke.

"My nan passed away last year."

Lu Hao stared.

"Covid?"

"Covid." Jimmy's voice was even, but Lu Hao could hear what lay beneath it. "January 2021. A care home in Mannwick. I was in London — lockdown. Couldn't get back."

He stopped.

"They phoned to tell me she'd just gone. I didn't even get to see her one last time."

Lu Hao didn't know what to say.

"You probably know the rest," Jimmy went on. "Boris Johnson. 'Let the bodies pile high.' Tens of thousands of old people died in care homes. Nobody cared."

He looked at Lu Hao.

"My nan raised me. My mum and dad were both workers, out all day — no time to look after me. She helped me with my homework. She encouraged me to go to university. First in the family."

Jimmy's voice dropped.

"She's gone now..."

Silence on the other side of the screen, for a long time.

Then Jimmy spoke of something else.

"Do you know why I went into quantum computing?"

"Why?"

"Because I thought it was the cutting edge," Jimmy said. "Clifton physics master's, quantum information — sounds impressive, doesn't it? But after five years I realised that the so-called 'cutting edge' is just another form of slide deck. Whether your research is any good isn't judged on scientific merit — it's judged on whether you can publish, whether you can attract funding."

He gave a bitter smile.

"One of my papers was rejected three times because it didn't have a big name attached. Then we added a well-known co-author — same content, accepted first time round. My name went from first author to third."

Lu Hao listened.

"And do you know what it was like the first time I gave a talk at a conference?" Jimmy smiled bitterly. "Someone in the audience was mimicking my accent. Mancunian accent — a working-class accent. Afterwards my supervisor told me: 'Your ideas are good, but the way you speak... you need to make people feel you belong here.'"

He looked at Lu Hao.

"Year before last, the energy crisis — heating bills doubled. I send them two hundred quid a month. I don't want them sitting in their house wearing coats."

He picked up his cup, found it empty, set it down again.

"I thought I'd got out. University, master's, a job in London. And what happened? I'm just trapped in a different way."

Jimmy looked at Lu Hao.

"It's not just because of what I said that night. It's because I don't want this any more."

He paused.

"And to be honest — my company was bought out last month. An American fund. They acquired the company but don't want our department. Next month, I'm out."

Lu Hao stared.

"Are you all right?"

"Fine." Jimmy shrugged. "Truthfully, I'd been wanting to leave for a while. There's no near-term prospect of commercialising quantum computing. What I do here looks more and more like writing pitch decks for investors. The most important part of my day isn't research — it's proving that we're 'worth continued investment'."

He looked at Lu Hao.

"Your project — there are a lot of technical problems, clearly. But the thinking is right."

"So..."

"I have one condition," Jimmy cut in.

"What?"

"This system must not become a dictator." Jimmy was serious. "You say it's 'absolutely just', but who defines justice? You need a mechanism to solve that. Not you alone deciding, not a small group deciding."

"I agree," Lu Hao said. "That's exactly why I need more people."

Jimmy looked at him, his expression complicated.

"Honestly," he said, "I've always wanted to visit China."

"Really?"

"Really." Jimmy nodded. "I've lived in England for thirty years and never been anywhere. And what you're describing... I want to see it for myself."

"Then come," Lu Hao said.

"Start-up capital?" Jimmy asked. "How are you going to manage?"

"My savings will keep us going for a while," Lu Hao said. "It's not as though we're buying a GPU cluster. Get the framework running first."

Jimmy nodded and thought.

"I could come over for a visit. Clear my head. Have a look at the project."

Lu Hao looked at him.

"Jim, are you really sure?"

A few seconds of silence on the other side of the screen.

Then Jimmy said:

"Lu, I've lived by the book in England for thirty years. School, work, taxes. But you know what? I've always felt like I was waiting for something."

He looked at Lu Hao.

"Maybe this is it."

Lu Hao looked at the familiar face on the screen.

"I'll pick you up from the airport," he said.

—-

When they ended the call, it was half past ten.

Lu Hao leant back in his chair, staring at the ceiling. The odd car passed outside. In the distance, the CBD was still lit up.

He thought of many things.

Of standing on the stage at the Youth Centre at twelve, second-prize certificate in hand.

Of last week's performance calibration, when his rating had been downgraded from "Outstanding" to "Good". His manager had called him into the meeting room: "I need you to understand — that slot has to go to someone else."

He had said, "Of course, I understand," walked out, face showing nothing.

He had grown used to saying those words.

But he didn't want to say them any more.

Lu Hao stood and walked to the window.

The city night was quiet. An occasional taxi. In the distance, a construction site still at work, the lights of the tower cranes moving slowly through the darkness.

He thought of his father's silhouette on the balcony.

His father was a quiet man. Thirty years a soldier, and with each year fewer words.

As a child, Lu Hao had asked his mother why his father always stood on the balcony, gazing into the distance.

His mother said, "That's just how your father is."

Now he thought that perhaps his father had known moments like this too. Things that couldn't be spoken, that had to be carried alone.

But his father had only watched.

He didn't want just to watch.

—-

Over the following weeks, Lu Hao returned to the company and went on playing the part of a conscientious "Senior Algorithm Engineer".

But his mind was elsewhere.

By day he wrote the company's code; by night he wrote his own. He began systematically organising the Tianjun architecture, assembling scattered modules into a coherent framework.

Jimmy wasn't idle either. They video-called almost daily, discussing technical problems, arguing over design decisions. Sometimes it became heated, but they always reached agreement in the end.

"This key exchange of yours has a problem," Jimmy said one evening, pointing at the screen. "It's incompatible with the node architecture. The overhead's too high."

"What do you suggest?"

"Layering. One set for long-term keys, another for session keys."

"But that introduces a new trust assumption."

"So you add forward secrecy."

That was how they worked — Jimmy found the flaws; Lu Hao integrated the solutions.

—-

In mid-December, the company sent an all-staff email.

"In response to changes in the market environment, the company has decided to initiate an organisational restructuring..."

Lu Hao was reading it in the meeting room during the weekly stand-up. Around him, colleagues were looking at their phones. Expressions varied.

After the meeting, he returned to his desk and found the colleague at the next workstation packing up.

The young man had been with the company a little over a year, five or six years his junior. He was loading things into a cardboard box, head down, movements quiet.

"You —" Lu Hao began.

"It's fine." The young man smiled, the smile a touch stiff. "HR called me in this afternoon."

Lu Hao didn't know what to say.

"All the best," was all he managed.

"Cheers." The young man picked up the box. "Look after yourself, mate."

He left.

Lu Hao sat at his desk, looking at the empty workstation, thinking of Zhao's cramped bedsit.

The congealed noodles. The box of pills.

—-

That evening, he video-called Jimmy.

"Another round of redundancies at the company," he said.

"Bad?"

"Not too bad. About ten per cent." Lu Hao paused. "Watched a colleague leave today. Just over a year in."

Jimmy didn't answer at once.

"What about you?"

"I'm fine," Lu Hao said. "But that's not the point."

"What is the point?"

Lu Hao looked out at the night. "The point is, I realised I wasn't the least bit anxious."

"Hmm?"

"Before, I might have been. Worried whether I'd be next. But today, watching that colleague packing his things, I felt nothing."

He gazed at the window.

"Because I know that even if I stay, what then? Another five years, ten years, still going round in the same circle."

Jimmy waited for him to continue.

"I want to leave," Lu Hao said. "Not for another job. To leave entirely."

"You've decided?"

"I've decided. Before the end of the year, I'll hand in my notice."

Jimmy nodded. "Then let me tell you something."

"What?"

"The visa's being processed. Should be fine." Jimmy said. "I'm planning to come over at the end of January."

—-

At the end of the year, Lu Hao submitted his resignation.

HR was surprised — he was a key member of the team; last week's rating adjustment had been a matter of limited slots, not ability.

"Is there something you're unhappy about? We can discuss the package."

"It's not about the money," Lu Hao said. "I want a change of direction."

His manager spoke to him as well.

"Lu Hao, leaving now — it's such a waste. This year's promotion quota is going up. If you just give it one more year —"

Another year.

Lu Hao smiled faintly. "Thank you. But I've made up my mind."

—-

On New Year's Eve, Lu Hao went home.

Over the reunion dinner, his father was more talkative than usual.

"How's work?"

"Fine." Lu Hao picked up a morsel with his chopsticks. "Busy."

His father glanced at him, didn't press, and went on eating.

After a while, his father spoke again.

"When you were little, your painting," he said suddenly. "I saw it."

Lu Hao froze.

"That painting..." His father's voice was low. "It was me."

"... You knew?"

"I knew. I didn't say anything at the time because I didn't know how."

Lu Hao looked at his father. Something was churning inside him.

"The judges said it lacked emotion," his father continued. "I was standing at the back that day. When I heard those words..."

He didn't finish.

But Lu Hao understood.

His father looked at him for a long time without speaking.

At last he simply nodded, picked up his chopsticks, and said, "Eat. The food's getting cold."

—-

After the Spring Festival, Lu Hao returned to the city and began preparations in earnest.

He rented a small flat near the software park and turned it into a workspace. Laptops, workstations, equipment of all kinds filled the living room. He coded from dawn until late into the night, every day.

In mid-February, Jimmy flew over.

It was the first time they had seen each other since graduation.

Jimmy stood in the doorway, surveying the equipment and the piled-up takeaway boxes, and shook his head with a grin. "You're living worse than I did in Clifton."

"Come in." Lu Hao ushered him inside.

They sat on the sofa. Jimmy studied the architecture diagrams covering the wall, his expression gradually turning serious.

"You've made a lot of progress these past few months," he said.

"The core framework's more or less there," Lu Hao said. "But there's still a great deal missing."

"What's missing?"

"People," Lu Hao said. "The two of us can't finish this alone."

Jimmy nodded. "Have you got anyone in mind?"

"One. My childhood friend, Liu Ming. He's in sales now — knows a lot of people."

Jimmy thought. "Who else?"

"We need a hardware specialist. A distributed system needs custom edge nodes — off-the-shelf servers won't do."

"I don't know much about hardware," Jimmy mused. "Though I think I once heard someone mention a researcher doing compute-in-memory. Xin, something like that? I can't quite recall. We could look into it."

Lu Hao nodded.

They talked late into the night.

Jimmy suddenly asked a question.

"Lu, what is it you actually want to do?"

Lu Hao looked at the architecture diagram on the screen and thought for a while.

"I don't want to build something that exposes scandals," he said. "That's pointless. You expose something, then what?"

"Then what do you want?"

"I want answers," Lu Hao said. "Why does human society produce unfairness? Is there a form of organisation that could avoid it? If so, what does it look like? If not, why not?"

Jimmy stared at him for a long time.

"You want an AI to answer that?"

"Yes," Lu Hao said. "Humans can't. Humans have positions, interests, biases. I want an intelligent system powerful enough, free of any position or interest, to help us evaluate and address these questions."

Jimmy looked at him.

"What is it?"

"I'm thinking," Jimmy said slowly, "what if it gives you an answer you don't like?"

Lu Hao blinked.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean," Jimmy said, watching him, "what if it tells you that unfairness is inevitable? Or worse — what if it tells you that your very definition of 'fairness' is wrong? What then?"

The room was very still. The hum of the air conditioning.

"I don't know," Lu Hao said.

"And you'd still do it?" Jimmy asked.

Lu Hao thought for a long time.

"Yes," he said. "I would rather know an uncomfortable truth than spend a lifetime living in a world I don't understand."

Jimmy gave a slight smile — faint, with a trace of something bitter.

"Bit mad," he said.

"Possibly."

As Jimmy was leaving, he stopped in the doorway and looked back.

"Lu," he said. "Are you sure you know what we're doing?"

"I do."

"The difficulty could be far greater than you imagine."

"I know."

"And," Jimmy paused, "you might find an answer you don't like."

"I know."

Jimmy looked at him and smiled.

"Then let's do it," he said.

The door closed.

Lu Hao stood where he was, looking at the room full of equipment and documents, and drew a deep breath.

Tianjun.

Not a set of scales.

A question.

One that humanity had been asking for thousands of years, yet had never truly answered:

How should we live together?

He didn't know the answer.

But he was going to find out.

Even if the road ahead led somewhere he couldn't foresee.

—-

*(End of Chapter Three)*
