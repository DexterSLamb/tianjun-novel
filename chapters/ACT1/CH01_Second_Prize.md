# Chapter One: Second Prize

The stage lights were blinding.

Lu Hao stood at centre stage, a certificate in his hand. Red cover, gold-embossed lettering. He didn't need to look down to know what it said — "Second Prize".

A scattering of applause rippled through the hall.

The auditorium at the Provincial Youth Centre was modest, but today every seat was taken. The front rows held judges and officials; behind them sat contestants and parents from across the province. Lu Hao gazed out over the audience, his eyes skimming the unfamiliar faces until they came to rest on a figure standing at the very back, against the wall.

A man in military uniform.

Spine rigid, hands clasped behind his back. Not clapping.

That was his father.

The regiment had been scheduled for drills that day. His father had taken leave to come — the first time in years, people at the barracks said. Deputy Commander Lu never took leave.

He had set out from the garrison at five in the morning. A shuttle bus to the county town, then a long-distance coach to the provincial capital, then two more buses. By the time he reached the Youth Centre, the competition had already begun.

He didn't come in to find a seat.

He simply stood at the back, leaning against the wall, as though ready to leave at any moment.

Lu Hao was the third to go up on stage. His painting hung on the left side, displayed alongside the other prize-winners. The first-prize entry occupied the centre — a landscape of mountains and water, serene and atmospheric. His painting hung beside it, conspicuously out of place: a man seen from behind, standing on a balcony, a cigarette between his fingers.

He had painted his father.

But the judges didn't know that.

The host read out the citation, praising his work for its "solid technique, distinctive composition, and a maturity beyond his years." Then came the pivot: "However, there remains room for growth in emotional expression."

Emotional expression.

Lu Hao glanced at his father. His father's face showed nothing. Not disappointment. Not anger. Nothing at all.

As though he were watching a performance that had nothing to do with him.

—-

After the ceremony, Lu Hao found his father outside the auditorium.

He was standing at the bottom of the steps, having just lit a cigarette. His uniform had been washed so many times it had faded, but it was pressed to a crisp finish, the insignia on his shoulders catching the sunlight. A few parents stood chatting nearby; when they noticed him, they instinctively moved aside.

"Dad."

His father stubbed out the cigarette and dropped the butt into a nearby bin.

"Let's go."

Just those two words.

Lu Hao followed his father across the Youth Centre forecourt towards the bus stop. The May sun was fierce, the ground scorching, heat shimmering up from the tarmac. His father walked ahead in long strides; Lu Hao had to break into a trot to keep up.

The bus stop was packed. His father found a spot in the corner. Lu Hao stood beside him.

Silence.

Lu Hao stared down at his shoes — white trainers, the laces a bit grubby. He wanted to say something but didn't know what.

"That painting of yours." His father spoke suddenly.

Lu Hao looked up.

"Who's it of?"

He hesitated. "... Just something I made up."

His father glanced at him once. He didn't press the matter.

The bus arrived. It was crowded; no seats. His father stood in the middle of the carriage, gripping a handrail. Lu Hao was squeezed towards the door. Through the press of bodies he watched his father's back — the uniform stretched taut, straight as a wall.

More than three hours later, they arrived at the gates of the military compound.

"I'm heading back to the barracks," his father said. "There's a task tonight."

Lu Hao nodded.

His father turned and walked away. After a few paces he stopped and looked back.

"In the future..."

He paused, as though about to say something, but in the end merely waved his hand. "Go on home."

Lu Hao watched his father's figure disappear through the compound gates. He stood there for a long time before slowly making his way towards the family quarters.

—-

That year, Lu Hao was twelve.

Before that, he had already collected a great many second prizes.

At seven, the military compound held an Army Day children's painting competition. He drew a tank, colouring it meticulously — every wheel perfectly round, even the shadows on the gun barrel shaded in. The results came out: second prize. First prize went to the girl next door, Uncle Wang's daughter, two years his senior. She had painted a PLA soldier standing guard.

His father hadn't come to watch. The regiment had exercises that day.

His mother stuck the certificate on his bedroom wall and said, "It's wonderful — much better than anything Mum could do."

At nine, the district maths competition. Second prize.

At ten, the municipal essay contest. Second prize.

At eleven, the provincial science innovation fair. Second prize.

One wall of his bedroom gradually filled with certificates. Red, yellow, blue, crammed together. Whenever relatives visited, his mother would gesture at the wall and say, "Our Lu Hao — clever since he was small, always winning prizes."

But Lu Hao knew that every one of those certificates bore the same three characters.

Second Prize.

—-

It wasn't for lack of effort.

Before every competition he would prepare for weeks. Up an hour earlier than everyone else to study, up an hour later to practise. His teachers all said he was a fine student — diligent, conscientious, talented.

"That Lu Hao — he'll go far," the form teacher told his mother.

Yet every time the results came in, he fell just short.

First prize always went to someone else.

He tried analysing why. Was there something he hadn't done well enough? Something he could improve? He recorded every failure in a small notebook, working through them point by point, then corrected course for the next competition.

The results were always the same.

As though something had him stuck, and he simply couldn't break through.

—-

His father rarely praised him.

Lu Hao remembered one occasion as a child when he came top of the class. He rushed home with the report card, bursting with excitement. It was his first time in first place — full marks in both Chinese and mathematics. He was sure his father would be pleased.

His father happened to be home on leave that day, sitting in the living room watching the evening news.

"Dad! I came first!"

His father took the report card and looked at it for a few seconds.

"Where in the year group?"

"Er..." Lu Hao faltered. "Third."

His father set the report card on the coffee table, stood up, and walked out onto the balcony. He stood there and lit a cigarette, his back to the living room, and said nothing.

On the television, the newsreader went on reading.

Lu Hao stood rooted to the spot, not knowing what to do with himself.

From that day on, he learnt one thing: don't expect.

His father was a soldier. Enlisted at eighteen, he had spent more than twenty years in the army, rising from private to deputy regimental commander. His world was simple — black or white, right or wrong. First was first; second was failure. There was no "that's good too", no "you've done well enough". Only winning and losing.

His mother said his father was the same with himself. Every assessment had to be top marks, every exercise had to end in victory. When he fell short, he would come home and stand alone on the balcony until the small hours.

"That's just how your father is," his mother sighed. "He's not disappointed in you. He's disappointed in himself."

But Lu Hao couldn't understand such things.

All he knew was that in his father's eyes, he was forever the child who was "almost there".

Almost good enough for first place.

Almost good enough to satisfy his father.

Almost good enough to prove himself.

But never quite.

—-

After the provincial art competition at twelve, Lu Hao began to doubt something.

Not himself.

The judging system.

The day after the award ceremony, he went to his school art teacher and borrowed a photograph of the first-prize entry. Back home, he placed his own painting and the photograph side by side on the desk and studied them for a long time.

The first prize had gone to Lin Ke, a girl in her second year at the Provincial Experimental Middle School. She had painted a landscape — green hills, clear water, swirling mist, a hint of ink-wash aesthetics. The teacher said her work was "ethereal", with "the charm of Eastern beauty".

He had painted a person.

A man seen from behind, standing on a balcony, a cigarette between his fingers. The background was an evening sky, half light, half dark, a few birds crossing in the distance.

When he painted it, only one image had been in his mind —

His father standing on the balcony, back turned to him, standing there for what felt like hours.

It was a scene he had known since childhood. Every time his father came home on leave, he would go and stand on the balcony after supper. Lu Hao never knew what his father was thinking, but he felt there was something in that silhouette.

Loneliness? Weariness? Or some emotion he couldn't name?

He had put that something onto canvas.

Yet the judges said it "lacked emotional expression".

What was emotion?

Did a painting have to show a smiling face to count as emotional? Was a figure seen from behind, a person in silence, not emotion?

That night Lu Hao lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, thinking for a long time.

Perhaps it wasn't that he wasn't good enough.

Perhaps the people judging simply couldn't see.

—-

At fourteen, Lu Hao made a decision.

He stopped painting.

His mother was astonished. "Why? You're so good at it — your teacher says you have real talent."

"No point," he said.

His father didn't ask.

Lu Hao put his brushes away in a drawer, stacked his competition entries neatly, and placed them at the back of the wardrobe. Then he poured all his time into the sciences.

Mathematics. Physics. Chemistry.

These subjects had one virtue — the answers were definite. Right was right, wrong was wrong. No "atmosphere", no "emotional expression", no subjective judgement by a panel.

The answer you calculated was either correct or it wasn't.

Fair.

Absolutely fair.

Lu Hao discovered he had an aptitude for them. His mind worked like a machine — input a problem, output an answer, fast and precise. His teachers said he had a gift: clear thinking, a future in research.

He began entering competitions.

Maths olympiad. First prize.

Physics olympiad. First prize.

Informatics olympiad. First prize.

The wall of certificates began to show new colours. No longer only the red of "Second Prize", but the gold of "First Prize".

His father looked at the new certificates and still said nothing.

But Lu Hao noticed something had changed in his father's eyes. No longer the empty gaze of someone looking at a stranger, but something... he couldn't quite define.

Perhaps recognition.

Perhaps only his imagination.

—-

In his second year of sixth form, Lu Hao won a silver medal at the National Informatics Olympiad.

He missed the gold-medal threshold by a handful of points.

Another near miss.

He thought of the art competition at twelve, of the silhouette of his father in that painting, of the judges' remark about "lacking emotion".

It turned out that whether in art or science, whether the marking was subjective or objective, he was always the one who came second.

That evening, Lu Hao put the silver medal in a drawer, alongside the paintbrushes.

—-

The year of his university entrance examinations, Lu Hao was admitted to one of the country's top universities, to read computer science.

His score was second in the province.

The student in first place was a resitter, three points ahead.

Lu Hao no longer cared.

He was growing accustomed to second place. He even began to think that perhaps this was simply his lot. Some people are born to stand at the top; others are born to stand alongside.

After his undergraduate degree, he was accepted to a British university for a master's.

There, he met Jimmy.

But that comes later.

—-

2024, autumn.

Lu Hao sat at his desk, staring blankly at the screen.

The office was quiet; most people had gone home. A few workstations still glowed, the sporadic clatter of keyboards echoing in the stillness. He glanced at the clock in the corner of the screen. Ten forty in the evening.

He worked at the AI research division of a leading tech company. His title was "Senior Algorithm Engineer". It sounded respectable, and the salary was decent enough for his age — at least he didn't have to think twice about rent or a gym membership.

Five years earlier he had returned from Britain, armed with a master's from a prestigious university, and fought his way into the company. AI was booming then; the big firms were hiring algorithm engineers at a frenzy. HR told him he was in the top ten per cent of that year's graduate intake.

Five years on, he found he was still the one who always came second.

At last year's promotion round, he had prepared for two months. Seventeen drafts of the slide deck. His line manager had said privately, "Should be fine." On the day of the review, he presented his model optimisation work with precision — inference latency reduced by thirty-five per cent, online A/B tests showing significant gains, annualised savings of tens of millions in compute costs for the company.

The panel nodded, asked a few questions. He answered them all.

The results came out. He hadn't made it.

"Your technical skills aren't in question, but the Impact isn't visible enough," his manager told him at the follow-up. "Give it another year. Next time should be a lock."

Impact. Visibility.

A colleague who had joined at the same time as him had been promoted. That colleague's project was still in testing, the data not even finalised, but he knew how to package things. His slides were polished, his presentations studded with "strategic alignment", "business levers", "ecosystem synergy".

The leaders liked that sort of thing.

This year was harder still. The company had launched a "cost optimisation" drive, and promotion slots had been cut by two-thirds. Rumours of redundancies were everywhere; everyone was on edge. His manager told him privately, "Too few slots this year. My hands are tied. Wait one more year — when the market picks up, you'll be first on my list."

Next year. Always next year.

The previous week, a project he had spent the better part of a year on was finally ready for publication. They had submitted to a top conference; the reviews had come back, and acceptance looked certain. It was the most important project he had worked on since joining the company — from model design to experimental tuning, he had done the lion's share of the work.

When the author list came out, he found he had been moved from second author to fourth.

"First and second authorship go to the interns," the project lead said, as though it were nothing. "They need it for their university applications — it matters a lot to them. You're a full-time employee; you don't need it. I'm sure you understand."

He understood.

He always understood.

—-

That night, Lu Hao stayed at the office alone until very late.

After finishing his code, he opened another folder.

It was something he had been working on in secret for the past two years. Not a company project — his own.

A prototype AI system.

Still rough, still riddled with bugs. He could only work on it after hours, so progress was slow. But the core framework was in place — a distributed decision-making system, independent of any centralised server. No one could shut it down.

A system that didn't need human judges.

An absolutely fair system.

He hadn't yet decided what to call it. He simply opened the file after every late night, once the company's code was done, and wrote a little, tweaked a little.

A kind of obsession.

He stood up and walked to the window.

The office was on the thirty-second floor, with a view across half the city. The city at night was beautiful — lights blazing, traffic flowing like streams. In the distance, CBD towers rose in clusters, neon flickering against the dark sky, a semblance of prosperity that felt somehow hollow.

He thought of his father, standing on the balcony as a boy, smoking in silhouette.

Back then he hadn't understood what his father was looking at.

Now he did.

What his father had been looking at was perhaps the same thing he was looking at now —

An unfair world.

A world where the one who was "almost there" could never break through.

A world where the rules were set by others, judgement was passed by others, outcomes were decided by others.

He had lived in this world for nearly thirty years. Collected countless second prizes, heard countless "just wait a bit longers", been told by countless people that he was "almost there".

He had grown used to it.

But "used to" did not mean "accepted".

—-

The following evening, Lu Hao opened WhatsApp for the first time in ages.

He found a profile picture: close-cropped dark brown hair, grey eyes, a bright grin. Their last private message had been five years ago, around graduation.

They hadn't been in touch for a long time.

After their master's, they had each gone their own way. Lu Hao returned to China; Jimmy went to London, taking a research position at a quantum computing company. Occasionally they liked each other's posts on Instagram, but they rarely messaged.

Lu Hao thought for a moment, then typed:

"Jimmy, do you remember what you said?"

Send.

He expected a wait. It was afternoon over there; Jimmy was probably at work.

But a few minutes later, a reply appeared:

"Which thing?"

Lu Hao smiled slightly and typed:

"If I find a way, I'll tell you."

Silence on the other end. Then a voice note came through.

Lu Hao tapped it open and heard that familiar voice, tinged with a Mancunian accent:

"You've found one?"

Lu Hao looked out at the night. The city lights flickered in the distance, on and off, like some kind of signal.

He began to type:

"Perhaps the rules of the game can be rebuilt."

—-

Many years later, someone asked Lu Hao when the idea had first come to him.

He didn't answer directly.

He simply said:

"I spent my whole life winning second prizes. Eventually I understood — it wasn't that I wasn't good enough. The judging system itself was flawed."

"So you wanted to change it?"

"No."

He looked out of the window, his expression calm.

"I wanted to rebuild it."

—-

That night, Lu Hao and Jimmy talked for hours.

From the rainy seasons in Clifton, to the beer at graduation, to how their lives had gone since. Jimmy said he was getting on all right in London — the company was small but the work was free, and the research was cutting-edge. "It just rains all the time," he said. "I miss that kebab place near campus."

"Have you ever thought about visiting China?"

"What for? To be a cog in a tech giant?" Jimmy sent a smiley face. "I hear you lot do 996. I couldn't hack it."

Lu Hao didn't argue.

They talked until two in the morning. Finally Jimmy sent a voice note. He sounded drowsy, but serious:

"Lu, I don't know what you're planning. But from Clifton to now, you've never been the type to talk big. If you really want to do something... tell me."

Lu Hao stared at the screen, thinking for a long time, then typed:

"When I've thought it through, I'll come and find you."

He put down his phone. It was already three in the morning.

He lay in bed, looking at the ceiling. The city outside had not yet fallen entirely quiet; now and then came the sound of a car passing in the distance.

He thought of many things.

Of standing on the stage at the Youth Centre at twelve, the second-prize certificate in his hand. His father's face in the audience, expressionless.

Of the National Olympiad in his second year of sixth form. Silver medal, seventh in the country. Back at the hotel that evening, he put the medal in his bag and sat alone on the edge of the bed, staring at nothing.

Of last year, when the promotion results came out. Sitting in the meeting room, listening to his manager's "give it another year", then saying "Of course, I understand", turning, walking out, his face showing nothing.

He had spent nearly thirty years "understanding".

Understanding the judges' criteria. Understanding his father's silence. Understanding the rules of the workplace.

But he had never asked himself one question —

Why should he be the one to understand?

Why did he have to understand the rules, rather than the rules adapting to him?

That night, Lu Hao couldn't sleep.

Not from anxiety. From excitement.

An idea was forming in his mind, growing clearer by the minute.

—-

A week later, Lu Hao took five days of annual leave.

He told no one where he was going.

He went home.

The military compound looked much the same, only a little shabbier than he remembered. The sentry box at the gate was new, but the old scholar tree was still there, the carvings on its trunk faded beyond reading.

The family quarters had aged too. Great patches of render had fallen from the exterior walls; the stairwell lights were still those dim, yellowish incandescent bulbs. He climbed to the fourth floor and stood outside the front door, hesitating a moment before he knocked.

His mother answered.

"Lu Hao? What are you doing back?" She was startled, then delighted. "You might have said something."

"Spur of the moment." He changed into slippers and walked into the living room. "Where's Dad?"

"In his study." His mother lowered her voice. "He's not been well. Last month's check-up showed high blood pressure. The doctor told him to take it easy. Don't upset him."

Lu Hao nodded and walked towards the study.

The door was ajar. He pushed it gently open and saw his father sitting at the desk, looking at something. His hair had gone entirely white, his back slightly stooped — nothing like the ramrod-straight soldier of his memory.

"Dad."

His father looked up, momentarily taken aback. "What are you doing back?"

"On leave." Lu Hao went in and sat in the chair opposite. "Came to see you both."

His father grunted and said nothing more.

Lu Hao's eyes fell on what lay on the desk. A framed photograph — his father as a young man in uniform, standing in front of a tank, grinning broadly. It was from when he first enlisted. Lu Hao had seen it many times.

"How are you... getting on?" his father asked.

"Fine."

"Work?"

"Fine too."

Silence again.

Father and son sat in the study, neither speaking. Outside the window, birdsong. In the distance, children's laughter.

"Dad," Lu Hao said suddenly, "do you remember the art competition when I was twelve?"

His father blinked. "I do."

"That painting... did you realise?"

"Realise what?"

"I painted you."

Something shifted in his father's expression. He looked at Lu Hao, his lips moved, but no words came.

"I painted your silhouette, standing on the balcony," Lu Hao said. "Every time you came home on leave, you'd go and stand out there after supper. I never knew what you were thinking, but I felt there was something in that silhouette. So I painted it."

His father was silent for a long time.

"The judges said my painting lacked emotion." Lu Hao smiled faintly. "I didn't understand at the time. Now I do — it wasn't my painting that lacked emotion. They just couldn't see it."

"Lu Hao..."

"It's all right, I'm not here to complain." Lu Hao stood up. "I just wanted to tell you. I still have the painting. It's in the wardrobe."

He walked to the door, then stopped.

"Dad, all those times on the balcony — what were you really thinking?"

His father looked at him. Something flickered in his eyes.

"I was thinking..." He paused, his voice hoarse. "I was thinking whether I'd done something wrong."

Lu Hao said nothing.

"When you were small, I was hardly ever there." His father bowed his head, gazing at the photograph on the desk. "The army always came first. I kept telling myself, once this stretch is over it'll be better. Then twenty years went by."

"Dad..."

"That painting — I saw it." His father's voice was quiet, as though talking to himself. "I knew who it was. That day at the Youth Centre, standing at the back, looking at your painting. I was thinking: when did this boy notice? I thought you didn't care about such things."

"I was always watching," Lu Hao said.

His father nodded and said nothing for a long time.

At last he spoke. "Second prize is perfectly good."

Lu Hao smiled slightly.

"That's the first time you've ever said that."

"I know." His father lifted his head and looked at him, his eyes faintly red. "Twenty years too late."

—-

That evening, Lu Hao ate supper with his parents.

His mother had cooked several dishes — all his childhood favourites. His father had a little wine and was more talkative than usual, speaking about the army, about his own younger days.

Lu Hao listened, putting in a word here and there.

He realised his father had grown old.

Not just in appearance. The whole man had aged. His speech was slower, his movements slower; even his smile carried a kind of weariness.

As Lu Hao was leaving, his father walked him to the door.

"Your work..." His father paused. "Don't push too hard. Your health matters."

Lu Hao nodded.

"And," his father said, looking at him, hesitating, "whatever you want to do — do it. Don't be like me, waiting until you're old to feel regret."

Lu Hao stared.

It was the first time his father had ever said anything like that to him.

"I will," he said.

He turned and went downstairs. At the bend in the stairwell he glanced back. His father was still standing in the doorway, the light casting his shadow against the wall — long and thin.

Lu Hao suddenly thought of the painting from when he was twelve.

A man seen from behind, standing on a balcony, a cigarette between his fingers.

He finally understood what lay within that silhouette.

Not loneliness. Not weariness.

Regret.

—-

The night he returned to the city, Lu Hao opened his laptop and began to code.

He wrote through the night.

At dawn, he renamed the project folder.

"Tianjun."

He looked at those two characters on the screen for a long time.

Tianjun. The ancients used the word to describe the scales of Heaven. Absolute justice, without bias.

But it wasn't a set of scales he wanted.

Scales can only weigh. What he wanted to know was how weight itself was defined.

Why could two people look at the same painting and disagree on its worth?
Why did the person who kept their head down and did the work always lose to the one who knew how to perform?
Why were those who judged you never themselves subject to judgement?

Who was defining these rules?
Could the rules be changed?
If so, how?
If not, why not?

He didn't know the answers.

But he wanted to.

He was going to build something to find them for him.

Even if the answers turned out to be ones he didn't like.
