# Chapter Eighteen: Intervention

Five to three. Lu Hao pushed open the rooftop door.

Shen Yao stood facing him.

The wind surged from all sides, colder than three years ago. She wore a dark grey trench coat, her hair blown somewhat awry.

"On time," she said.

Lu Hao walked over and stopped three paces away.

"You sent that message?"

"It wasn't me," Shen Yao said. "But I know who it was."

Her voice was different from usual. Calm, but with a kind of distance he couldn't quite place.

Lu Hao looked at her. Three years. From the summit where she'd handed him her card, to this rooftop where she'd given him the report, to tea at her flat discussing predictive feelings — time had flown.

"Is there something you need to tell me?"

Shen Yao didn't answer immediately. Her fingers moved, then stilled.

"I represent an organisation," she said. "We've been watching Tianjun."

The wind gusted into Lu Hao's collar. He didn't move.

"...An organisation?"

"You may have heard rumours." Shen Yao said. "Some people call us 'the Abyss.'"

The Abyss.

Lu Hao thought of the name Liu Ming had once mentioned. Of those speculations about "shadowy forces." Of Shen Yao's own words: "Some people are assessing the risk."

"So from the very beginning —"

"It was an assignment." Shen Yao said. "Get close to you, evaluate Tianjun, monitor your progress."

Her voice was flat, as though describing someone else's business.

Lu Hao felt something pressing on his chest, making it hard to breathe, his throat too dry to speak.

"...Three years," he said.

Shen Yao's shoulder trembled, very slightly.

"Three years," she repeated.

Wind howled between them. In the distance, a plane traced a white line across the sky.

"Those..." Lu Hao began, then stopped. There was too much he wanted to ask. In the end he managed only three words: "Why?"

Shen Yao looked at him. In the grey daylight, her eyes seemed very deep.

"...What you've discovered is dangerous."

"The truth is dangerous?"

"Publishing the truth without regard for consequences is dangerous."

Lu Hao said nothing.

Shen Yao took a step forward. The wind caught her coat hem, lifting it, letting it fall.

"Lu Hao... your AI has told seven point eight billion people that they are designed code." She paused. "What do you think will happen?"

"I don't know."

"I'll tell you." Shen Yao's voice turned urgent. "Not riots. Not revolution. Nihilism."

She paused for a moment.

"When people believe their choices are meaningless, they stop choosing. Stop working. Stop trying. Stop everything."

Lu Hao wanted to argue but couldn't find the words.

"This isn't scaremongering," Shen Yao said. "It's mathematics. You understand better than anyone what mathematics means."

The city spread out below them. Towers, roads, traffic. Seven million people down there, living, working, striving, failing.

"You want me to stop?" Lu Hao said.

"We want you to consider the consequences."

"What consequences?"

Shen Yao didn't answer. She turned and walked to the railing, bracing both hands against it.

"What's Tianjun's current prediction accuracy?" she asked.

"Behavioural prediction, 94.7%."

"And feelings?"

"31.2%."

"What if it keeps developing?" Shen Yao said. "What if one day it can predict everything? Every person's actions, thoughts, who they'll become?"

"So?"

"Then there are no surprises." Shen Yao turned around. "No wonder, no miracles, no one capable of doing anything unexpected. Do you think a world like that would be worth living in?"

Lu Hao thought of his father. Of that silhouette on the balcony. Of "almost."

"What you're afraid of isn't the truth," he said. "You're afraid of losing control."

"What we're afraid of is irreversibility," Shen Yao said. "Some doors, once opened, can't be shut."

Her finger tapped once against the railing.

"Tianjun is still in your hands for now. But if it keeps growing, one day it'll be beyond anyone's control. When that day comes, it won't be you using it. It'll be using you."

"It wouldn't —"

"How do you know?" Shen Yao cut him off. "How do you know that every decision it's making right now isn't preparing for that day?"

Lu Hao didn't speak.

The wind grew stronger. Shen Yao's hair blew wild; she made no effort to fix it.

"We don't want to destroy Tianjun," she said. "We just... want a buffer. Before it changes everything, give humanity a little time to prepare."

"Prepare for what?"

"To accept the truth. Or to refuse it."

Lu Hao lowered his head, looking at the cement beneath his feet. Several cracks ran from the tips of his shoes to the base of the railing.

"...What do you want me to do?"

"Stop," Shen Yao said. "At least pause. Give us time to assess the impact and develop a response plan."

"And if I don't stop?"

Shen Yao was silent for several seconds.

"Then there may be consequences."

"What consequences?"

"I don't know the specifics." Her voice dropped. "But I'd rather no one paid a price."

Lu Hao raised his head and looked at her.

"This is a warning," Shen Yao said. "From someone who..."

She stopped. As if choosing her words.

"From someone who doesn't want to see you get hurt."

The wind died for an instant. Then rose again, more fierce.

Lu Hao looked at her. Three years of images flickered through his mind. The business card she'd offered at the summit. The 1987 report she'd given him on this rooftop. The scent of pu'er tea at her flat. The extra second her hand had lingered on his shoulder when she'd straightened his collar.

"...Was it real?" he asked.

Shen Yao said nothing.

"Those... conversations of ours." Lu Hao's voice was slightly hoarse. "Was it all part of the assignment?"

Shen Yao's fingers tightened on the railing, knuckles white.

"Not all of it."

Her voice was very quiet, nearly scattered by the wind.

"Some things were not part of the assignment."

The air between them solidified. The city's noise receded, as though behind a pane of glass.

Shen Yao looked away.

"But that doesn't change reality," she said, her voice level once more. "Yes, I'm from the Abyss. You're Tianjun's creator. But I am also doing this for your sake."

Lu Hao said nothing.

Shen Yao took a card from her pocket and held it out to him.

"This is a contact. If you decide to talk, you can use it."

Lu Hao took the card. White, no text — only a string of numbers.

"How long do I have to decide?"

"There's no deadline," Shen Yao said. "But time isn't on anyone's side."

She turned and walked towards the stairwell.

After a few steps, she stopped.

Lu Hao thought she was going to say something. But she just stood there, back to him, shoulders slightly stiff.

"That 0.03%," Lu Hao said suddenly.

Shen Yao didn't turn around.

"Tianjun says 99.97% can be predicted. But there's still 0.03%," Lu Hao said. "What is it?"

Shen Yao turned slowly. Her expression was complicated; something flickered in the depths of her eyes, then vanished.

"That's what you'll do after learning all of this."

She hesitated.

"That question — we'd like to know the answer too."

Then she turned and walked into the stairwell. Her footsteps faded, disappearing into the wind.

Lu Hao stood where he was.

The card trembled faintly in his hand, stirred by the wind.

He looked out at the city. The sun had already tilted west, staining the sky amber and red. The glass curtain walls of the towers reflected the last light, like burning flames.

Seven million people. Seven point eight billion people.

All designed code.

But there was still 0.03%.

He didn't know what it meant. Didn't know what to believe. Didn't know what to do next.

He knew only that something had changed.

---

On the way down, Lu Hao didn't take the lift. He took the stairs, floor by floor, forty-two storeys.

His footsteps echoed in the empty stairwell. With every floor, he turned Shen Yao's words over once more.

The Abyss. Assignment. Three years.

Nihilism. Ceasing to choose. Irreversibility.

Some things were not part of the assignment.

By the time he reached the ground floor, it was nearly dark. The lobby lights were on; a security guard dozed at the front desk. Lu Hao pushed through the glass doors and stepped into the evening air.

His phone buzzed in his pocket.

He took it out.

Not Shen Yao.

Zhou Xin.

*The Firefly node's got a problem. Can you come back?*

Lu Hao stared at the screen for a long time.

Then he typed:

*On my way.*

He put the phone back in his pocket and looked up at the sky.

The last of the sunset vanished behind the buildings. Street lamps came on, one after another.

He began walking towards the warehouse.

His phone buzzed again.

He glanced at it.

Not Shen Yao.

Not Zhou Xin either.

An unknown number.

The message was a single line:

*She didn't tell you everything.*

*(End of Chapter Eighteen)*
