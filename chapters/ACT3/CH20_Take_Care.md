# Chapter Twenty: Take Care

The rain started in the afternoon.

Not a downpour — the fine, relentless sort that came with Nanhai's monsoon season. Rain threads traced long thin streaks down the warehouse's high windows, blurring the city outside into a grey-green watercolour.

Lu Hao sat at his workstation, hands on the keyboard, but the screen was dark. He stared at his own reflection — a vague, slightly warped outline. The eyes in the reflection were black voids.

The server array gave off its constant low hum. The air conditioning held the temperature at 21.5 degrees Celsius, humidity 45%. These were Zhou Xin's settings. She'd said those values were kindest to the hardware's lifespan. Lu Hao remembered the way she'd looked saying it — fingers sliding across her tablet, ponytail swaying gently with the movement.

Now those settings were still running. But she was gone.

The door opened.

Lu Hao didn't turn. He heard footsteps — wet soles on the anti-static floor, not heavy, but each step solid. Then the rustle of a jacket being removed, draped carelessly over the back of a chair.

Liu Ming walked over and sat in the chair beside him.

Only then did Lu Hao turn to look.

Liu Ming had lost weight. Not slightly — the kind of collapse that happens in a single week. Cheekbones jutting, eye sockets sunken, a layer of blue-black stubble across his jaw. His hair was wet, a few strands plastered to his forehead. His eyes were hollow, as though something had been drained from them.

But he was neatly dressed. Clean grey T-shirt, dark jeans, laces tied properly. Only his shoes were wet. He hadn't changed them.

"It's raining," Liu Ming said.

Lu Hao nodded.

Liu Ming took two bottles of beer from the canvas bag he'd brought. Green glass, beaded with fine condensation. He placed one on the workstation in front of Lu Hao and held the other.

They each opened theirs. Neither spoke.

Liu Ming raised his bottle and drank. Not fast, but with force, as though trying to flush something from his throat. When he finished, he set the bottle on the desk, his fingers sliding slowly along the cold glass.

Lu Hao drank too. The beer was cool, faintly bitter. He rarely drank. The taste reminded him of the convenience shop below the university dormitory, of staying up with Liu Ming to watch football. They'd drunk this same beer then, drunk till the small hours, then fallen asleep slumped over the desk.

"It's been a week," Liu Ming said.

"Mm."

"The funeral... any news?"

Lu Hao shook his head. "Alexei says the process over there will take another month. Complicated paperwork."

Liu Ming didn't reply. His fingers tightened slowly around the bottle.

"Does her family know?"

"No. And they can't."

Silence.

Rain filled the silence. Along with the low hum of the server fans, the hiss of the air conditioning, the occasional sound of a car passing through standing water in the distance. These sounds wove together into a constant background drone, like the breathing of time.

Liu Ming drank again. Slowly this time, eyes fixed on the window. Rainwater gathered into streams on the glass, meandering downward.

"I'm leaving," he said.

Lu Hao looked at the bottle in his hand. Condensation was beading on its surface, growing larger, then sliding along the curve, leaving a circular wet ring on the desk.

"I know," he said.

"You're not going to ask why?"

"Zhou Xin."

Liu Ming shook his head. The motion was slight but definite.

"It's not just her," he said. "It's everything."

Lu Hao looked up at him.

Liu Ming wasn't looking back. His gaze had fallen on the far end of the warehouse — Zhou Xin's workstation. The desk was neatly tidied, keyboard centred, monitor dark. Her navy blue cardigan still hung over the chair.

"We grew up together." Liu Ming's voice was level. "But this road — I can't walk it any further."

Lu Hao said nothing. He squeezed a bead of condensation between his fingers; the cold liquid seeped into the gaps.

"Will you betray us?"

Liu Ming looked at him.

"No," he said. "But I won't help you any more either."

Lu Hao nodded.

A long silence.

Liu Ming's fingers tapped the desk absently, without rhythm, just an unconscious motion. His gaze drifted back to the window, to the city in the rain. Under the grey sky, the towers were like faded building blocks. Occasionally a pair of headlights cut across a street, like a shooting star.

"Hao-zi," he said suddenly, "do you remember when we were kids?"

Lu Hao looked at him.

"I fought your fights, you did my homework." Liu Ming's voice was very quiet, as though speaking to himself. "It was so simple back then."

---

The setting sun had turned the playing field burnt orange. Dust floated in the shafts of light like golden powder.

The bigger boys had Lu Hao surrounded, shoving his shoulders. Lu Hao kept his head down. His schoolbag lay on the ground, textbooks scattered. He wasn't crying, but his lips were pressed tight, fingers clutching the hem of his shirt.

Then Liu Ming charged in.

He stood in front of Lu Hao, sleeves rolled to the elbows.

"Try touching him!" he bellowed. The bigger boys froze. Liu Ming bent down and picked up a brick, gripping it, eyes wide.

"Come on then!" he bellowed again.

Sunlight on his face, sweat and dust mixed together. The boys glanced at one another and walked away.

Liu Ming set down the brick and turned to Lu Hao.

"All right?"

Lu Hao shook his head. He crouched to pick up his textbooks, hands shaking slightly. Liu Ming crouched beside him, gathering the scattered pages one by one, brushing off the dirt.

"Next time they come for you, just shout for me," Liu Ming said.

Lu Hao looked at him. The setting sun gilded his eyelashes.

"Thanks," Lu Hao said.

Liu Ming grinned.

"What's to thank? We're mates."

They stuffed the books back into the bag and walked home side by side. Two small shadows, stretched long by the setting sun, trailed thin across the dusty playing field.

---

Liu Ming drank, closed his eyes.

"It was so simple back then."

Lu Hao looked at the bottle in his hand.

"We can't go back."

"I know." Liu Ming opened his eyes. "I just sometimes wonder what it would've been like if I'd never come with you."

He didn't finish.

Silence.

"When you came to me," Liu Ming said, "I thought — Hao-zi's always been a good bloke. Whatever he's doing, it must be fine."

Lu Hao said nothing.

"But you weren't forced into this, were you?"

"No."

Liu Ming looked at him for a long time.

"That's what makes it hardest."

---

The desk lamp cast a warm yellow circle on the desk. Two sets of homework lay open — one neat, one scrawled.

Lu Hao was correcting Liu Ming's maths. Liu Ming lay propped beside him, eyes half-shut, nearly asleep.

From the kitchen came the sound of frying, then a smell. Braised pork belly. Rich sauce, star anise, cassia bark.

Liu Ming's mother pushed open the door. Apron on, a large bowl in her hands. Braised pork, glistening, the chunks wobbling.

"Hao-zi, Ming-ming, dinner."

She set the bowl on the corner of the desk and went back to the kitchen for bowls and chopsticks. Returning, she served Lu Hao a bowl of rice first, then Liu Ming.

"Hao-zi's too thin. Eat more." She picked up the biggest piece of pork with her chopsticks and placed it in Lu Hao's bowl.

Fat and lean in perfect layers, sauce seeping into the rice, soaking in.

Lu Hao looked up at her. Liu Ming's mother's smile deepened the creases at the corners of her eyes, but her eyes were bright.

"Auntie, I..."

"Eat, eat, don't be shy." She dropped another piece into Liu Ming's bowl. "You too, Ming-ming. Stop messing about."

Liu Ming pulled a face. "Mum, I'm doing homework."

"Homework or not, you still need to eat." She patted Liu Ming's head, then turned to Lu Hao. "Hao-zi, your mum's working late tonight, so you'll eat here. Finish your homework after dinner and then Ming-ming can walk you home."

"Thanks, Auntie."

"Thanks for what? We're all family."

She turned and left, pulling the door gently shut. The fragrance of braised pork filled the room; the lamplight was warm and still. Lu Hao bowed his head and ate. The pork was savoury, the rice soft. Liu Ming beside him was wolfing it down, cheeks bulging.

Outside, the sky was darkening. Lights came on in the block opposite, one by one. Two boys ate dinner under a desk lamp, did homework, spoke in low murmurs and laughed.

Back then, the world was very small. Small enough to be a single desk, a bowl of braised pork, a desk lamp.

---

Liu Ming raised the bottle to his lips but didn't drink. He looked at the liquid inside, watched the bubbles rise from the bottom.

"Tianjun says we're code," he said, voice very quiet. "So is my choice part of the code too?"

Lu Hao was silent.

"If my leaving is designed..." Liu Ming turned his head and looked at Lu Hao. "Then why should I still leave?"

Lu Hao was silent.

Liu Ming held his gaze for a long time, then turned back to the window. The rain was heavier, rattling against the glass.

"Because I want to leave." Every word bitten off clean. "Whether it's written or not... I want to leave."

He paused. Drew a deep breath. His chest rose and fell; his shoulders trembled slightly.

"This is my choice."

---

The concrete of the military compound was scorching under the July sun. You could feel the heat through your soles. Cicadas screamed in the trees, the sound rising and falling in waves.

Two boys filled a basin from the tap room and threw water at each other. The spray glittered in the sunlight like shattered diamonds. Lu Hao's T-shirt was soaked through, clinging to his body. Liu Ming was worse — drenched head to toe, hair dripping, shoes squelching.

"Take that!" Liu Ming flung another basin.

Lu Hao dodged, but his trouser legs still caught it. He laughed, bent to fill his own basin. They chased each other, water flying everywhere. Laughter echoed around the empty compound, bouncing off walls and back.

At last they were both spent, slumped on the steps in the shade. Sunlight leaked through gaps in the leaves, casting trembling speckles on the ground. Wet clothes clung to skin; the breeze blew through, a touch cool.

"Brilliant," Liu Ming said, tipping his head back and gulping tap water. It leaked from the corners of his mouth, ran down his neck.

Lu Hao drank too. The tap water tasted of chlorine, but it quenched.

They sat for a long time, until their clothes were half dry. From far off came a mother's call to dinner — one shout, two — drifting through the afternoon heat haze.

"Again tomorrow?" Liu Ming asked.

"Yes."

"Right."

They got up, brushed the dust from their backsides, and walked home. Two wet silhouettes leaving a trail of dark footprints on the scorching concrete.

---

Liu Ming stood. He picked up his bottle, tipped his head back and drained it. His Adam's apple bobbed. He set it down. Empty.

Lu Hao's bottle was still more than half full. He picked it up and took a sip. The beer had lost its chill; the bitterness was sharper now.

"I'm off," Liu Ming said.

Lu Hao nodded.

Liu Ming walked to the door. His steps were steady, unhesitating. But at the threshold he stopped. His hand rested on the handle, but he didn't turn it.

He stood there a long time. Back to Lu Hao, shoulders slightly bowed. Outside, the rain intensified, hammering the glass like countless tiny stones.

Lu Hao watched his back. He had been watching this back for over thirty years. From the back of a head in the nursery queue, to the shoulders that had shielded him on the primary school playing field, to a back that broadened through secondary school, to the slightly stiff outline of a suit in adulthood.

Now this back was leaving.

Liu Ming's shoulder moved. As though he meant to turn around, but in the end didn't. He drew a deep breath, then exhaled.

"Take care," he said.

The words were very soft, nearly drowned by the rain.

He turned the handle and walked out. The door shut; the latch clicked — crisp, like something snapping.

Lu Hao sat in the chair. Motionless.

He stared at the door. A white panel, a few pale scratches where a server had scraped it last year during a move. The handle was metal, giving off a cold gleam in the lamplight.

He wanted to call out to Liu Ming, but his throat felt blocked, and no sound would come.

He just sat there.

The rain went on falling. The servers went on running. The air conditioning went on holding 21.5 degrees, 45% humidity. Same as a minute ago, same as a day ago, same as before Zhou Xin left.

Liu Ming was gone.

Lu Hao looked down at the bottle in his hand. The golden liquid rocked gently, reflecting the ceiling's fluorescent tube in miniature. He raised the bottle and drank again. This time a large mouthful — he nearly choked.

When he set it down, his hand was shaking. Very slight, but unstoppable, as though rising from deep inside the bone.

He closed his eyes.

Images flashed. Liu Ming standing in front of him, sleeves rolled to the elbows. Liu Ming's mother placing braised pork in his bowl. Liu Ming on the playing field, flinging water, hair dripping. Liu Ming clapping his shoulder at the airport — hard.

Then the images shifted to Zhou Xin. Zhou Xin sitting before the rack, hands still on the keyboard. Zhou Xin asking: did you really read that thesis of mine? Zhou Xin saying: tell them for me — what I designed was put to good use.

And finally, Tianjun's interface. Black background, white text. 62.7%.

All the numbers, all the code, all the design and the designed.

But Liu Ming had said: this is my choice.

Lu Hao opened his eyes.

He stood, walked to the window. The rain was heavier now, the world outside completely blurred — nothing but shifting grey. Street lamps flickered on, their dim glow bleeding through the rain, like melting sugar.

Liu Ming had said: this is my choice.

If everything is code, is "choice" still choice?

He didn't know.

He turned and walked back to the workstation. Liu Ming's empty bottle still sat on the desk. His own — a little left, bubbles gone, the liquid perfectly still.

Lu Hao sat down, fingers on the keyboard.

The screen lit up. Tianjun's interface appeared automatically — black background, cursor blinking, awaiting input.

He stared at the cursor. One second, two seconds, three seconds.

Then he typed nothing at all and simply closed the interface.

The screen dimmed, once again showing his reflection. Blurred, slightly warped, black empty eyes.

Outside the window, the rain went on. Endless rain.

In the warehouse, only the low hum of server fans, the hiss of the air conditioning, and the sound of his own breathing.

And two beer bottles.

One empty.

One not.

---

Night deepened.

Lu Hao still sat at the workstation. He hadn't turned on the light; only the screen's faint glow lit his face. The rain softened to a patter, becoming background.

He thought of his father. The silhouette on the balcony.

Almost.

Always almost.

The servers went on running.

Lu Hao picked up the last of the beer and drained it. The beer was completely warm now, bitter to the point of astringence. He set the empty bottle beside Liu Ming's.

Two green glass bottles, side by side on the desk.

He looked at them for a long time.

Then he rose, walked to Zhou Xin's workstation, placed his hand on the back of her chair, and stood there, head bowed, for a long while.

He walked back to his own desk.

Tianjun's interface was lit. The cursor blinked.

He typed:

*Liu Ming is gone.*

*I know. Based on his parameters, the probability of departure was 99.97%.*

Lu Hao stared at the line.

*And the 0.03%?*

*That is freedom.*

He closed his eyes.

The rain had stopped.

Outside the window, the world was utterly still. In the distance, the city blazed with light — like a luminous nebula, adrift on the ocean of night.

---

*(End of Chapter Twenty)*
