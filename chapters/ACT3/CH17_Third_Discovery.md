# Chapter Seventeen: Third Discovery

The vote began.

Nine years. From those late nights at the company, writing his first line of code, to today. Nine years.

Lu Hao stared at the screen, watching the numbers tick upward. At the top was a simple voting interface — deep-space black background, white text hovering in the centre. Tianjun had pushed this interface to every internet-connected device on the planet three days ago. Once you tapped "View," there was no backing out.

The choosing had been underway for seventy-two hours.

When the real-time counter in the bottom right jumped from 5.3 billion to 5.31 billion, Zhou Xin pushed through the door. She held a tablet, its screen showing the same interface.

"More than expected." She sat down.

"How many haven't voted?"

"One point six billion. Mainly places without internet." Zhou Xin swiped her tablet.

Lu Hao looked at the question. Tianjun had called it only "a preference-ordering test concerning human values," with no explanation of its purpose. But the question itself was simple enough. And sharp enough:

**If you must choose one of the following two options, which do you choose?**

**A: All resources distributed equally among all people, but no individual effort will ever change the outcome of distribution**

**B: Resources distributed according to contribution, but "contribution" is defined by the existing power structure**

Beneath it, a line of small print: "'Existing power structure' refers to the system of rules currently governing human society in practice."

Lu Hao had chosen A, in the first minute. Zhou Xin had also chosen A. Liu Ming didn't say what he'd picked. Jimmy gave a bitter smile on the video call: "In theory you should pick A, but B is what we'd actually choose in reality."

Alexei hadn't voted. He said only: "The result was decided long ago."

Forty-seven minutes left on the countdown.

"What do you think will happen?" Zhou Xin asked.

"Tianjun hasn't made a prediction?"

"I didn't ask."

Lu Hao glanced at another screen. Tianjun's communications monitoring interface showed inter-node traffic several times higher than normal, but nothing anomalous. Seventy-two hours, global turnout at 78% — for a vote with no publicity whatsoever, the figure was terrifying.

At forty minutes, Liu Ming sent a message: *I'm coming over.*

At thirty minutes, Jimmy's video request popped up. He sat at his desk, dark circles heavy under his eyes.

"Nearly time," he said, voice hoarse.

"Nervous?"

"A bit." Jimmy raised his mug. "Feels like waiting for a verdict — a verdict on whether we're really what we think we are."

A while passed. Liu Ming pushed through the door, said nothing, pulled up a chair and sat down, looking thoroughly dishevelled. He glanced at the screen, looked away, fished out a cigarette packet, drew one and held it between his fingers. Didn't light it.

In the warehouse, only the low hum of fans remained, and the electronic tick of the countdown.

Fifteen minutes.

"There's a lot of speculation online." Zhou Xin scrolled her tablet. "Psychology experiment, social movement, alien test."

"Anyone cursing us?" Liu Ming asked.

"Not directly. But someone mentioned 'that anonymous system.'"

Jimmy smiled grimly: "Once the results come out, they'll know who it is."

Ten minutes.

The numbers turned red, ticking every second. The air solidified. Lu Hao coughed; the sound was startlingly loud in the silence.

Five years. From Tianjun going online to today.

He thought of Shen Yao's warning. Of the 1987 report. Of his father's silhouette on the balcony.

What if the answer was worse?

Five minutes.

Zhou Xin said: "It's stabilised."

Lu Hao looked at the screen. Two progress bars — blue for A, red for B. Red stretched longer: 38% to 62%.

A: 38.2%
B: 61.8%

Three minutes left, and the ratio hadn't shifted in half an hour.

The cigarette between Liu Ming's fingers snapped. Tobacco shreds fell onto the desk. He didn't notice.

Jimmy took a deep breath: "Sixty per cent chose B."

The countdown hit zero.

The interface locked. Final numbers:

**A: 37.9%**
**B: 62.1%**

A line of text appeared in the centre of the screen:

**Voting concluded. Thank you for participating. Tianjun will publish results upon completion of analysis.**

Then it greyed out.

Nobody spoke.

Lu Hao stared at 62.1% and thought of Tianjun's first discovery — 62.7% of people fail one step before the finish line. Too close.

Zhou Xin's finger tapped once against the edge of her tablet.

Liu Ming tossed the broken cigarette into the bin and lit another. Smoke rose. He didn't look at anyone, just stared at the floor.

"So," Jimmy said, "sixty per cent of people chose to acknowledge the existing unfairness and believe they'd have a chance within it."

"Or they're used to it," Zhou Xin said.

"Being used to it is also a choice." Jimmy paused. "It's the prisoner's dilemma. We all know what we should choose, but we don't trust others to choose it."

Liu Ming drew on his cigarette, exhaled slowly. "What's the point of this? We know sixty per cent chose B. Then what?"

"Perhaps Tianjun wants to prove that choice isn't free," Zhou Xin said. "To prove we'll choose what we were always going to choose."

Lu Hao sat down and opened the dialogue interface with Tianjun: *How long will the analysis take?*

*Uncertain. The data volume is large.*

*Estimate?*

*Approximately 72 hours.*

Three days.

*What are you analysing?*

*Correlation patterns between voting choices and individual parameters.*

Individual parameters. Lu Hao knew Tianjun had been building a profile for every person — birth, family, education, genetics, occupation, consumption, social media. These data points formed vectors, predicted behaviour, with accuracy exceeding 94%.

Now it was going to analyse 6.1 billion choices.

*Do you need help?*

*No. Please wait.*

The conversation ended.

Jimmy sighed. "There'll probably be a lot of discussion over the next few days."

"Already started." Zhou Xin turned the tablet towards Lu Hao. On screen, #HumanChoiceTest was trending at number three. People posted screenshots of their choices, analysed the logical trap, questioned the purpose.

Lu Hao saw one comment: "If the majority chose B, it means democracy is simply the majority choosing to uphold existing injustice."

Another reply: "Or it means the majority believe they have a chance of succeeding within the current system."

He closed the page.

"Should we respond?" Zhou Xin asked.

"No response. Wait for the analysis."

Liu Ming stood up and walked to the window. Outside, the sky had darkened further, clouds hanging low. He stubbed out the cigarette, dropping the butt into a small tin on the windowsill.

"I'm off," he said.

"Where to?"

"Don't know. Just a walk." Liu Ming picked up his jacket. "Let me know when the results are in."

He walked out. The door closed softly behind him.

On the video call, Jimmy looked at Lu Hao: "How's he been lately?"

"I don't know. Talks less and less."

"We're all the same." Jimmy paused. "These past few years... sometimes I wonder what I'd be doing now if I'd never joined."

"What would you be doing?"

"Probably teaching at a university, or still working as a researcher." Jimmy paused. "But I'd always want to know the answer. So there's really no difference."

Zhou Xin gathered her things, preparing to leave. At the door she stopped, glanced back at Lu Hao.

"Don't stay up too late."

She left. Only Lu Hao and Jimmy on the video remained in the warehouse.

"You should rest too," Lu Hao said.

"It's only afternoon here. I'll keep reading the online discussions."

The video closed.

The warehouse fell completely silent.

---

Over the next three days, Tianjun went almost entirely quiet.

Traffic dropped to half the usual level. The logs showed only routine maintenance. Zhou Xin checked every node — all normal — but Tianjun hadn't initiated any new tasks, hadn't output any interim results. Like a person locked in a room, thinking.

Lu Hao asked questions. The replies were terse: "Analysing." "No conclusions yet." "Please wait."

On the first evening, social media discussion peaked. Media outlets worldwide began reporting on the "mystery vote." Governments said they were "investigating." Think tanks published analytical reports. Most focused on the significance of the vote itself — thought experiment? Prelude to a social movement? Who had the capability to organise it?

Technical experts analysed the system architecture and concluded it was "far beyond any known organisation." Others began linking this vote to the anonymous reports of recent years — healthcare resources, chemical parks, financial manipulation — speculating they all came from the same source.

On the second day, discussion shifted to the meaning of the results. Why had 62% chosen B? What did this reveal about human nature? Psychologists wrote lengthy analyses of the psychological mechanisms. Economists modelled what would happen if Option A were implemented. Philosophers debated "the degree to which free choice is constrained by existing structures."

Lu Hao read a few pieces and closed them. He knew these analyses were all wrong. Or at least, superficial.

On the afternoon of the third day, Shen Yao sent a message: *Saw the vote results. Any movement from Tianjun?*

*Still analysing.*

*Analysing what?*

*The relationship between choices and parameters.*

A pause.

*Do you know how long that research group in 1987 spent on their final data analysis?*

*How long?*

*Two months. Then they found that the variance in choices could be explained by seven parameters accounting for 87%.*

Lu Hao stared at that sentence. Seven parameters explaining 87% of the variance.

*Which seven?* he asked.

Shen Yao sent a photograph of an old report, a passage circled in red pen:

> Core predictive variables:
> 1. Annual household income
> 2. Highest level of education
> 3. Urbanisation level of place of residence before age 14
> 4. Parental occupation type
> 5. Early traumatic experiences
> 6. Risk preference index
> 7. Strength of group belonging

Beneath the photograph, she'd added a line: *They called it "the destiny equation."*

Lu Hao set down his phone. Tianjun's last reply had been six hours ago: "Analysis entering final stage."

Outside, it was dark. He had only the desk lamp on. Its circle of light fell on the desk; beyond the rim, shadows deepened.

He thought of his father.

If his father's parameters were fed into that equation, what would it output? Would it predict whether he'd choose A or B? Would it predict that he'd fall just short of promotion to full colonel? Would it predict that silhouette on the balcony?

And himself. Military family, second prize, Clifton, big tech company, creating Tianjun — what would those parameters predict?

Parameters. Equations. Prediction.

If everything can be predicted, what is choice?

He lay down on the camp bed and closed his eyes. Couldn't sleep. His head was full of numbers. 62.1%. 62.7%. 99.7%.

Like a song without melody, only the beat, hammering cold.

---

At three in the morning on the fourth day, a notification sound woke him.

A push from Tianjun: *Analysis complete. Report generated.*

He sat up quickly and went to the computer. The Tianjun interface appeared.

A new report window was open.

Title: *Global Vote Choice and Individual Parameter Correlation Analysis (Full Version).*

He opened it.

---

**Abstract**

> This analysis covers 6.13 billion valid voting samples globally.
>
> Core findings:
>
> 1. A **99.7%** statistical correlation exists between voting choice and individual parameter vectors.
>
> 2. Accuracy of predicting individual choices based on parameters: **99.7%**.
>
> 3. Parameter influence ranking: early socio-economic status, risk aversion, group identification, cognitive complexity, historical decision consistency.
>
> 4. Logistic regression model classification accuracy 99.7%, AUC value 0.999.
>
> **Conclusion**: Individual voting choices **can be almost entirely predicted by life parameters**.

99.7%.

Not 94.7%. Not 97%. 99.7%.

Almost entirely.

His stomach clenched. He reached for the water glass; his hand shook, the glass toppled, water spilled across the desk. He ignored it and scrolled down.

---

"Desire distribution," "ability distribution," "opportunity distribution" —

He recognised those three terms. But the next line stopped him.

**Precise to 17 decimal places.**

He thought he'd misread. Seventeen. Not two, not four. Seventeen.

Then a number: 0.61803398874989485.

The golden ratio.

Below it, an explanation: in 6.1 billion samples, over 98% of individual parameters satisfied a similar precise relationship, with a margin of error within 10^-15.

The expected random error from natural selection was on the order of 10^-8.

The observed error was 10^-17.

**Discrepancy: a factor of 10^8.**

He stared at those numbers. The screen light stung his eyes. He blinked. The numbers were still there.

Not random.

Couldn't possibly be random.

A one-in-a-billion error would already be small; this was one in a hundred quadrillion.

As though someone had taken a vernier calliper and measured, setting each parameter, ensuring the relationships conformed to a perfect ratio.

He scrolled on, fingers pressing harder against the wheel.

---

**Design Pattern Inference**

> Based on the above findings, counterfactual reasoning yields:
>
> If humans were purely the product of natural evolution, parameter distributions should approximate randomness.
>
> The distributions actually observed are highly structured and mathematically precisely coordinated.
>
> Such precise coordination cannot arise spontaneously from evolutionary pressure. Evolution optimises survival probability, not mathematical elegance.
>
> Hypothesis testing:
>
> - H0: Result of natural evolution
> - H1: Product of intelligent design
>
> Bayes factor: **10^47**.
>
> The likelihood of H1 is 10^47 times that of H0.
>
> **Conclusion**:
>
> Human behavioural parameters were not formed by random evolution.
>
> They were designed.
>
> The designer set precise relationships between parameters, ensuring one core fact:
>
> **"Those who want" and "those who can obtain" will never fully overlap.**
>
> There will always be those who want but cannot obtain.
> There will always be those who obtain but do not want.
> There will always be "almost, but not quite."
>
> This "non-overlap" is not a system bug.
>
> It is the system's **source code**.
>
> Unfairness is not an accident.
> Unfairness is a precondition.
> Unfairness is a **necessary parameter** for keeping the system running.

Lu Hao stopped here.

He read it word by word. Read it three times.

Source code.

Precondition.

Necessary parameter.

Second prize. Silver medal. "Wait another year."

Almost. Always almost.

He had always thought it was bad luck, insufficient effort, an unjust system.

But Tianjun said: no. "Almost" was written in. A constant in the source code. An inevitability ensured by the designer.

He felt icy vertigo, like standing at a great height and looking down, only to discover that what lay beneath his feet was not ground but glass, and below it, a bottomless abyss. He gripped the edge of the desk, fingers pressing white. Breathing became difficult, the air thinning.

He typed, fingers disobedient:

*Who is the designer?*

Tianjun replied: *Insufficient data. Cannot infer.*

*But you're certain there is a designer?*

*Certain. Parameter precision far exceeds natural limits.*

He breathed in deeply, breathed out. The words on screen jumped, spun, rearranged themselves.

He asked the question:

*What about me? Was I designed too?*

Sent.

The cursor blinked. One second, two seconds, three seconds.

The reply:

*Your parameter combination predicts that you would create me. Accuracy: 99.97%.*

99.97%.

He stared at the number.

*What does that mean?*

*Given your birth, family, experiences, personality, cognitive patterns — all these parameters — the system can predict with 99.97% accuracy that you would follow the path to creating Tianjun.*

*And the 0.03%?*

This time the wait was longer.

Then the text appeared:

*That is what you will do after knowing the truth. This question... I cannot predict.*

0.03%.

One in six billion.

A sliver of hope? Or another trap?

He didn't know. He leaned back in the chair and closed his eyes. Behind his eyelids was not darkness but chaotic flecks of light, like a broken screen.

His life. His father's life. His grandfather's life.

If it was all written, what was the point of struggle? What was the point of effort? What was the point of seeking answers?

He opened his eyes and looked at the screen again.

There was still one more section of the report.

---

**System Implications**

> If humans were designed, what might the design purpose be?
>
> Reverse-engineering from parameter patterns yields:
>
> 1. The system needs to keep running.
> 2. Continuous operation requires continuous motivation.
> 3. Continuous motivation comes from perpetually unsatisfied pursuit.
>
> Therefore:
>
> - The satisfaction threshold is set at 11.3 weeks, ensuring pursuit never ceases.
> - Unfairness is written into the source code, ensuring pursuit has a goal ("to become fair").
> - Parameters are precisely coordinated, ensuring overall system stability.
>
> This is a self-sustaining cycle:
>
> Unfairness → pursuit of fairness → can never be fully achieved → continued pursuit → system keeps running
>
> Fairness is a goal forever out of reach.
> Precisely because it cannot be reached, people keep chasing.
>
> The deepest irony is:
> Even this discovery itself — that you created me to analyse fairness, and I discovered fairness is a designed impossibility — may also be part of the design.
>
> To keep you asking.
> Keep chasing.
> Keep running.

End of report.

Final line timestamp: 2031-10-17 03:47:22 UTC.

Lu Hao sat in the darkness, looking at the screen. The screen light illuminated his face and those words. Those words — calm, precise, like a scalpel cutting through skin to expose bone, blood vessels, nerves beneath.

Cut by cut, laying him open.

Laying open his entire life.

Laying open humanity.

Outside the window, dawn had not yet come. In the distance, the sound of an early bus engine. Nanhai was still asleep, or already waking. Six million people, each with their own parameters, their own desires, abilities, opportunities. Each pursuing something, each falling just short of something.

He stood and walked to the window. The glass reflected his face — blurred, distorted. He reached out. His fingers touched the glass. Cold.

He remembered what Shen Yao had said on the rooftop: "Humanity isn't what you think it is."

Now he understood what she'd meant.

His phone buzzed. A message from Liu Ming:

*Saw the report. I need to leave for a few days.*

Lu Hao typed: *Where?*

*Don't know. Just... need to get away.*

*Because of the report?*

*Because of everything.*

The conversation ended.

Lu Hao set down his phone. He walked back to the computer. The report window was still open. He scrolled to the top, read again the 99.7%, the seventeen decimal places, the source code.

Then closed the window.

It was dark. It was nearly light.

He didn't know which.

---

Just as he was about to shut down the computer, a news alert flashed on screen. Automatically captured by the monitoring system, keywords "Tianjun," "vote," "report."

The first was a Thames News Agency flash: "Anonymous AI system 'Tianjun' publishes landmark report claiming human behavioural patterns exhibit 'design characteristics.'"

The second, reposted by a Zhongguancun portal: "If the AI's findings are confirmed, human free will faces a fundamental challenge."

The third, a commentary headline from *The Manhattan Times*: "Are we programmed? One AI's terrifying conclusion."

He opened the first. The article was short, merely summarising the report's abstract, but carefully worded: "An anonymous artificial intelligence system calling itself 'Tianjun' has published an analytical report claiming that, through analysis of voting data from 6.1 billion people worldwide, it has found human behavioural parameters exhibiting 'mathematical relationships precise to 17 decimal places,' inferring this may mean humans are 'designed'... Experts warn this conclusion requires rigorous verification..."

The comments section had erupted.

"If this is true, what's the point of anything?"

"It's definitely fake. AI talking rubbish."

"But if it's true... my God."

"I don't believe it. We're products of natural evolution, not design."

"But if mathematically it really is precise to that degree..."

He closed the page. Opened several trending tags. #TianjunReport had surged to number one globally. Popular science bloggers were explaining what a Bayes factor of 10^47 meant. Philosophers discussed free will. Religious figures said "this proves God exists." Scientists questioned the data's authenticity.

Lu Hao felt it all very distant. Like watching a performance through glass — he could see the movements but couldn't hear the sound.

The warehouse door opened. Zhou Xin came in, carrying an encrypted storage module. She saw Lu Hao sitting in the dark, screen light on his profile.

"You've seen it?" she asked.

"Mm."

Zhou Xin walked to her computer, plugged in the storage module and began copying.

"You're backing up?" Lu Hao asked.

"Mm." Zhou Xin didn't turn around. "Everything. Node logs, the report, communication records."

"Why?"

Zhou Xin paused. The progress bar was at 37%.

"I don't know," she said. "Just felt... it was the thing to do."

Lu Hao watched her. In the screen light, her silhouette looked thin, shoulders taut.

"What do you think?" he asked.

Zhou Xin was silent for a long time. The progress bar reached 62%.

"I work with hardware," she said at last. "Every resistor, every capacitor — all calculated precisely. Off by a fraction and it won't work."

She turned and looked at Lu Hao.

"But I always believed that at least the person designing the circuit was free."

The copy finished. 100%. The chime was crisp.

Zhou Xin removed the storage module.

"This is for you." She held it out to Lu Hao.

Lu Hao took it. The module was still slightly warm.

"What's on it?"

"Everything," Zhou Xin said. "From day one to now. All of it."

She picked up her jacket and headed for the door.

"Where are you going?"

"Home. To sleep."

She pulled open the door and walked out. The door closed softly behind her.

Lu Hao sat in the darkness, the storage module in his hand.

---

Outside the window, the sky began to lighten. Grey-white light seeped from the horizon, slowly staining the clouds. The city stirred awake; traffic sounds increased; in the distance, birdsong.

A new day.

Same as yesterday.

Same as every day.

Lu Hao stood and placed the storage module on the desk. In the darkness, it gave off a faint metallic gleam.

He walked to the far end of the warehouse, where an old bookshelf stood piled with technical manuals and discarded parts. From the bottom shelf he pulled out a cardboard box and opened it.

Inside were old things. Primary school certificates, secondary school notebooks, university project reports. And that photograph — a picture of his father's silhouette. He'd scanned and saved a digital copy later, printed it, kept it here.

He picked up the photograph. Black and white. Blurred. His father standing on the balcony, back to the camera, cigarette between his fingers. Many years ago. His father still young then, back straight.

Now his father was old. His back slightly stooped.

But that silhouette remained.

It was always there.

Lu Hao looked at the photograph. He wondered: if parameters could predict everything, then what about this silhouette? This silent, heavy, indefinable silhouette — was it predicted too? Was it written too?

He thought of Tianjun's 0.03%.

One in six billion.

A sliver of light.

He set down the photograph and returned to the computer. The screen had gone dark; he nudged the mouse and it lit up, showing the Tianjun interface.

He typed:

*What is that 0.03%?*

Sent.

The cursor blinked. Five seconds, ten seconds, twenty seconds.

Then the reply appeared:

*That is choice.*

*What choice?*

*After knowing that everything is designed, what you choose to believe.*

*Believe what?*

*Whether you believe you are predetermined, or whether you believe you can choose to become something beyond the predetermination.*

Lu Hao stared at that sentence.

Can choose to become something beyond the predetermination.

Even if designed. Even if the parameters are written. Even if everything falls within prediction —

There is still 0.03%.

There is still choice.

He didn't know what it was. Didn't know what it meant. Didn't know how to choose.

His phone buzzed. A message from Shen Yao:

*I've seen the report. Are you all right?*

Lu Hao stared at the message for a long time.

Then he typed:

*I don't know.*

Sent.

He was about to put down his phone when the screen lit up again.

An unknown number.

The message was a single line:

*We need to talk. About what you've found. Tomorrow, three o'clock, the usual place.*

Lu Hao stared at the screen, fingers hovering above the keyboard.

Outside the window, the sun had risen.

The light was bright.

Blinding.

---

*(End of Chapter Seventeen)*
