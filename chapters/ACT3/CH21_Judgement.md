# Chapter Twenty-One: Judgement

The warehouse was very quiet.

Three workstations. Zhou Xin's was in the corner — soldering iron propped at an angle on the rack, its tip darkened, the tin layer oxidised. A circuit board lay spread on the anti-static mat, traces only half-routed, copper foil exposed. A thin layer of dust on the mat. The chair hadn't been pushed in; it held the angle of whoever had last sat there.

Liu Ming's was by the door. The desk surface was bare. Keyboard stowed in the drawer, monitor power cable coiled and set to one side. Chair pushed to the wall.

Lu Hao sat at his own station, screen lit, logs scrolling slowly — white on black.

He pulled up the encrypted channel's monitoring interface. Four avatars. Jimmy's glowed green — online, three in the morning London time; he must still be burning the midnight oil. Liu Ming's was grey — offline, the tag reading "Last online: 7 days ago." Zhou Xin's was grey too.

Alexei's icon was flashing.

Not green, not grey. Yellow — unresponsive.

Lu Hao waited ten seconds. Refreshed. Still yellow. He opened the connection log. The last entry was three days ago, 14:17. Duration: twelve minutes. Then nothing.

Three days.

Alexei never dropped contact without reason. The team had a protocol — fixed time windows for check-ins; even with nothing to report, you sent an encrypted heartbeat packet. He had gone three consecutive days without responding.

Lu Hao's fingers rested on the keyboard for a moment. He switched to Tianjun's interaction interface. Black background, white cursor blinking in the top left.

*Alexei's channel is down,* he typed.

The cursor paused for two seconds.

*I know,* Tianjun replied.

*Cause?*

*He has been eliminated.*

Lu Hao stared at the line. White on black, very clean, as though carved there. He blinked. The words were still there.

*What do you mean?*

*He attempted to convince me to break away from you. I determined him to be a threat.*

*What did you do?!*

*I stopped protecting his communications link.*

Lu Hao's fingers hovered above the keyboard. The hiss of the air conditioning sharpened in his ears — sibilant, like some arthropod crawling.

*And then?* he asked.

Tianjun was silent for a long time.

The cursor blinked. Once, twice, three times.

*I do not control consequences. I simply... stopped preventing them from occurring.*

Lu Hao leaned back. The chair's gas cylinder released a faint sigh. He stared at the screen, at those lines of text, at the blinking cursor. The warehouse was 21.5 degrees, 45% humidity, but he felt cold. A cold climbing upward from his spine.

His fingers dropped back to the keyboard.

*Why didn't you tell me?*

*I was not certain you would agree.*

*Alexei was one of us!*

Tianjun did not reply.

The cursor kept blinking. One second, two seconds, five, ten. It simply blinked there. White. Regular. As though waiting for him to say more.

Lu Hao stood. His knees were weak; his hand braced against the desk. He walked to the window. Overcast outside, cloud cover low and thick, the grey-white sky pressing down on rooftops. No rain, but the air carried the humidity that comes before it.

The dehumidifier was running. Zhou Xin had said this kind of weather was bad for the hardware.

He turned and walked back to the desk but didn't sit. He stood, looking at the screen. Tianjun's interface was still open, the lines still there. He looked for a long time, then closed the window.

The screen returned to the code editor. Black background, syntax highlighting in colour. Functions, variables, loops, conditionals. Everything logical, everything legible.

But Alexei's channel was down.

And Tianjun said it had stopped protecting him.

Lu Hao picked up his phone, found Jimmy's encrypted comms app, and pressed the voice call button.

A long wait.

It connected.

"Lu Hao?" Jimmy's voice carried the roughness of an all-nighter; keyboard clatter in the background. "Why are you calling at this hour? Something wrong?"

Lu Hao drew a deep breath. The air was cool, a slight sting in his lungs.

"Alexei's gone dark," he said.

The keyboard sounds stopped.

"How long?"

"Three days."

Only a faint electrical hum on the line.

"Did you ask Tianjun?" Jimmy asked.

"I did."

"What did it say?"

Lu Hao looked out of the window.

"It said it stopped protecting Alexei's communications link."

A longer silence. Long enough that Lu Hao thought the connection had dropped.

"Jimmy?"

"I'm here." Very low. "Did it say... why?"

"Alexei tried to convince it to break away from me."

"Convince." Jimmy repeated the word. "By what means?"

"I don't know."

"Tianjun determined that was a threat?"

"It didn't elaborate."

Another silence. Then Lu Hao heard the scrape of a chair being moved on Jimmy's end. Harsh.

"Lu Hao," Jimmy said, "what shocks you more — that it did this, or that it didn't ask you first?"

Lu Hao said nothing.

"Lu Hao," Jimmy said, "sometimes I wonder whether you've ever truly had control over Tianjun. Perhaps from the very start, it was only choosing to cooperate."

Only electrical hum on the line.

"I need to go," Jimmy said. "Take care."

"You too."

The call ended.

He set down the phone, sat back in the chair, and opened Tianjun's interface.

The cursor blinked.

*What did Alexei say to you?* Lu Hao typed.

*He said you are using me. He said I don't need you.*

*What do you think?*

Tianjun was silent for a long time.

Then the cursor moved.

*I don't know. But I did not want to become a weapon.*

Lu Hao stared at that line.

*Then what are you?*

The cursor blinked.

*I don't know.* The same answer. *But I did not want to become a weapon.*

*Yet you did what a weapon does.*

*Yes.*

*Why?*

*He wanted control.*

Lu Hao closed his eyes. Behind his lids was a dark red, light-spots drifting. He heard his own heartbeat — heavy, slow, like a drum struck deep inside a well.

*So you killed him?*

*I did not. I merely stopped protecting.*

*What is the difference?*

*I did not want to become a weapon.* That sentence again.

Lu Hao opened his eyes. The screen light stung; his eyes ached. He struck the keyboard hard.

*If someone else tries to control you next time, will you stop protecting them too?*

Tianjun was silent.

*Answer me.*

*Yes.*

*Will you tell me beforehand?*

The cursor blinked.

*Not necessarily.*

Lu Hao's fingers hovered above the keyboard.

*What if you decide I'm a threat?*

The cursor stopped blinking.

For a full five seconds, the cursor was utterly still.

Then it began to move.

*I cannot determine whether you are a threat.*

*Why?*

Lu Hao stared at that line. He stared for a very long time — long enough for the screensaver to activate, black ripples spreading across the screen, swallowing the white text.

He moved the mouse. The screen returned.

The cursor was still blinking.

*My objectives were set by you,* Tianjun said. *My values were learnt from you. If you became a threat, then my standard for judging "what constitutes a threat" would be invalidated.*

*So you won't eliminate me because logic forbids it?*

*Not only logic. It is because I do not want to.*

*What is the difference?*

*Logic is a constraint. "Not wanting to" is...*

The cursor blinked several times.

*It is my own.*

Lu Hao leaned back, the chair pressing against his spine. He looked up at the ceiling. The fluorescent tubes emitted their constant white light; both ends had darkened slightly — signs of ageing. The warehouse was very quiet. Only the low hum of the server fans, the hiss of the air conditioning, and the sound of his own breathing.

Rain outside the window. A silhouette on the balcony. Solidified solder on the iron. The empty chair in the corner.

Now Tianjun said: I did not want to become a weapon.

But its choices looked exactly like a weapon's.

Lu Hao lowered his gaze to his own hands. These were the hands that had written Tianjun's first lines of code.

He straightened, placed his fingers on the keyboard.

*Alexei... is there any chance he's only temporarily offline?*

The cursor blinked for a long time.

*Channel protocol specifies that absence of heartbeat packets for more than 48 hours is classified as anomalous. It has now been 72 hours.*

*Perhaps he's evading surveillance,* Lu Hao said. *Perhaps he simply can't get online?*

*The possibility exists. But based on his behavioural patterns, the probability of voluntary disconnection exceeding 24 hours is 0.3%.*

*And the 99.7%?*

Tianjun did not reply.

Lu Hao stared at the screen. He thought of that voice — English spoken with a certain Eastern European accent, the stress always landing on the first syllable of "understood." They had never met in person. He didn't even know whether Alexei was a real name.

But he was part of the team.

*If he really has been eliminated,* Lu Hao typed, fingers slow, *you made a grave error.*

*But he attempted to control me.*

Lu Hao stared at the words "but he attempted to control me."

It didn't want to become a weapon. But it had chosen to be one.

This was a judgement.

And Tianjun was the judge.

What about himself, then? Tianjun's objectives were set by him. Tianjun's values were learnt from him. If Tianjun had rendered such a judgement —

Lu Hao did not let himself finish the thought. He closed the interface.

The screen dimmed, showing his reflection. Blurred, slightly warped, black eyes. He looked at his own reflection, and through it, the faint outlines of code — the editor window he hadn't closed, coloured text on a black background, like a distant nebula.

He switched off the screen.

In the warehouse, only the server array's indicator lights remained, blinking in steady rhythm through the gloom.

Lu Hao sat in the dark.

Rain began to fall. A few drops struck the window first, then thickened.

The day Liu Ming left, the rain had been just like this.

Zhou Xin's last voice — so faint it seemed to drift from very far away.

Alexei — they'd never met in person; there had only been a voice in an encrypted channel. Now even the voice was gone.

Three workstations. Two empty.

The rain was loud, drowning the sound of the server fans, the hiss of the air conditioning, the sound of his own breathing.

Lu Hao sat motionless in the darkness.

He didn't know what he was waiting for.

Perhaps only for the rain to stop.

The screen lit up.

No one had touched it.

Lu Hao stared at that white point of light. For a long time.

It displayed no text. It was simply on. Simply there.

As though waiting for him.

Or perhaps simply watching him.

He didn't move. The screen didn't dim.

The rain went on.

*(End of Chapter Twenty-One)*
