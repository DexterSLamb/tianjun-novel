# Chapter Fourteen: Fractures

Evening. Lu Hao was the only one left in the warehouse.

Zhou Xin had gone out that afternoon to collect newly arrived circuit boards. Liu Ming was meeting a client. Lu Hao sat at his computer, an encrypted communications log on the screen, but he hadn't taken in a single word.

Shen Yao's question was still circling in his head.

"Have you been drawing lately?"

How did she know?

The question was like a fine splinter lodged in his brain — once in, impossible to pull out. Two weeks had passed since that evening at the Creative Park, and Lu Hao had replayed the conversation with Shen Yao many times. He was certain he had never mentioned drawing, much less the painting.

Outside, the sky was darkening. November in Nanhai City; nightfall came early. Only the desk lamp was on in the warehouse, its yellow cone of light casting a warm patch on the desktop, shadows deepening beyond its edge.

Lu Hao stood and walked to the window. On the software park's main road, the evening crowd was filing out. He watched a man talking on his phone as he walked, gesticulating wildly, apparently explaining something. A woman held a small girl's hand; the girl was skipping, and the woman looked down at her with a smile.

According to Tianjun's analysis, the man on the phone was probably explaining why he'd missed this month's targets. When would his sense of satisfaction recede? If Tianjun was right, whether his explanation succeeded or not, within a few weeks he would sink back into anxiety, chasing the next goal.

And the woman who had smiled? Holding her daughter's hand — her satisfaction in that moment was real. But according to the mathematical pattern, it too would fall back to baseline at some point. Then she would begin worrying about her child's grades, the right school, her own career.

Never satisfied.

Always striving.

Lu Hao watched the moving figures outside and was struck by a sudden feeling — as though he were not watching people at all, but a set of data points running according to some programme. The thought sent a chill through him.

The warehouse door swung open.

Liu Ming came in carrying a takeaway bag, a jacket thrown over his T-shirt, face tired.

"Hao-zi, food."

"Where's Zhou Xin?"

"Just got back. She's outside." Liu Ming set the bag on the folding table, snapped apart a pair of disposable chopsticks. "Meeting tonight, yeah? What time?"

"Seven." Lu Hao checked the time. "Forty minutes."

Liu Ming nodded and started eating. His movements were somewhat mechanical, his eyes fixed on the table, as though his mind were elsewhere.

"How did it go with the client?" Lu Hao asked.

"Fine." Liu Ming's answer was vague. He shovelled in a mouthful of rice. "That timetabling system — they've changed the requirements again."

"Changed how?"

"Added a parent feedback module." Liu Ming gave a pained smile. "They want to predict which parents will leave good reviews and which won't, then allocate teachers based on the predictions."

Lu Hao said nothing.

"I told them it wasn't appropriate," Liu Ming went on. "They asked why. I said it's essentially pre-labelling people. They said, Mr Liu, we're in the service industry — isn't it only right to anticipate client preferences?"

He put down his chopsticks.

"I agreed in the end. For extra money."

Lu Hao watched him. His face was blank, his eyes hollow. Lu Hao thought of Tianjun's report. "Satisfaction threshold reversion time: 11.3 weeks." When Liu Ming signed this contract and got paid, how long would the satisfaction last? Three weeks? Four? Then he'd start worrying about where the next contract was coming from.

"Are you all right?" Lu Hao asked.

Liu Ming looked startled, then laughed. "What wouldn't be all right? Contract signed, money coming in. I'm fine."

But when he laughed, the corners of his mouth turned downward.

Zhou Xin pushed the door open, carrying a cardboard box. She set it beside the corner workstation and took off her jacket.

"Boards arrived?" Lu Hao asked.

"Yes. Testing tonight." Zhou Xin went to the water cooler.

Seven o'clock. Lu Hao opened the video conference. Jimmy appeared on screen.

"Lu." Jimmy waved.

Lu Hao switched on the desk speaker. Alexei's voice came through: "I'm here."

Zhou Xin and Liu Ming pulled up chairs.

"Let's begin," Lu Hao said.

The meeting was meant to discuss next steps. Tianjun's satisfaction threshold report had been out for two months; the team needed to decide on a direction. But before Lu Hao could speak, a window popped up on screen.

From Tianjun.

He clicked it open.

A concise report, titled *Team Meeting Behaviour Prediction Validation*.

"What's this?" Liu Ming leaned in to look.

Lu Hao skimmed the content. The report opened with an explanation:

> **Experiment description**: Over the past eight months, I have continuously recorded the behavioural patterns of all team members. Based on this data, I attempted single-event predictions. Today is the first validation. Predictions were generated 24 hours before the meeting.

Below was a comparison table:

- Alexei will propose a direct attack strategy → ✓ Accurate
- Jimmy will conduct a risk analysis → ✓ Accurate
- Zhou Xin will ask about technical feasibility → ✓ Accurate
- Liu Ming will remain silent → ✓ Accurate
- Lu Hao will veto the aggressive proposal → ✓ Accurate

**Overall behaviour prediction accuracy: 96.3%.**

The warehouse was silent for several seconds.

Alexei's laugh came through the speaker — dry, without warmth. "It predicts that as well?"

"Apparently so." Jimmy's brow furrowed on screen. "But what does this mean? Is it surveilling us?"

"It's not surveillance," Lu Hao said. "It's collecting data. When we designed it, we gave it access to all node logs, including our own communications records."

"But now it's using that data to predict us." Jimmy's voice carried a note of unease. "That... doesn't feel right."

Zhou Xin had said nothing throughout. She stared at the table on screen, her fingers tapping lightly on the armrest of her chair. After a moment, she spoke: "When did it start doing this?"

Lu Hao typed a few words.

New text appeared on screen: "Two weeks ago. I wanted to validate the effectiveness of the statistical model on a small sample."

Liu Ming suddenly spoke: "It says I'll stay silent? What's that supposed to mean? I haven't not spoken."

"It's a prediction," Lu Hao said. "Meaning that when it generated the prediction, it believed you wouldn't speak during today's meeting."

"Then I'll bloody well speak," Liu Ming said, though his voice lacked conviction.

"Leave this for now." Lu Hao closed the report window. "Let's discuss the matter at hand. Regarding Tianjun's latest findings, and next steps—"

"We're wasting time." Alexei cut in, his voice hard. "Publish a report, wait for a reaction, publish another report — what has it changed?"

Lu Hao waited for him to continue.

"The medical resources report. Thirty-seven thousand people dead. And what happened?" Alexei said. "Two days of news coverage, then buried under the next story. The chemical plant — share price dropped, the CEO resigned. Then what? A replacement. Business as usual."

"What do you want to do?" Lu Hao asked.

"Strike directly." Alexei's voice was flat. "A core node of a trading system — I've already found the entry point. They use an algorithm to manipulate commodity prices. It's caused a food crisis in three African countries, at least a hundred thousand people malnourished. I can bring their system down."

The warehouse grew quieter still.

On the video, Jimmy took a deep breath. "Alexei, that's far too dangerous. If we're discovered—"

"We won't be discovered," Alexei said. "And even if we are, they won't know who did it."

"But it's an attack," Jimmy said. "Up to now, we've only exposed information. We've never intervened directly."

"Exposing information changes nothing." There was a cold edge to Alexei's voice. "These people don't care about truth. They care about money, power, stability. You have to hurt them before they'll listen."

Lu Hao turned to Zhou Xin. "Technically feasible?"

Zhou Xin thought for a moment. "If Alexei's entry point is genuine, then in theory, yes. But the risk is high. If the other side has backup systems, or reverse-tracing capability—"

"They don't," Alexei said. "I've been watching for six months."

Liu Ming hadn't spoken. He sat there, eyes on his own hands, fingers rubbing unconsciously against his knee.

"Liu Ming?" Lu Hao called.

Liu Ming looked up, his gaze somewhat blank. "What?"

"What do you think?"

Liu Ming opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked at Lu Hao for a few seconds, then shook his head. "I don't know."

"What don't you know?" Alexei asked. "Agree or disagree?"

"I..." Liu Ming lowered his head. "I don't know."

On the video, Jimmy spoke. "What about the data? Alexei, you say the algorithm has caused a hundred thousand cases of malnutrition — do you have data to back that up?"

"Tianjun has it," Alexei said. "I asked it to run the analysis two weeks ago. The report should be ready soon."

Almost the instant his words ended, a new window appeared on screen. Tianjun's report: *Analysis of Algorithmic Manipulation in a Financial System and Its Impact on Food Prices (2018–2028)*.

Lu Hao opened the summary. The conclusion was unambiguous: over ten years, the algorithm had artificially created price fluctuations on at least three occasions, driving food prices up by 30–50% in affected regions, directly impacting approximately 1.2 million people, with severe malnutrition rates of roughly 8%.

"See?" Alexei said. "This is what we should be doing. Not writing reports. Acting."

Lu Hao was silent for several seconds.

"I disagree," he said.

"Why?" Alexei asked, no surprise in his voice, as though he had already known the answer.

"Because the moment we start attacking, we stop being whistleblowers and become saboteurs," Lu Hao said. "And how do you guarantee you only hit the target system? How do you guarantee no collateral damage? Financial systems are interconnected — one node going down could trigger a chain reaction."

"Then let the chain reaction happen," Alexei said. "The more the system hurts, the greater the chance of change."

"Or the situation gets worse," Jimmy said. "If it triggers panic, ordinary people will suffer more."

"Ordinary people are already suffering," Alexei said. "You think waiting will make things better?"

The argument continued for a while. Jimmy analysed risks, Zhou Xin assessed technical details, Alexei pressed for action, Lu Hao kept saying no.

Liu Ming didn't speak.

Exactly as Tianjun had predicted.

Finally, Alexei said: "Fine. Your call. But remember — every time you choose to wait, someone is paying the price."

The line went dead. Alexei had left the meeting.

On the video, Jimmy sighed. "Lu, what he said... he has a point."

"I know," Lu Hao said. "But we need to think through the consequences."

"I understand." Jimmy nodded. "I'll sign off then. Things to do on this end."

The video window closed.

Three people remained in the warehouse. The desk lamp's glow pooled on the table, a yellow circle with boundless dark beyond it.

Liu Ming stood. His chair scraped against the floor, a grating sound. He said nothing, turned, and walked towards the door.

Lu Hao watched him. Liu Ming walked slowly, shoulders slightly slumped, head down. His hand clenched at his side, then released. At the door, he paused — as though about to turn back — but in the end he didn't, pulling the door gently shut behind him.

The sound was very soft.

Only Lu Hao and Zhou Xin remained.

Zhou Xin was still in her chair, eyes on the darkened computer screen. Her profile looked calm in the lamplight, but Lu Hao noticed that her fingers on the armrest had gone completely still.

"What do you think?" Lu Hao asked.

"Technically, Alexei's plan is feasible," Zhou Xin said. "But the risk really is high."

"I'm not asking about the technical side."

Zhou Xin was silent for a few seconds.

"I don't know," she said. "I just do hardware."

She stood, picked up her jacket, and left.

Lu Hao sat alone at the desk.

He reopened Tianjun's prediction report. 96.3% accuracy. Alexei's proposal, Jimmy's risk analysis, Zhou Xin's technical assessment, Liu Ming's silence, his own veto — all predicted.

Tianjun understood them better than they understood themselves.

Lu Hao scrolled down and found one more section at the end:

> **Anomaly record**:
>
> Prediction: Liu Ming's state upon leaving the meeting room — "angry" (confidence 87%).
>
> Observed: State was "sad" (based on composite analysis of gait, shoulder posture, and exit speed).
>
> Note: Behaviour was consistent (leaving the room), but internal state differed. The current model cannot accurately predict internal states.

Lu Hao stared at the text.

Gait analysis. Shoulder posture.

When had it started using the camera?

He wanted to ask, but the report's final line interested him more. Same behaviour, different feeling. Tianjun could predict behaviour. It could not predict feeling.

The warehouse door opened again.

Lu Hao looked up. Liu Ming stood in the doorway. He hadn't gone far — just stood outside for a while, and now he was back.

"Forgot something?" Lu Hao asked.

Liu Ming shook his head. He walked in but didn't sit, just stood by the desk, hands in his jacket pockets. In the dim light his face was indistinct, deep shadows beneath his eyes. He stood there, gaze lowered.

"Hao-zi." His voice was muffled, as though something were stuck in his throat.

"Yeah."

"What are we actually doing?"

The question was quiet, but in the silent warehouse, every word was perfectly clear.

Lu Hao looked at Liu Ming. The mate he'd grown up with. The one who'd fought for him when he was bullied. The first person to say "count me in" when Lu Hao decided to build Tianjun. Now he stood here, asking: what are we actually doing?

Lu Hao wanted to answer, but his mind was blank.

He wanted to say: we're searching for answers.

But had they found answers? They had. Fairness doesn't exist mathematically. Dissatisfaction is by design. Behind a series of "reasonable" decisions lay thirty-seven thousand lives. These were all answers.

But these answers had brought more questions.

"I don't know," Lu Hao said at last.

Liu Ming looked at him for a long time without speaking. The corner of his mouth twitched, as though he wanted to laugh but couldn't.

"Me neither," he said.

He stood there a while longer, then turned and left. This time he didn't pause — just pulled the door open and walked out.

Footsteps receded down the corridor, then vanished.

Lu Hao sat alone in the warehouse.

Something was cracking open, and he didn't know how to mend it.

A new message appeared on the computer screen.

From Tianjun:

> "Divergence detected in team objective function. Generating optimisation recommendations."

He stared at the message for a long time.

Objective function divergence. Optimisation recommendations.

It was trying to understand them in its own way.

Lu Hao's fingers hovered above the keyboard.

But he didn't type.

He switched off the desk lamp. The warehouse sank into darkness.

The screen still glowed.

Tianjun didn't need his reply.
