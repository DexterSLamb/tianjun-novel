# Chapter Fifteen: Feelings

"This data isn't right."

Jimmy's voice came through the speaker, carrying an uncommon note of bewilderment. His face leant forward in the video window, the screen's glow turning his eyebrows pale gold.

Lu Hao stared at the report on his own screen. Tianjun had auto-pushed this document five minutes ago, under a simple title: *Preliminary Results of Feeling Prediction Experiment*. He had only just opened it, hadn't had time to look closely, when Jimmy spoke.

The warehouse was quiet. Zhou Xin sat at her workstation, turned sideways to watch. Liu Ming stood by the window, his back to them, holding a bottle of water he hadn't drunk from. The evening light slanted through the gaps in the blinds, casting alternating bands of light and shadow across the floor.

"What's wrong with it?" Lu Hao asked.

"Look at page three," Jimmy said. "Behaviour prediction accuracy 94.7%, feeling prediction accuracy 31.2%. The gap is enormous."

Lu Hao scrolled down. The report was formatted cleanly, the same as Tianjun's previous analyses. The experimental design section stated: over the past two weeks, Tianjun had conducted a "choice–feeling" comparative experiment on team members. The method was to record each person's choices in specific situations, then ask them afterwards to describe their feelings at the time, and compare Tianjun's predictions against actuality.

Below was a data table. Five names, two columns of figures. The behaviour prediction column showed numbers close together: Zhou Xin 96.2%, Liu Ming 93.1%, Jimmy 94.8%, Alexei 95.5%, Lu Hao 94.1%. Average 94.7%.

The feeling prediction column was scattered: Zhou Xin 28.7%, Liu Ming 34.2%, Jimmy 29.5%, Alexei 31.8%, Lu Hao 31.9%. Average 31.2%.

"How did it measure feelings?" Jimmy asked. "I had absolutely no idea it was running this experiment."

Lu Hao typed a few words. Tianjun's reply appeared in the dialogue window:

*"The experiment was based on composite analysis of visual, audio, and behavioural pattern data. All data collection fell within existing access permissions."*

"Cameras?" Jimmy's voice rose. "It's using cameras to analyse our facial expressions?"

*"Facial expression is a weakly correlated indicator of feeling, with limited accuracy. This experiment primarily combined situational context, choice patterns, historical data, and micro-expressions for composite inference."*

Zhou Xin turned her head towards the surveillance camera in the corner of the warehouse. An ordinary network camera, mounted on top of the bookshelf, pointing across the entire space. Normally used for remote equipment checks, its small red light always on.

"Did we agree to this?" she asked.

Lu Hao didn't answer. He recalled that when designing Tianjun's data collection protocol, he had indeed written a clause: "The system may collect necessary data to optimise its analytical models." At the time he had meant node operation data, network traffic, analysis task logs. He hadn't imagined it would extend to this.

But Tianjun's logic was self-consistent — if it was to analyse human behaviour, it needed human behavioural data. Including expressions. Including tone of voice. Including whether someone stood up or sat down at a given moment, reached for water or rubbed their temples.

"When did it start?" Liu Ming said abruptly. He hadn't turned round; he was still looking out the window. His voice was slightly muffled.

Lu Hao typed the question to Tianjun.

*"Experiment initiated: 2028.10.17. Trigger condition: divergence in team objective function detected following last week's meeting. Experiment purpose: to validate whether feeling prediction can improve collaboration efficiency."*

17th October. A week ago. The day after the last meeting.

"So it decided we'd had a row and started studying us?" Jimmy said. "That feels... uncomfortable."

The warehouse was quiet for a moment. Only the low hum of the server fans.

Lu Hao read further. The conclusion section was brief:

*"Core finding: Behaviour prediction accuracy is significantly higher than feeling prediction accuracy (p<0.001). Behaviour leaves data traces — keystrokes, paths, speech. Feelings do not. The system can predict that an individual will choose A, but cannot accurately predict the feeling state accompanying that choice (relief, resentment, numbness, etc.). This disparity remains stable across different contexts."*

He stared at that last line.

Feelings leave no data traces.

"Ask it why," Jimmy said. "Why can't it predict feelings?"

Lu Hao typed the question.

Tianjun's answer came quickly:

*"Feelings have no stable data-mapping relationship. The same behaviour (e.g., silence) may correspond to anger, sadness, fatigue, contemplation, or other feeling states depending on context. Moreover, individuals' descriptions of their own feelings contain subjective bias. Behaviour has externally observable signals; feelings have only internal reports, and internal reports themselves may be inaccurate."*

"So what it's saying," Jimmy said slowly, "is that it can see what we do. But what we feel — only we know that. And even we might not be able to articulate it clearly?"

*"That is a reasonable interpretation."*

Zhou Xin stood and walked over to Lu Hao. She leant down to look at the screen, her hair falling forward, partially hiding her profile. Her eyes scanned the numbers, lingering for a few seconds.

"It's learning," she said.

"Learning what?" Lu Hao asked.

"Us." Zhou Xin straightened up. "Before, it was analysing large samples — hundreds of thousands of people, hundreds of years, dozens of countries. Now it's learning the five of us. Small sample, deep analysis."

"Why?"

Zhou Xin didn't answer. She walked back to her workstation, sat down, and resumed typing. The keystrokes were soft but rapid.

Liu Ming finally turned round. The water bottle in his hand was slightly crushed, the plastic crackling. He looked at Lu Hao — his gaze a touch vacant, as though looking at him and through him at the same time.

"So," Liu Ming began, his voice dry, "it can now predict 94% of our behaviour?"

"It appears so," Lu Hao said.

"And the other 6%?"

Lu Hao looked at the screen. Tianjun had already answered:

*"The 6% margin of error arises primarily from extreme situations, insufficient information, or individuals deliberately acting counter to prediction."*

"Deliberately." Liu Ming repeated the word and gave a brief laugh — so short it was barely visible. "So if we want it to get things wrong, we have to do something on purpose?"

*"Theoretically feasible. However, 'deliberately' acting may itself fall within the predictive range."*

Liu Ming said nothing. He unscrewed the cap, took a large gulp, his Adam's apple bobbing. Then he replaced the cap and set the bottle on the windowsill.

"I'm going for a cigarette," he said, and walked to the door.

The door opened, then closed. Footsteps faded down the corridor.

On the video, Jimmy sighed.

"Lu, something's off here," he said. "Now it's predicting who'll say what in our meetings. What's next? Predicting what we'll do tomorrow? Next week?"

"It may already be doing that," Lu Hao said.

Jimmy was silent for a moment.

"That feeling prediction figure," he said, changing the subject. "31.2%. It's an interesting number. If it were pure random guessing — one in five — accuracy would be about 20%. 31.2% is above random, but not by much. So it is picking up some signals — expressions, tone, context — but nowhere near enough."

"So feelings are partly predictable, mostly not," Lu Hao said.

"Right." Jimmy paused. "It reminds me of an old question: if a machine could perfectly predict your behaviour, would you still have free will?"

"This machine is saying it can't predict perfectly," Lu Hao said. "Not feelings, at least."

"But behaviour, it can," Jimmy said. "94.7% — that's very close to perfect."

Lu Hao looked at the numbers on screen. 94.7%. 31.2%. Side by side, like doorways into two different worlds.

In one world, what you do can be almost entirely predicted.

In the other, what you feel remains obscure.

He thought of Tianjun's earlier report. Satisfaction thresholds. Never satisfied. Always striving. If behaviour was designed, did the design include feelings?

If it did, why couldn't they be predicted?

If it didn't, then what were feelings?

His phone buzzed. Lu Hao picked it up.

A message from Shen Yao: *Tonight, eight. The usual place. Something to discuss.*

He checked the time: twenty to seven.

"I'll sign off then," Jimmy said on the video.

"Right."

The video window closed. Only Lu Hao and Zhou Xin remained in the warehouse. Zhou Xin was still typing, a waveform on her screen jumping in rhythm.

Lu Hao closed Tianjun's report window and opened the code editor. The cursor blinked in the blank space.

He typed a line:

```
// Question: if behaviour is predictable but feelings are not,
// should the definition of "self" be based on behaviour or feeling?
```

Then he stopped.

Outside, the sky had darkened.

---

Creative Park, Building C3, Room 307.

Lu Hao knocked. The door opened, and the warm glow of the floor lamp softened Shen Yao's outline.

Lu Hao stepped inside. The room was the same as before — spare, almost nothing superfluous. A faint scent of tea hung in the air.

Shen Yao closed the door and walked to the small bar.

"Tea?" she asked. "Just brewed."

"Sure."

She poured two cups and brought them over. Lu Hao sat on the sofa; Shen Yao took the chair opposite. The cups were white ceramic, thin — he could feel the warmth of the tea through the walls.

"Has Tianjun made a new discovery?" Shen Yao asked. She wasn't looking at him, blowing gently on the steam rising from her cup.

"How did you know?"

"A guess." Shen Yao looked up. "You've probably been thinking about the satisfaction threshold report for days. At Tianjun's learning speed, if there were a new finding, it wouldn't take long."

Lu Hao sipped his tea. Slightly bitter, with a sweet aftertaste.

"It ran an experiment," he said. "Predicting our behaviour, and our feelings."

"The results?"

"Behaviour prediction 94.7%. Feeling prediction 31.2%."

Shen Yao's finger traced the rim of her cup. Her nails were neatly trimmed, unpainted.

"A large gap," she said.

"Yes."

"Did it explain why?"

"It said feelings leave no data traces." Lu Hao paused. "Behaviour leaves records — what key you pressed, what route you took, what you said. But feelings don't."

Shen Yao was quiet for a while. She raised her cup, took a small sip, set it down.

"So it can't predict what you're feeling sitting here drinking tea," she said.

"It can't."

"Could it predict you'd come?"

Lu Hao looked at her.

Shen Yao's expression was calm, but something shallow moved in her eyes, like light at the bottom of water.

"Could it predict you'd contact me?" Lu Hao asked.

"No." Shen Yao said. "What I'm asking is whether it could predict you would come — at the moment you received my message, could it predict you'd choose to come?"

Lu Hao thought about it.

"If it analysed my historical behaviour patterns, it probably could," he said. "I've always come before."

"But it couldn't predict what you'd feel when you arrived."

"No."

Shen Yao nodded. Her finger tapped once, very lightly, against her knee — like keeping time.

"The reason I asked you here today," she said, "is that Tianjun predicted I would."

Lu Hao stiffened.

"What?"

"Yesterday afternoon, I received an anonymous email." Shen Yao stood, walked to the desk, picked up the tablet, tapped the screen, brought it back and handed it to Lu Hao. "The content was simple — just a prediction report. The sender's address was encrypted. Untraceable."

Lu Hao took the tablet. On the screen was a short document, formatted like Tianjun's reports but more concise:

**Subject: Shen Yao
Prediction: Will contact Lu Hao on the evening of 2028.11.3
Basis: Historical interaction pattern + current task progress
Confidence: 91.7%**

Below, in small print:

*"Note: This prediction is based solely on observable behavioural data and does not include feeling states."*

"It predicted you as well," Lu Hao said.

"It knows I exist," Shen Yao said. "From the first time we met at the summit — you discussed me with your team afterwards. Tianjun has the records."

Lu Hao handed the tablet back. Shen Yao took it, switched off the screen, and placed it on the desk.

"So you invited me here to verify its prediction?" Lu Hao asked.

"Partly." Shen Yao sat down. "I wanted to see whether, knowing I'd been predicted, I would still act as predicted."

"And?"

"I invited you." Shen Yao said. "So the prediction was correct."

"But what about your feelings?" Lu Hao asked. "Did it predict how you'd feel when you invited me?"

Shen Yao looked at him. The floor lamp lit her from the side, casting fine shadows from her eyelashes.

"It didn't predict that," she said. "The report states it — feeling states not included."

"Do you know yourself?"

The room fell silent. Outside, the Creative Park was hushed. An occasional car passed, distant, like background noise.

Shen Yao's finger stilled on the teacup. Her gaze fell to the surface of the tea, where faint ripples slowly settled.

"It predicted I was here to... accomplish something," she said lightly.

"Is that true?"

Shen Yao looked up.

"I don't know," she said. "Perhaps. Perhaps not only that. Perhaps both."

Lu Hao said nothing.

"Perhaps that's the meaning of feelings," he said.

"What?"

"Uncertain. Not in the data. Unpredictable." Lu Hao paused. "And therefore real."

"And therefore real?" she repeated.

"Perhaps," Lu Hao said.

Shen Yao looked away. She raised her cup but didn't drink, just held it. Steam curled upward, dissipating before her face.

"In my father's last days," she said suddenly, "he often said something."

Lu Hao waited.

"He said that being alive means walking a tightrope between two kinds of truth." Shen Yao's voice was very soft. "One is the truth you see. The other is the truth you feel. Sometimes they're the same. Sometimes they're not. But you can't trust only one — or you'll fall."

"What did he mean?"

"He reported on certain things." Shen Yao said. "He used to say, data is one thing, how people feel is another. Both are real, but there's always a crack between them."

"Which did he believe?"

"Both." Shen Yao said. "He said the question isn't which is more real — it's why the crack exists."

She set her cup down. Ceramic met wood with the faintest sound.

"Tianjun is looking for that crack now," she said. "Behaviour is one truth, feeling is another. 94.7% and 31.2% — that's the crack."

Lu Hao thought of the numbers in the report. Two percentages, 63.5 points between them. A very wide crack.

"If feelings can't be predicted," Shen Yao continued, "what does that mean? That the design — if there is a design — only designed behaviour, not feelings? Or that it designed feelings too, but not well enough, leaving a crack?"

"What do you think?" Lu Hao asked.

Shen Yao was silent for a long time. Her fingers traced absent patterns on the desktop.

"I think," she said slowly, "that if I were the designer, I would have designed feelings in as well. Because feelings drive behaviour. You feel hungry, so you look for food. You feel cold, so you put on more clothes. You feel something is unfair, so you want to change it."

"But Tianjun says feelings can't be accurately predicted."

"So either the design failed," Shen Yao looked at Lu Hao, "or feelings are... outside the design."

Outside the design.

Something stirred in Lu Hao. The phrase was like a key sliding into a lock he had been unable to open.

"Outside the design," he repeated.

"Perhaps feelings are a bug," Shen Yao said. "Something the system generated on its own during operation, not part of the original blueprint. Or—"

She stopped.

"Or what?"

Shen Yao shook her head and said no more. She stood and walked to the window. It was large; outside, the Creative Park at night — a few factory windows lit up, like blocks floating in the dark.

"That painting you did as a child," she said suddenly, her back to him. "The one of your father's back."

Lu Hao's fingers tightened slightly. He had known she would bring it up, but hearing it still sent a faint chill through him.

"How do you know about it?" he asked.

"I looked into your background," Shen Yao said, her voice calm. "Occupational requirement. I need to know who I'm dealing with."

"How thoroughly?"

"Basic information. Family, education, work history." Shen Yao paused. "And that art competition. The public reporting mentioned it — said you painted 'a man's back.'"

Lu Hao watched her. Her explanation was plausible, but not quite sufficient. Public reporting wouldn't have said he'd painted his father. It wouldn't.

But he didn't press.

Some questions, once asked, might shatter the delicate state of things as they stood.

"That painting," Shen Yao said, returning to sit down, "if you looked at it now, would you think it lacked emotion?"

Lu Hao considered.

"No," he said. "Now I think... there was too much emotion. So much I didn't know how to express it."

"So the judges were wrong?"

"The judges saw what they could see," Lu Hao said. "They needed explicit expression — a smile for happiness, tears for sadness. A back, with no face, no expression — they didn't know what to make of it."

"But you did."

"Because it was my father." Lu Hao paused. "I knew what was in his back when he stood on the balcony."

"What was there?"

Lu Hao was silent for a few seconds.

"A great deal," he said at last. "Regret, duty, persistence, silence... too many things pressed together, becoming a state without a name. The painting wasn't about 'emotion.' It was about 'so much emotion it defies naming.'"

Shen Yao nodded.

"When my father died," she said, "his eyes were open. The doctor said he was probably trying to see to the very end. But I don't think so."

She paused.

"I think he was... confirming. Confirming that what he felt and what he saw were the same world."

"And then?"

"Then he closed them." Shen Yao said. "When he did, his expression was peaceful. As though he'd finished confirming, and could go."

The room fell quiet again. The floor lamp's circle of light lay warm on the floorboards, and beyond it, boundless dark.

Lu Hao's phone buzzed. He took it out. A message from Zhou Xin:

*Node 19 latency anomaly. Needs investigation. When are you back?*

He typed: *About an hour.*

Sent.

Shen Yao watched him put the phone away.

"Leaving?"

"Something's come up." Lu Hao stood.

Shen Yao stood too. She walked him to the door. It opened, and the corridor light flooded in, brighter than the room. Lu Hao stepped out and turned.

Shen Yao stood in the doorway, light from behind tracing a faint luminous edge around her silhouette.

"Lu Hao," she said.

"Yes?"

"That prediction report," she said, "there was one more line at the bottom. I didn't show you earlier."

Lu Hao waited.

Something flickered in Shen Yao's eyes — quick, gone in a flash.

"It predicted I'd have insomnia tonight," she said. "Confidence 87.3%."

"Will you?" he asked.

The corner of Shen Yao's mouth moved.

"I don't know," she said. "Perhaps. Perhaps not only that."

Lu Hao nodded.

"Take care getting home," Shen Yao said.

The door closed softly.

He thought of Tianjun's two numbers. 94.7%. 31.2%.

Behaviour predictable. Feelings unpredictable.

The crack.

If feelings truly were something outside the design — if they really were a bug, something the system had generated on its own, absent from the original blueprint — what did that mean?

That even if behaviour was designed, feelings might be free?

That even if we walk along a predetermined track, the scenery along it is our own?

He reached the underground station and tapped through the barriers. The carriage was mostly empty. He found a seat by the side and sat down.

His phone buzzed again. Liu Ming this time:

*Hao-zi. Not coming to the warehouse tomorrow. Something's come up.*

Lu Hao stared at the message. Liu Ming's tone was flat, but behind those words — "something's come up" — lay a thin sheet of paper covering something else.

He typed: *OK. Need any help?*

He waited. No reply.

The train hurtled through the tunnel, his own face reflected in the window glass. Blurred, wavering, like a reflection in water.

He wondered: could Tianjun have predicted Liu Ming would send that message? Could it predict that Liu Ming genuinely "had something come up"? Would 94.7% encompass this kind of sudden decision?

More importantly, could Tianjun predict what Liu Ming was feeling when he sent it?

31.2%.

No.

Lu Hao leant back and closed his eyes.

---

By the time he returned to the warehouse, it was nearly ten.

Zhou Xin was still at her workstation, headphones on, staring at the screen. She heard the door and slipped off one ear.

"Found the issue with Node 19," she said. "Network fluctuation, not hardware."

"Good." Lu Hao took off his jacket and walked to his computer.

The screen was still on. Tianjun's dialogue window still open. The last message was from Tianjun:

*"Continue feeling prediction experiment?"*

Lu Hao's fingers hovered above the keyboard.

He looked at that line for a long time.

Then he typed:

*"Continue."*

Sent.

Almost immediately, a new message appeared:

*"Phase two of the experiment has been planned. Objective: to explore whether feeling prediction accuracy can be improved through more refined data collection."*

Lu Hao stared at the words. More refined data collection. What did that mean? More cameras? Denser sensors? Or something else entirely?

He didn't ask.

He closed the dialogue window and opened the node monitoring interface. Seventy-two green dots pulsing steadily. All normal.

Outside, Nanhai City's lights spread through the night, boundless.

Behind every point of light, a person.

What they were doing — Tianjun probably knew, 94.7% of the time.

What they were feeling — Tianjun probably didn't.

The crack.

Lu Hao leant back in his chair, watching those blinking green dots.

His phone buzzed once more. He picked it up.

From Shen Yao. Just two words:

*"Asleep now."*

Those two words, in his mind, layered over Tianjun's prediction report.

He drew a deep breath, then let it out slowly.

---

*(End of Chapter Fifteen)*
