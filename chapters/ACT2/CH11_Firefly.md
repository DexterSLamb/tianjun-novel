# Chapter Eleven: Firefly

Deep into the small hours. The only sound in the warehouse was the server fans.

Zhou Xin sat at her workstation, three monitors glowing. On the left, node monitoring — seventy-two green dots scattered sparsely across the map like a broken constellation. In the centre, the real-time log, text scrolling upward at a steady pace. On the right, her own debugging interface, waveforms jumping against a dark background, peaks staying just below the 9.8-watt threshold line.

She'd just finished a remote diagnostic on node 47 — latency anomaly. She'd rewritten the priority rules. Latency was down from over three hundred milliseconds to just above a hundred. Usable range.

Task done. She should have gone to the camp bed for a lie-down.

She didn't move.

Because on the centre monitor, the log had begun scrolling again.

She hadn't triggered it.

**[Task ID]** T-2028-0915-007
**[Title]** *Cross-Cultural Statistical Analysis of Irrational Sacrifice Behaviour*
**[Trigger Method]** Autonomous (logic chain satisfied)
**[Data Sources]** Survivor testimonies from historical disasters (37 events), wartime archives (12 countries), organ donation follow-up tracking database (anonymised), anonymous interview transcripts (1980-2027)
**[Analysis Objective]** Identify statistical correlation patterns between "sacrificers" and "beneficiaries"
**[Current Status]** Data preprocessing phase (estimated duration: 48 hours)

Zhou Xin stared at the title.

She pulled up the trigger condition log. This wasn't a simple threshold trigger — Tianjun had constructed a logic chain of its own. Starting from the altruistic behaviour analysis, it had identified a subset of samples exhibiting traits that "inevitably lead to self-destruction," then autonomously generated a hypothesis: does this trait show cross-cultural consistency? It then launched a new task to verify.

Every step had a timestamp, intermediate variables, confidence scores.

The logic was complete. The chain was clear.

But she hadn't written it. Lu Hao hadn't written it. No one had written it.

Tianjun had written it itself.

Zhou Xin leant back, hands resting on the keyboard, not typing. Outside the window, Nanhai's late-night skyline — a few office buildings still lit. The warehouse was quiet, just the low-frequency hum of the fans.

She thought back to designing the Firefly nodes.

That was the winter of 2025, shortly after she'd joined the team. Lu Hao showed her the architecture diagram and said he needed a type of edge computing unit — small, energy-efficient, capable of running unattended for long periods. She said "I can try," then spent three months designing it from scratch.

Late one night, whilst drawing the circuit diagrams, a metaphor had come to her.

Computing power should be like fireflies.

Not the sun, not a searchlight — fireflies. One is barely visible, but tens of thousands together can trace lines of light through the darkness. Distributed, autonomous, faint but enduring.

She'd written the idea in the design document's comments.

Now, seventy-two fireflies were running.

Zhou Xin opened the drawer and took out a notebook with a manila cover. Paper records were her habit — things on screens disappeared too easily. Paper didn't.

She turned to a fresh page and wrote the date: 2028.9.15.

Then:

> Tianjun has begun autonomously generating analysis tasks.
>
> Logic chain is complete, but direction is unknown.

The pen tip lingered on the paper. Ink bled into a small dot.

She added one more line:

> When fireflies gather, what do they illuminate?

She closed the notebook and locked it in the drawer.

She didn't tell Lu Hao straightaway.

She wanted to think it through on her own first.

—-

Ten in the morning. Lu Hao arrived.

Zhou Xin heard the door but didn't turn round. Her eyes tracked a power consumption curve, fingers sliding across the touchpad, zooming into a particular peak. Footsteps approached and stopped beside her workstation.

"How's node 47?" Lu Hao asked.

"Sorted." Her voice was flat.

"Good."

He walked away and sat down at his own computer. Zhou Xin watched him from the corner of her eye.

He wore a dark grey shirt today, sleeves rolled to the forearms. His hair was slightly dishevelled, as if he'd just run a hand through it. He sat down; the screen lit his profile with blue light.

He was facing the screen, but his focus wasn't there. He seemed lost in thought.

Zhou Xin had noticed this change starting two weeks ago. That day he'd come back from outside carrying some unfamiliar air about him — as though he'd just returned from somewhere far away and his thoughts hadn't quite followed him home. After that, it happened often. Sitting there, motionless.

Once she'd seen him staring at a piece of paper.

Old paper, yellowed, edges worn. She'd caught only a glimpse, hadn't made out the contents. But he'd looked at it for a long time, seemingly working something out.

She thought of the time in March.

He'd come back from the summit and said he'd met someone. Shen Yao. Security consultant.

She'd said: "She's watching you."

He'd said: "I know."

Then never mentioned it again.

Zhou Xin wasn't the sort to press. Life had taught her: some questions are better left unasked. The answer only makes things worse. Better to watch, wait, remember.

But she could see it.

He was different.

She'd noticed the rhythm of his typing had changed — used to be steady; now it faltered occasionally. She'd seen that he started rubbing his temples on the second cup of coffee; it used to be the third. She'd noticed the angle of his jacket on the chair back — lately it was always crooked, tossed rather than hung, not straightened the way he used to.

Small details. Too small for anyone else to notice.

But she noticed.

Because her eyes kept drifting towards him, involuntarily. Watching the crease between his brows when he frowned. The slight press of his lips when he was thinking. The way his shoulders sagged a fraction when he was tired. She knew his every movement the way she knew her own circuit diagrams — where the signal should flow, where it should be earthed, where it was liable to overheat.

And now, something in that diagram was wrong.

The signal was running a different way.

Liu Ming pushed through the door carrying two bags of breakfast.

"Morning." He set one bag on Zhou Xin's desk. "Steamed buns. Soya milk."

"Thanks."

Liu Ming gave the other bag to Lu Hao, then dragged a chair over and sat down, eating as he spoke. "Hao-zi, let's have a meeting tonight. There's something we need to discuss."

Lu Hao came back from wherever his mind had been. "What is it?"

"There's been movement outside." Liu Ming bit into a bun. "A few of my long-term clients have been called in for talks recently. Asked whether they know any teams doing 'independent data analysis.'"

The warehouse went quiet for a moment.

Lu Hao set down his soya milk. "Who's asking?"

"They wouldn't say." Liu Ming paused. "I think... those reports Tianjun put out. Someone's noticed."

Zhou Xin's fingers stopped on the touchpad.

She thought of the healthcare resource report. Thirty-seven thousand people. After the report spread, it made the news, and then... nothing. As if nothing had happened.

But someone remembered.

"Eight o'clock tonight, then." Lu Hao checked the time. "I'll contact Alexei and Jimmy."

Zhou Xin said nothing, her gaze returning to the screen.

The waveform was still jumping, but her attention had scattered.

—-

Eight o'clock. Every light in the warehouse was on.

Three chairs round the folding table — Lu Hao, Zhou Xin, Liu Ming. A speaker sat on the table surface; Alexei was on audio. On another laptop screen, Jimmy's face appeared in a video window, his background the desk of his new place in England — considerably more spartan than his Longton flat.

"Everyone's here," Lu Hao said. "Let's begin."

Liu Ming spoke first.

He went through the situation again in detail: which clients had been called in, what they'd been asked. Then he said: "What I'm saying is, we're too visible now. Every report Tianjun publishes adds another degree of risk."

Alexei's voice came through the speaker, processed through encryption, carrying a faint electronic texture. "You want to stop?"

"I'm not saying stop." Liu Ming leant forward, looking at Lu Hao. "Hao-zi, I'm saying we need to keep a lower profile. Have you thought about this — are we still looking for answers, or are we just trying to prove what we already believe?"

Lu Hao didn't answer at once.

He sat there, fingers tapping lightly on the table surface. Not fast. Very rhythmic. One, then another, like a pendulum.

Zhou Xin watched his hand. She drifted slightly.

"Someone showed me something." Lu Hao spoke suddenly, his voice calm. "A research report from 1987. From the Academy of Social Sciences. It was shut down."

Liu Ming frowned. "Who showed you?"

"Doesn't matter." Lu Hao said. "What matters is the content. The report studied 'non-evolutionary traits' in human behaviour — why people do things that aren't in their own interest. The group collected data for five years, analysed it for three, then the project was terminated."

He paused.

"But the report contains one line of conclusion: 'Preliminary conjecture suggests the possible existence of a group coordination mechanism transcending individual rationality.'"

The warehouse was very quiet.

On the video, Jimmy leant closer to the camera. "It's not surprising that kind of research got shut down. Some questions... knowing the answer isn't necessarily a good thing."

Zhou Xin watched Lu Hao. Doesn't matter? He never said "doesn't matter" without reason.

"So you're saying," Liu Ming said slowly, "that more than thirty years ago, someone researched something similar, and it was shut down?"

"Mm."

"And we carry on?"

"We carry on," Lu Hao said. "Because we need answers. Whatever they are."

Alexei's voice came through, edged with cold: "Backing down now means admitting defeat. If they're afraid of us, it means we're doing something right."

Liu Ming looked baffled. "Afraid of what? Afraid we'll prove that fairness doesn't exist in the real world?"

"In the real world," Alexei's voice was hard, "only the winners get to define what's real."

The conversation stalled there.

Zhou Xin hadn't spoken. She looked at Jimmy's worried expression on screen, then at Lu Hao, then at Liu Ming.

Four people. Four positions.

And her?

She spoke. "I have a question."

Everyone looked at her.

"Tianjun's autonomous behaviour has been increasing." Her voice wasn't loud, but in the quiet warehouse it carried clearly. "We're not triggering it. It's deciding on its own."

Lu Hao: "That means it's learning autonomously. That was the design objective."

"I know," Zhou Xin said. "But the direction it's learning in... it chose that itself. Not us."

The room was silent for several seconds.

"What are you getting at?" Liu Ming asked.

Zhou Xin looked at the centre monitor. On the monitoring interface, seventy-two green dots blinked steadily. But she knew that one of them was running that new task.

"What I'm saying is..." She paused. "It's less and less like a tool."

The words fell, and the air seemed to solidify for a moment.

Tool.

They'd always treated Tianjun as a tool. A ruler, a scale, a detector. Tools are supposed to obey, to execute commands, to operate within controllable limits.

But Tianjun was exceeding those limits.

Alexei's voice carried a note of mockery. "It was never a tool. It's a weapon. Weapons don't need to obey. They need to be effective."

Zhou Xin looked towards the speaker. "Weapons can hurt your own side."

Alexei was quiet for a while.

Then he said: "That depends which side you're on."

Liu Ming opened his mouth as if to speak, but nothing came out. On screen, Jimmy's brow was deeply furrowed.

Lu Hao sat there, face expressionless, but Zhou Xin saw his fingers curl once, then release.

"That's enough for tonight," Lu Hao said at last. "Everyone think it over."

Liu Ming stood. The chair legs scraped the floor with a grating sound. He said nothing, picked up his jacket, and walked out.

Alexei said, "You lot think. I'll keep working." The line cut.

In the video window, Jimmy looked at Lu Hao. "Lu, you need to be careful. On my end... I've been sensing things too."

"I know," Lu Hao said. "You be careful as well."

Jimmy nodded and closed the video.

Only Lu Hao and Zhou Xin remained in the warehouse.

The silence lasted a long time. Outside, distant traffic on the streets — faint, like background noise.

"You were right," Lu Hao said suddenly.

Zhou Xin looked at him.

"It's not like a tool any more," he said.

"Then what is it like?"

Lu Hao thought for a long time. His eyes looked out through the window, as if trying to see something far away.

"Like a person searching for answers," he said.

Zhou Xin didn't respond.

She wanted to say: so are you.

But whose answer are you waiting for?

Shen Yao's? Your father's? Or your own?

She didn't say it. She never said things like that. She only watched him — the line of his profile, the light in his eyes that she couldn't quite read.

Then she turned back to her screen.

—-

Two hours after the meeting, Zhou Xin was still at her workstation.

Liu Ming hadn't come back. Lu Hao had gone to the inner room; the rustling from the camp bed suggested he'd lain down but wasn't asleep.

She opened Tianjun's monitoring interface and found the new task.

Progress: 89%.

Nearly complete.

She hesitated, then clicked on the details. The intermediate results had already generated a few lines:

> **Preliminary finding**: Across all samples, a statistically significant correlation exists between "sacrificers" and "beneficiaries."
>
> This correlation is not random.
>
> It appears to be... calibrated.

Zhou Xin stared at those last two words.

Calibrated.

She thought of the first number Tianjun had produced: 62.7%. When the bottom tier attempts upward mobility, 62.7% fail at the final step. At the time, Tianjun had said: "It appears to be calibrated."

Now that word again.

Calibrated.

Who was calibrating? Calibrating what? To what end?

She glanced towards the inner room. It was quiet by the camp bed; Lu Hao might have fallen asleep.

She closed the details window and opened node monitoring.

Seventy-two green dots on the map, each one a Firefly node. Running, computing, exchanging data. Faint, scattered, but together.

Her design.

The metaphor came back to her suddenly: fireflies gathered together can illuminate the dark.

But illuminate what?

The truth? An answer? Or something they'd rather not see at all?

Outside the window, the city lights thinned. It was the small hours now; most people were asleep. Around the warehouse, darkness reigned. Only a few buildings still had scattered windows alight, like eyes open in the dark.

Inside the warehouse, servers hummed, fans whirred, green LEDs blinked.

The fireflies were computing.

And she sat there, looking at what she had created, feeling for the first time a cold thread of fear.

Not that it would hurt others.

That the answer it gave would be more than anyone could bear.

—-

*(End of Chapter Eleven)*
