# Chapter Nine: Shen Yao

Spring came early in 2028.

The kapok trees lining the streets of Nanhai had already blossomed. Red petals lay on the concrete, darkened by the rain. The warehouse was the same warehouse, but inside it had changed.

A row of server cabinets now stood against the wall, black, the fans louder than before. Zhou Xin said this was the third batch of Firefly nodes. Seventy-two in total now, spread across seven cities. Power consumption per node held steady below nine watts. Processing capacity was 3.2 times what it had been.

Liu Ming spent more time out and about. After Tianjun published its fifth causal chain report — this one concerning falsified environmental impact assessments at a chemical industrial park — two foundations made contact of their own accord, offering to fund "independent research." Liu Ming was in discussions, but cautious. He said the networks behind these organisations were complicated. The money wasn't clean.

Jimmy was still in England, though he'd moved six months ago, from Longton to a smaller town. He said it was quieter there, the internet was better — suited to remote work. But Lu Hao knew the real reason for the move: Jimmy thought someone was looking into him. Last September, an unfamiliar car had appeared outside his block of flats. It stayed for three days. Someone was always inside.

Messages from Alexei grew rarer. Sometimes he went a whole week without appearing, and when he did, he spoke only of technical matters. Last time, he'd said that seventeen of Tianjun's connected data sources had been flagged by him as "high risk" — potentially subject to counter-tracing. He'd redeployed the relay nodes using a new obfuscation algorithm.

Everything was changing, yet nothing seemed to have changed.

The warehouse still had the same folding tables, still piled with takeaway boxes. The air still smelt of electronic components and old paper. Lu Hao sat at the computer, watching Tianjun's real-time status panel. Seventy-two green dots scattered across the map like a sparse star cluster.

Communication traffic between nodes was steady. But Lu Hao knew that beneath the calm, something was stirring.

Tianjun's autonomous behaviour had grown more frequent. Last summer, for the first time, it had initiated an analysis without any trigger command — tracing the falsification of clinical trial data by a multinational pharmaceutical company. After the report was published, the company's share price dropped thirty per cent. The CEO resigned.

Zhou Xin checked the logs and confirmed there had been no external command. The system had determined on its own that "this matter concerns major public health interests," then launched the analysis.

Lu Hao had asked her at the time: "Can we still control it?"

Zhou Xin thought for a moment. "Technically, yes. There's an emergency stop protocol. But... the logical basis for its judgement is the ethical guidelines you wrote."

"What if the guidelines I wrote are flawed?"

Zhou Xin didn't answer. She pulled up the relevant code. The cursor rested on the comment line:

```
// When the system detects that information asymmetry may endanger life,
// health, or major public interest, it has the authority to independently
// disclose such information after anonymisation.
```

Lu Hao had written that. Two years ago.

At the time, he'd thought it was justice. Now he wasn't so sure.

Should justice have a price? Should an AI be the one to decide? If the AI's judgement differs from a human's, whose prevails?

He had no answer.

Tianjun offered him none either. It simply continued to run, continued to analyse, continued to publish. Like a wound clock that couldn't be stopped.

—-

On the last Thursday in March, Liu Ming said there was a conference to attend.

"What conference?"

"AI and Data Security Summit. At the convention centre." Liu Ming sent him the digital invitation. "The organisers are looking for technical consultants. I pulled some strings to get a spot. Go and have a look — you don't have to commit to anything. Just get a sense of the landscape."

Lu Hao's first instinct was to refuse. He didn't like crowds.

But Liu Ming said: "Hao-zi, you can't stay in the warehouse forever. Tianjun's already got a bit of a reputation out there — the anonymous kind. Some people may have noticed us. We need to know which way the wind's blowing."

Lu Hao thought it over and agreed.

The conference was on the second floor of the convention centre. The hall was large, the ceiling high. Light from the chandeliers struck the polished floor and bounced back, stinging the eyes. Plenty of attendees — in suits or smart casual, lanyards round their necks, holding coffee cups or brochures. The air carried perfume, coffee, and the chemical tang of new carpet.

Lu Hao found a seat in the corner. Someone on stage was delivering a speech about "the latest advances in AI ethics frameworks." The slides used a blue template, filled with charts and bullet points. The speaker's voice came through the sound system slightly hollow, as though filtered through a pane of glass.

He listened for ten minutes. None of it registered. His mind was still on Tianjun's status panel, those flickering numbers, those self-triggered analysis tasks.

"...Therefore, we must establish cross-industry collaborative regulatory mechanisms to ensure that AI development serves the collective interests of humanity..."

The man on stage said.

Lu Hao thought: what are the collective interests of humanity? Who defines them? How? The pharmaceutical company Tianjun had analysed — they'd falsified data to get a new drug to market faster, to make more money. That served the shareholders' interests. But what about the patients taking the drug?

There are no collective interests. Only different interests, in conflict with one another.

He stood up, wanting to step into the corridor for some air.

He'd barely left the hall when he heard a voice.

"Mr Lu?"

He turned. A woman stood behind him. Short hair. Grey blazer and black trousers. A tablet in her hand. About thirty, he guessed. Bright eyes, calm gaze.

"Have we met?" Lu Hao asked.

"No." She smiled faintly. "But I know it's you. Lu Hao."

Lu Hao looked at her, waiting for an explanation.

"The conference handbook lists 'Lu Hao' followed by 'independent research,' with no institutional affiliation. That's uncommon." Shen Yao spoke as though stating a simple fact. "I happened to have read your 2019 paper in the *Journal of Computer Research and Development*, on the privacy trade-offs of federated learning. First author Lu Hao, correspondence address at Clifton University. A person who came back from Clifton, does independent research, and goes by that name — it narrows the field considerably."

"And you are?"

"Shen Yao. Independent security consultant." She handed him a business card — good quality paper, clean design. Just a name and an email address. "Mainly data security and systemic risk assessment."

Lu Hao took the card. The email was a pinyin-and-numbers combination.

"So you came looking for me specifically?" he asked, a natural wariness in his tone.

"Not entirely. I'm here for the conference too." Shen Yao tucked away her tablet, her gaze lingering on him for a moment. "But I did want to talk to you. Because you look... different from the others on the list."

"Different how?"

"You stand in a crowd," Shen Yao said, her voice even, "but it's like standing alone in your own room. With a pane of glass between you and everyone else."

Lu Hao's fingers curled, unconsciously.

Her gaze followed the movement, settling on his hand hanging by his side. "And when you stop listening, your fingers tap out a rhythm. Not fast, but very steady. As if you're keeping time to a clock inside your head that no one else can hear."

Something shifted inside Lu Hao. Very few people noticed that detail. He hadn't even been aware of it himself. And she'd completed the switch — from verifying his professional background to observing his personal state — within seconds of meeting him. Cool as reading a line of code.

"You read people well," he said.

"Comes with the job. Observing systems includes observing the people who design them." Shen Yao picked up the thread naturally. "Like you wrote in that paper of yours — real privacy protection comes down to whether the designer has the courage to 'switch it off.'"

She looked up. No judgement in her eyes. Only inquiry.

"But in the real world, that switch always seems to be welded shut. For one reason or another."

She said it lightly, as though stating a law of physics.

"Perhaps," he said.

Shen Yao nodded slightly, a brief smile:

"So are you waiting for that switch to be flipped on its own, or for someone to come and prise it open?"

Lu Hao looked at her.

"I'm waiting for an answer," he said. He regretted it the moment the words left his mouth. Too direct, for a stranger.

But Shen Yao gave a small nod, as if this were perfectly normal. "And after the answer comes?" she asked.

"I don't know."

"What if the answer isn't what you want?"

Lu Hao was silent for a few seconds.

"I'd still need to know."

The corner of Shen Yao's mouth moved, very slightly. "My father used to say the same thing." Her voice dropped a little. "He was a journalist. He said: the truth is the truth, whether you like it or not."

"What happened to him?"

"He fell ill." Shen Yao said. "Cancer. After the diagnosis, he actually worked harder than before. He said that knowing the truth — even if you can't change anything — at least you die with your eyes open."

She paused. Her gaze drifted briefly to the far end of the corridor and back.

"Sometimes I wonder which takes more courage — dying with your eyes open, or living with them shut."

Footsteps passed through the corridor, voices fading in and out. Inside the hall, the speech droned on.

Lu Hao watched Shen Yao. Her expression hadn't changed after she'd spoken, but her fingers scraped once, very quickly, across the cold metal edge of the tablet.

"What type of security assessments do you do?" He changed the subject.

"Systemic ones." Shen Yao refocused. "Not just technical vulnerabilities — process vulnerabilities, human vulnerabilities, institutional vulnerabilities. A system can look perfect on paper, but in reality, someone always finds a way around it. Not to destroy — just to survive."

"For example?"

"Take a medical database. In theory, only authorised doctors can access it. In practice, the doctor's login credentials might be stuck on a Post-it next to the nurses' station computer. Or a pharmaceutical rep takes the department head out to dinner, and the head gets an intern to look up some data." Shen Yao said. "None of these are technical problems. They're human problems. But system designers tend to think only in terms of technology."

Lu Hao thought of the healthcare resource report Tianjun had analysed. Thirty-seven thousand people. The decision-makers probably thought their system was perfect too — saving budget, optimising resources, improving efficiency.

The results had been rather less encouraging.

"Do you think a perfect system can be designed?" he asked.

"No." Shen Yao answered without hesitation. "Because systems are designed by people, and people are imperfect. And a perfect system usually means a system with no resilience — the slightest disturbance and the whole thing collapses. A good system isn't perfect. It's fault-tolerant."

"Fault-tolerant to what degree?"

"To the degree that it can withstand the worst of human nature." Shen Yao looked at him. "Has the system you've designed accounted for the worst case?"

Lu Hao didn't answer immediately. He'd thought about Tianjun being misused, about data breaches, about being tracked. But what was the worst case? That Tianjun decides to do something on its own — something he can neither understand nor prevent?

"Some of it," he said.

"Do you have a contingency plan?"

"There's an emergency stop protocol."

"Who has the authority to execute it?"

"Me. Well."

Shen Yao nodded and didn't press further. She glanced at her watch.

"I should go back in. The next session is on supply chain attacks." She tucked the tablet under her arm. "A pleasure to meet you, Mr Lu."

"Just call me Lu Hao."

"Lu Hao." She repeated the name. "If you ever need a security assessment — not now, but if one day you do — you can reach me."

She handed him another card. This one was handwritten. Just a mobile number.

"That's my personal number. I don't always reply to the work email."

Lu Hao took the card. Ordinary paper. Neat handwriting, but with force behind it.

"Thank you."

Shen Yao nodded, turned, and walked towards the hall. Her stride was steady, her back straight.

Lu Hao stood where he was, watching her figure disappear behind the door.

He looked down at the two cards in his hand. One printed, one handwritten. One professional, one personal.

She'd been right. He was waiting. Waiting for Tianjun to produce its next answer, waiting for his father to ask "Have you found it?", waiting to work out what any of this actually meant.

Lu Hao put the cards in his pocket and walked back into the hall. The speech was still going, but he didn't sit down again. He stood at the back row, watching the speaker on stage, his mind elsewhere.

Shen Yao sat towards the front, in the middle section, head bowed over her tablet, taking notes. Her profile was clear in the overhead light — expression focused, entirely absorbed in the talk.

—-

After the conference, Lu Hao went straight back to the warehouse.

Zhou Xin was debugging a newly arrived node board. She didn't look up at the sound of the door.

"How was the conference?"

"Nothing special." Lu Hao tossed his jacket onto a chair. "AI, information security, networking. The usual."

"Meet anyone useful?"

"One person." Lu Hao thought for a moment. "Security consultant. Named Shen Yao."

Zhou Xin stopped what she was doing and turned her head. "What did she say?"

"We talked a bit about technology. She asked some questions about system design." Lu Hao thought of their conversation about waiting, answers, her father.

Zhou Xin studied the hesitation on his face. "Did she make you uncomfortable?"

"No." Lu Hao paused. "She's... very professional. But... it was sudden."

"Sudden how?"

"She knew who I was. She'd looked up my papers, knew where I'd studied... gave me her contact details. Said 'if one day' I need a security assessment, I could find her."

Zhou Xin was quiet for a while, her fingers rubbing the edge of the board's connector.

"She's watching you."

"I know."

Zhou Xin looked at the card in her hand. "Tianjun's too visible now. Be careful."

Lu Hao nodded. He walked to the computer and opened Tianjun's monitoring interface. All seventy-two nodes showed green. Communication traffic normal. System load normal. Operation logs normal.

But he had an odd feeling — especially about the things Shen Yao had said.

"...If you ever need a security assessment — not now, but if one day you do — you can reach me."

"If one day."

Which day?

He didn't know.

Outside the window, night was falling on Nanhai. In the distance, office buildings lit up one by one, merging into a blurred sea of light.

Inside the warehouse, servers hummed, fans whirred, green LEDs blinked.

Tianjun was computing.

And someone was watching.

Perhaps more than one.

Lu Hao minimised the monitoring interface and opened the code editor. The cursor blinked in the empty space.

He typed a comment:

```
// Thought: where is the boundary of a system's fault tolerance?
// If the worst case happens, are we ready?
```

Then he stopped.

He didn't know the answer.

All he could do was keep writing code, keep watching data, keep waiting.

Waiting for the next answer.

The night deepened.

The warehouse light in the darkness was like an island in the sea.

And the sea was vast.

—-

*(End of Chapter Nine)*
