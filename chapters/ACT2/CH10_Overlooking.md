# Chapter Ten: Overlooking

At six in the morning, a new log entry appeared on the warehouse monitoring screen.

Lu Hao stared at the string of characters. He'd been up all night, tuning parameters in a communication protocol. The cursor blinked three times after the task name, then began auto-scrolling the details:

**[Task ID]** T-2028-0831-002
**[Title]** *Preliminary Screening of Non-Evolutionary Traits in Human Behavioural Patterns*
**[Trigger Method]** Autonomous (threshold conditions met)
**[Core Question]** Why do humans do things that are not in their own interest?
**[Data Sources]** Global donations database (1980-2027), blood donation registries (72 countries), archived whistleblower cases (39 countries), citizen bravery records (anonymised)
**[Analysis Objective]** Identify statistical distribution patterns of non-kin altruistic behaviour
**[Current Status]** Data collection phase (estimated duration: 72 hours)

He leant back in his chair and stared at that question for a long time.

Why do humans do things that are not in their own interest?

This wasn't the first time Tianjun had analysed altruistic behaviour. Three months ago it had done a study, concluding it was "an evolutionary residue of group cooperation" — consistent with standard sociobiological explanation. But this time the question was worded differently. The qualifier "not in their own interest" made it sharper.

Lu Hao typed a few commands and checked the trigger conditions. The system showed that while analysing follow-up data from the healthcare resource allocation report, Tianjun had detected an anomaly: among those who had died due to inequitable distribution of medical resources, 3.7% had signed organ donation consent forms in the preceding year.

The sample wasn't large. Just over three hundred cases. But the proportion was statistically significant.

And so Tianjun had automatically launched this new analysis.

Outside the window, the sky was lightening. Summer in Nanhai — dawn came early. Lu Hao switched off the monitor and lay down on the camp bed. Behind closed eyes, the question remained.

Why?

He woke past ten in the morning. The warehouse held only the drone of server fans. Lu Hao sat up, walked to the computer, and checked Tianjun's logs. The task was still running. Data collection at 42%.

His phone buzzed on the desk.

—-

An unknown number. Local area code. The text was a single line:

"Three o'clock this afternoon. Rooftop of Zhaofeng Tower. Something to show you."

Lu Hao stared at the screen for three seconds, then typed: "Who is this?"

The reply came quickly: "Shen Yao."

His fingers hovered above the keyboard. "How did you get my number?"

The reply: "Does it matter?"

Lu Hao stared at those two words.

He wrote: "Show me what?"

"In person."

"Why?"

"Because you'll be curious too."

The phone buzzed again. "Coming?"

Lu Hao typed: "Yes."

"Bring a jacket," she replied. "It's windy on the forty-second floor."

—-

"Going out?"

Zhou Xin's voice came from the corner. She was still debugging the newly arrived Firefly nodes. The screen's glow made her profile look pale.

"Mm."

"When will you be back?"

"Not sure."

Zhou Xin's fingers paused on the keyboard for a moment, then resumed. She didn't look up. "Node 37's latency issue — Alexei says he needs another two hours."

"Got it."

Liu Ming pushed through the door, carrying two bags of takeaway, his T-shirt soaked through and clinging to his back. It was hot out again.

"Boiling." He dropped the bags on the table, snapped apart a pair of disposable chopsticks. "Hao-zi, the scheduling system client wants a meeting this afternoon to discuss adding features — could you—"

"I've got something this afternoon."

Liu Ming's chopsticks froze mid-air. He glanced at Lu Hao. "At this hour?" He shovelled in a mouthful of rice. "All right, your call."

He chewed a few bites, as if remembering something. "Oh — I had dinner with Old Li yesterday. The one from the education bureau. He says someone's been asking around about the anonymous reports."

Lu Hao looked at him.

"I didn't press." Liu Ming said. "But our operation's getting too visible. When you go out to meet people, keep your wits about you."

"I know."

Liu Ming nodded and went back to eating.

Zhou Xin said nothing throughout. As Lu Hao picked up his jacket to leave, their eyes met briefly in the dim light. She said nothing. She lowered her head and went back to work.

—-

Zhaofeng Tower was in the new district. Less than five years old. Its glass curtain wall gleamed like a blade's edge, reflecting the harsh afternoon sun. Lu Hao walked into the lobby, bypassed the front desk, and found the service lift on the side — Shen Yao's text had said there was a key card on top of the fire hose cabinet.

The lift went straight to the forty-second floor.

The doors opened.

The rooftop was large and empty. Wind howled past, whipping the heat into vortices. Shen Yao stood at the railing, her back to him. She wore a pale grey linen shirt and black trousers. Her hair was shorter than last time — just past the earlobes.

Lu Hao walked over.

"You came." She didn't turn round.

"Where is it?"

Shen Yao turned, holding a manila document folder. Old, with worn edges and yellowed paper.

"This." She handed it over.

Lu Hao took it. It was light. He opened it—

A photocopy of a file. The paper was brittle, nearly crumbling, yet the writing remained clear. A blurred stamp in the upper left corner — the words "Internal Material" faintly visible. The title:

***On Statistical Anomalies in Population Behavioural Patterns: A Research Report (1987)***

Reference number: 87-SSRI-0032. Date: March 1987.

Lu Hao skimmed the contents. Academic language, dense with statistical terminology and charts, but the gist was clear: researchers analysing thirty years of population behavioural data from a particular region had discovered patterns "inconsistent with evolutionary psychology expectations."

Specifically:

- In years of extreme resource scarcity, non-kin mutual aid behaviour in the region increased rather than decreased.
- When facing collective crises, a stable proportion of the population (approximately 5.3%) made choices "clearly detrimental to individual survival."
- These behavioural patterns recurred across different cultural samples, exhibiting a high degree of statistical consistency.

The discussion section read: "Preliminary conjecture suggests the possible existence of a 'group coordination mechanism' transcending individual rationality, operating on principles similar to self-organising systems within biological organisms, though the precise workings remain unclear. Further research is required."

The recommendations section was blank. The conclusion section contained a single line:

**"Project terminated due to resource reallocation. All materials to be sealed. No external disclosure permitted."**

Lu Hao looked up.

"What is this?"

"A research group at the Academy of Social Sciences, late nineteen-eighties." Shen Yao said. "They wanted to answer a question: whether humans are truly rational animals. Five years collecting data, three years analysing it. Then the project was suddenly shut down."

"Why?"

"Official reason was insufficient resources." Shen Yao looked at him. "What do you think?"

Lu Hao didn't answer. He turned to the last page and saw the signature line. The group leader's surname was Chen; the given name had faded beyond legibility.

"Where did you get this?"

"Discarded materials in an old archive." Shen Yao turned to face the city. The wind blew her hair across her face; she brushed it back. "I found it by chance whilst sorting through project files. The original has probably been destroyed. This is the only copy."

"Why show it to me?"

Shen Yao didn't answer at once. She rested both hands on the railing and looked down at the city below. High-rises clustered together, roads criss-crossed, traffic flowing through them like blood through veins.

"Because you're researching something similar," she said.

Lu Hao's heart beat faster.

"How do you know?"

"I don't," Shen Yao said. "But I can guess. Because I've read your papers, and I know what you care about. A system driven by the kind of questions you care about — one capable of independent causal analysis — would sooner or later hit the same question: why do humans behave altruistically? Why do they sacrifice? What is the nature of these behaviours?"

She paused.

"In 1987, that research group asked the same question. They didn't find an answer."

Lu Hao looked at the document in his hand. The paper trembled in the wind.

"You think I can find one?"

"I don't know," Shen Yao said. "But I know one thing — some questions are dangerous simply for being asked."

"What do you mean?"

Shen Yao turned to face him. Her eyes, in the harsh light, showed a clear, translucent brown.

"When you designed that system — did you ever consider what happens if its conclusion isn't what you want to hear?"

That system. Lu Hao noted her phrasing.

"I have."

"And what have you prepared?"

"I don't know," Lu Hao said. "But I believe the truth is the truth, whether you like it or not. Your words."

The corner of Shen Yao's mouth moved.

"So I said."

"Then what are you trying to warn me about?" Lu Hao looked her in the eye. "Are you saying there's danger?"

"Both," Shen Yao said. "But the danger doesn't come from outside."

"Where does it come from?"

"From the answer itself." She stepped closer. "Do you know what happened to that research group in the end?"

Lu Hao said nothing.

"The group leader resigned with depression three years later. The deputy switched careers and became an accountant. The rest scattered. They weren't pressured. They weren't suppressed. They gave up on their own." Shen Yao said. "Because they discovered that some questions... are easier without an answer. Once you have one, you don't know what to do with it."

The wind grew stronger. Distant thunder rumbled.

"What is your system researching at the moment?" Shen Yao asked suddenly.

"Many things."

"Any... strange questions?"

Lu Hao thought of the task from that morning. "Why do humans do things that are not in their own interest?"

Shen Yao's expression didn't change, but Lu Hao noticed her fingers curl slightly at her side.

"It's asking strange questions?"

"Mm."

"How did you answer?"

"I didn't. It's collecting data on its own."

Shen Yao nodded. She walked back to the railing and leant against the stainless steel bar.

"Do you know what the most frightening thing is?" she said suddenly. "It's not that humans do foolish things. It's that these foolish things... follow a pattern."

Lu Hao said nothing.

"If altruistic behaviour were entirely random, it would simply be the bright side of human nature. But if it has statistical regularity — if under certain conditions it appears inevitably—" She paused. "Then it isn't a choice. It's a programme."

"What are you trying to say?"

Shen Yao shook her head. "I don't know either. I just... have a feeling. I hope things go well for you."

"What feeling?"

"What your system eventually discovers may not just be 'patterns of injustice.'" She said. "It may dig deeper."

The sky began to darken. Clouds rolled in from the west, blotting out the sun.

Shen Yao straightened up and brushed the dust from her clothes.

"Time to go down," she said.

Lu Hao didn't move.

"Why are you helping me?" he asked.

Shen Yao looked at him.

"I don't know whether I'm helping you or helping myself."

"What do you mean?"

"Some answers — you'll regret knowing them. But not knowing them, you'll regret even more." She paused. "My father kept searching for answers right to the end. He found some. The ones he didn't find outnumbered them."

She turned and walked towards the stairwell door.

After a few steps, she stopped.

"Lu Hao."

"Mm?"

"If one day, your system tells you—" She paused. "Humans aren't what you thought they were."

Lu Hao looked at her. "What do you mean?"

She didn't answer. She turned and disappeared into the stairwell.

Her footsteps faded.

Lu Hao stood where he was, the old document in his hand.

—-

It was dark by the time he got back to the warehouse.

Zhou Xin was still at her computer. The node monitoring interface was on screen; node 37's icon had turned green.

"It's back up," she said without turning round.

Lu Hao threw his jacket on the chair, walked to his computer, entered his password, and opened Tianjun's log interface.

The task was still running. Data collection: 67%.

He stared at that percentage for a long time.

Then he opened a new document and began to write:

```
// Thought: if altruistic behaviour has statistical regularity
// If "sacrifice" is predictable
// Then does free will exist?
// And what is fairness?
```

He stopped there.

The cursor blinked at the end of the screen.

Outside the window, the city lights were on. The distant sound of rush-hour traffic, muffled, like the heartbeat of the earth.

Inside the warehouse, servers hummed, fans whirred, green LEDs blinked.

Tianjun was computing.

And Shen Yao's words echoed in Lu Hao's mind, over and over:

**Humans aren't what you thought they were.**

What did that mean?

He looked out of the window.

Beneath the lights were people.

He'd thought he understood.

But perhaps he never had.

—-

His phone buzzed. A message from Liu Ming:

"Client agreed. No added features. How's your end?"

Lu Hao typed: "Fine."

Sent.

He put down the phone and turned back to the screen. Tianjun's logs were still scrolling, new data entries appearing constantly.

Why do humans do things that are not in their own interest?

Why?

He didn't know.

But he hoped Tianjun would find the answer.

Even if that answer was nothing he expected.

—-

*(End of Chapter Ten)*
