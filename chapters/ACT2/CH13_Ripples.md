# Chapter Thirteen: Ripples

Lu Hao stared at the code on his screen, fingers hovering above the keyboard.

Three weeks had passed.

That report on the "satisfaction threshold" was like a stone tossed into still water — the ripples spreading further and longer than he had expected. The warehouse window was half open. The evening breeze drifted in, carrying the sticky coolness particular to Nanhai City in October. Outside lay the software park's main road, rush hour, pedestrians and traffic weaving together. Lu Hao watched the figures hurrying past — a man clutching a briefcase, a woman scrolling her phone as she walked, a student with a rucksack waiting for the bus.

He tried to see them through Tianjun's eyes.

That man in the suit had just closed a deal; this month's targets met. According to the data, his sense of satisfaction would recede within a few weeks. Then he would fixate on higher targets, anxious about next month's figures.

That girl had probably just passed an interview, landed the offer she wanted. The joy would last a while, then give way to worries about the probation period, colleague dynamics, promotion prospects.

Never satisfied.

Always striving.

Was this by design?

Lu Hao pulled his gaze back and looked at his own hands. His fingers cast slender shadows across the keyboard. He recalled that line: "Your behaviour — including the very act of raising this question — falls within the predictive range of statistical patterns."

If even "questioning" itself was predictable, what was the point of questioning?

His phone buzzed once on the desk.

He picked it up. The screen lit with a text from an unknown number. No greeting, just the message:

*Tonight at seven. Binhai New District, Creative Park, Building C3, Room 307. Not work-related. Shen Yao.*

Lu Hao stared at the words. "Not work-related." Those four words circled through his mind. He thought of their first encounter months ago in the summit corridor, the wind on the rooftop and that 1987 report, her saying "Humans aren't what you think they are."

Now it was "Not work-related."

He glanced at the time: ten past six.

He replied: *OK.*

---

The Creative Park sat on the edge of the new district, converted from old factory buildings. Red brick walls smothered in ivy. At night, light spilled through the large windows onto the pitted concrete ground. Building C3 stood deep within the park — three storeys, bare concrete steps, rust-spotted handrails.

Lu Hao climbed to the third floor and found Room 307. The door was heavy, natural wood. No nameplate — just a simple black handle.

He raised his hand. Knocked.

Footsteps from inside, then the door opened.

Shen Yao stood behind it. She wore a dark grey linen shirt, sleeves rolled to the elbows, and loose black trousers. Her hair was still that short — just past the earlobes — catching a soft, deep brown sheen under the corridor's dim yellow light.

"Come in." She stepped aside.

Lu Hao walked in.

The space was large, the ceiling high, exposed concrete beams spanning overhead. The floor was polished concrete, reflecting the overhead light. Against one wall stood a massive timber desk with only a laptop, a tablet, and a white ceramic mug on it. Beside the desk, a plain black chair. A metal bookshelf leant against the opposite wall, books and folders neatly arranged but few in number. In the corner, a single armchair in off-white, a floor lamp standing beside it.

No trace of a life lived here.

"Sit." Shen Yao gestured towards the armchair. "What would you like? Water, tea, or coffee?"

"Water's fine."

She walked to a small bar in the corner. Lu Hao sat down. The armchair was soft; he sank into it slightly.

Shen Yao returned with a glass of water and set it on the small side table, then sat in the chair by the desk, facing him. The light fell from behind her, tracing a faint halo around her silhouette.

"This place is... very spare," Lu Hao said.

"Work requires it." Shen Yao's voice was flat. "Fewer things, fewer distractions."

Lu Hao picked up the glass. The water was warm, just right. He took a sip and set it down.

"You said this wasn't work-related."

"Mm." Shen Yao looked at him. "I just wanted to talk."

"About what?"

Shen Yao didn't answer immediately. Her finger tapped once, very lightly, against the desktop — barely audible. Then she said: "What have you been thinking about lately?"

Lu Hao blinked.

"What do you mean?"

"You look..." She paused, as though searching for the right word. "Like when you look at things, you're looking for what's behind them."

Lu Hao's fingers twitched involuntarily. He thought of the past three weeks — writing code during the day, debugging nodes, lying in bed at night staring at the ceiling, his mind full of those reports. Satisfaction thresholds. Designed. Never satisfied.

He had thought he was hiding it well.

"What do you mean?" he asked.

Shen Yao's gaze settled on his face and did not move. "If an ordinary person looked at this room, they'd see furniture, décor, the view from the window. Your eyes sweep across it all, but they're searching for something else — structure, wiring, possible exits. Not deliberately. Out of habit."

She paused. Her voice was level. "That kind of habit only develops in people who've been on guard for a long time."

Lu Hao was silent for several seconds. The precision of her observation unsettled him.

"How do you know?" he asked at last.

Shen Yao tilted her head slightly, as though considering how to answer. "I've done a lot of security assessments," she said. "Watched many people react to different environments. Some people, even sitting in the safest room, keep their shoulders tense. You're not tense — you're... detached. As if part of your attention is always somewhere else."

Lu Hao was silent for several seconds.

"I've made a discovery recently." The words were out before he realised he'd said too much, but there was no taking them back.

Shen Yao's expression didn't change, but her body seemed to lean forward slightly.

"About what?"

Lu Hao hesitated.

"About why humans are never satisfied." He spoke. "I found a mathematical pattern. No matter what someone gains, the sense of satisfaction lasts only a fixed period on average before falling back to baseline. The standard deviation is tiny — too small to be random."

Shen Yao listened in silence. Her eyes stayed on him, unmoving.

"I think it looks designed," Lu Hao continued, his voice lower than usual. "To ensure humans keep striving, keep advancing, keep... never stopping."

The room fell silent.

Outside, night had fully descended. From somewhere in the Creative Park came the faint sound of music — probably a studio still working late. The sound was distant, muffled, as though filtered through thick glass.

Shen Yao's finger traced an invisible pattern on the desktop.

"So now, when you look at anyone," she said slowly, "you're wondering — is their striving free will, or programming?"

Lu Hao did not deny it.

"What about you?" he asked in return. "What do you think?"

Shen Yao stood and walked to the window. It was enormous, spanning nearly the entire wall. Outside: the park at night — lights in other factory buildings, traffic on distant streets, the city's glow further still. She stood with her back to him, looking out.

"My father used to say something." Her voice was calm. "He said, truth is truth, whether you like it or not."

Lu Hao remembered. She had said it before. In the summit corridor.

"But later he added something." Shen Yao turned, leaning against the windowsill. "He said, but once you know the truth, you have to decide how to live."

Light from outside fell behind her, hiding her face in shadow. Lu Hao couldn't make out her expression — only a silhouette.

"If you believe your discovery," she said, "if you believe that human striving is designed — what are you going to do next?"

Lu Hao didn't answer immediately.

He had been thinking about this for days. If everything was designed, if free will was just an illusion, then what was the point? Keep writing code? Keep analysing? Keep chasing an answer he might never find?

But whenever his thoughts reached this point, he would think of his father.

His father's silhouette on the balcony. His father saying: "If you can find the answer, tell me."

His father didn't know about these discoveries. His father still believed in hard work, in perseverance, believed that "almost" was just bad luck. If he told his father that his "almost" might be a parameter set by the system — what would his father think?

"I don't know," Lu Hao said at last. "But I can't stop."

Shen Yao nodded, as though the answer was what she had expected.

"So you're waiting," she said.

"Waiting for what?"

"For the next answer. For more discoveries. For yourself to work it out." Shen Yao walked back to the desk but didn't sit — she leant against its edge. "You're waiting for a reason to keep going."

Lu Hao looked at her. Her eyes in the lamplight were light brown, very clear.

"And you?" he asked. "What are you waiting for?"

The corner of Shen Yao's mouth moved — the ghost of a smile that never quite formed.

"I'm waiting for..." She paused. "For certain things to become clear."

"What things?"

Shen Yao didn't answer directly. She turned and walked to the bookshelf, taking down a tin box from the top shelf. It was small, dark blue, the corners worn, paint flaking to reveal the dull metal beneath. She carried it back and sat on the low stool opposite Lu Hao.

The box rested on the floor between them.

"This was my father's," Shen Yao said. Her fingers traced the edge of the lid, very slowly, as though touching something fragile.

Lu Hao looked at the box. Old. Used for many years.

Shen Yao opened the lid.

There wasn't much inside. A few old photographs, edges curled and yellowed. A faded school badge. Several folded sheets of letter paper. And an old fountain pen, its black lacquer mottled.

She picked up the photograph on top and handed it to Lu Hao.

Three people. A middle-aged couple, a girl standing between them. The girl was about seven or eight, hair in two plaits, wearing a floral dress, beaming. The background was a park — a lake, trees, bright sunlight.

"These are my parents," Shen Yao said, her voice a little softer than before. "This is me."

"Your father..." Lu Hao recalled what she had said. "Was a journalist?"

Shen Yao's finger paused at the edge of the photograph.

"Yes." As she said it, she seemed to be remembering something.

The man in the photograph wore glasses and a white shirt, his smile gentle. He looked like a secondary school teacher or a civil servant.

Lu Hao didn't press further.

Shen Yao placed the photograph back in the box and closed the lid. Her fingers lingered on top for a few seconds, then withdrew.

"When he passed," she said, very quietly, "his eyes were open."

Lu Hao remembered those words. In the summit corridor, she had said them. At the time she had said: "Knowing the truth — even if you can't change anything — at least when you die, your eyes are open."

Now, hearing it again, he felt something different.

"What did he see?" Lu Hao asked.

Shen Yao looked up at him. Her eyes in the lamplight seemed very deep, like two pools of still water.

"He saw things he wasn't supposed to see," she said. "But he chose to look anyway."

The room fell quiet again. Outside, the music had stopped; the park seemed to have gone entirely silent. Only the faint sound of an occasional car on a distant street.

Lu Hao looked at Shen Yao. She sat on the low stool, shoulders slightly slumped. Her fingers rested on the tin lid, the tips white.

He realised, suddenly, that this was the first time she had shown him this side of herself — not the calm professionalism of a security consultant, not the sharp perception of an observer, but something vulnerable and helpless.

Though it lasted only an instant.

"Are you all right?" Lu Hao asked.

Shen Yao was silent for a while.

"I'm fine," she said slowly. "You remind me of him a little."

"How so?"

"Both waiting for an answer," Shen Yao said. "Even knowing it might hurt."

Lu Hao said nothing. He looked at the tin box on the floor, at Shen Yao's hand resting on the lid.

That line surfaced again: "Your behaviour — including the very act of raising this question — falls within the predictive range of statistical patterns."

What if even this moment's feelings — the curiosity, the resonance, the concern — were also designed?

If they were, then what?

"You know," Lu Hao began, his voice somewhat dry, "the analyses say that humans' satisfaction threshold is designed. The pursuit of fairness may be a mechanism to keep the system running. Even 'questioning' itself might fall within the predictive range of statistical patterns."

Shen Yao watched him, waiting for him to continue.

"If that's true," Lu Hao said, "then my sitting here, talking to you, thinking and feeling — all of it might just be part of the programme."

Shen Yao didn't respond immediately. Her finger tapped once on the lid, very lightly, like a raindrop on a leaf.

"So what?" she said.

Lu Hao blinked.

"If everything is designed," Shen Yao continued, her voice perfectly calm, "if even this conversation is the result of some programme running — so what? Would you stop talking because of it? Would you leave?"

Lu Hao looked at her.

"No," he said.

"Why not?"

"Because..." Lu Hao searched for the words. "Because even if it's designed, the feeling is real."

A true smile finally surfaced at the corner of Shen Yao's mouth. Faint. Genuine.

"That's enough," she said.

Two words, falling softly into the quiet room.

Lu Hao realised, suddenly, that this was the first time in weeks he had felt a kind of peace. Not an answer, not a release, but an acceptance — accepting that there might be no ultimate answer, accepting that everything might be designed, yet still choosing to go on.

Outside, the night had deepened. Music rose again from somewhere in the park, a gentle piano piece this time, barely audible.

Shen Yao stood, placed the tin box back on the top shelf. Her movements were gentle, as though handling something precious. Then she returned and sat down again.

"Have you been drawing lately?" she asked, abruptly.

Lu Hao stiffened.

"What?"

"Drawing." Shen Yao said. "Didn't you study art as a child?"

A faint chill crept up Lu Hao's spine. He had never told her he'd studied art. Never.

"How do you know that?" he asked, his voice dryer than he'd intended.

Shen Yao's expression didn't change, but something seemed to flicker in her eyes, quickly steadying.

"You mentioned it before," she said, her tone natural.

Lu Hao ransacked his memory. Had he? During that brief conversation in the summit corridor? He remembered mentioning that his father was in the military, saying a few things about himself, but what exactly, he couldn't recall. Perhaps he had said it. Perhaps not.

But Shen Yao's tone was so natural, so certain, that he couldn't challenge it.

"That was a long time ago," Lu Hao said. "I stopped."

"Why did you stop?"

"Lost interest." Lu Hao paused. "Or rather — I thought the judging criteria were flawed."

Shen Yao nodded, as if she understood.

"That painting of your father," she said. "What happened to it?"

Lu Hao's heart skipped a beat.

He was certain he had never told anyone about that painting. Apart from his parents, apart from the judges and teachers at the time, nobody knew he had painted his father. Even Jimmy didn't know the details.

But Shen Yao knew.

She didn't just know he'd studied art. She didn't just know he always came second. She knew he had painted his father.

How?

Lu Hao felt his throat tighten. He studied Shen Yao's face, trying to read something there. But she simply looked back at him, her gaze clear, without the slightest evasion.

Lu Hao stared at her. Every conversation across their few meetings — he remembered them all. He had not mentioned the painting. Absolutely not.

Yet Shen Yao's expression was so assured, so open, that he almost began to doubt his own memory.

In the end, he didn't press further.

"The painting's still there," Lu Hao said. "At my father's place."

"He kept it?"

"Yes. In the study, in a drawer." Lu Hao thought of the last time he'd been home, seeing the painting in his father's drawer. The paper had yellowed, the pencil lines somewhat faded, but well preserved.

"That's good," Shen Yao said. There was something in her voice that Lu Hao couldn't quite identify.

Then she stood.

"It's getting late," she said.

Lu Hao checked the time: twenty past nine. Over two hours had slipped away without his noticing.

He rose. The armchair gave a soft sound as he stood.

Shen Yao walked him to the door. It opened, and corridor light flooded in, brighter than the room. Lu Hao stepped out and turned.

Shen Yao stood inside the doorway, the light behind her hiding her face in shadow.

"Lu Hao." She stopped him.

He paused.

"There's something I want to remind you of," she said, her voice soft but clear. "Knowing too much isn't always a good thing."

Lu Hao looked at her. Her eyes glimmered faintly in the shadow.

"What do you mean?" He asked it anyway.

The corner of Shen Yao's mouth moved — a weary sort of smile.

"My work has shown me a great deal," she said. "Some people, once they learn things they shouldn't have learnt, can never go back to the life they had before."

"I don't understand."

"What I'm trying to say is..." Shen Yao said. "Those who pursue the truth may find, in the end, that the truth itself is a trap."

Lu Hao stood in the corridor, looking at her face. For an instant, he wanted to ask more. Wanted to know what exactly she knew, why she was telling him this.

But he didn't ask.

"I understand," he said.

Shen Yao nodded.

"Take care getting home."

The door closed softly. The sound of wood meeting wood echoed through the empty corridor.

Lu Hao stood there, staring at the shut door. After a few seconds, he turned and walked down the stairs.

The night wind was colder now. Lu Hao pulled his jacket tight and followed the streetlamps towards the underground station. Few people on the road; a car passed now and then.

The carriage was nearly empty. Lu Hao leant back in his seat, watching the advertisement light boxes flicker past outside the window.

He thought of Shen Yao's eyes in that last moment. As the door closed, there had been something in them — not wariness, not calculation, but a kind of weariness. Or perhaps resignation.

He thought of what she had said: "If everything is designed — so what?"

What if even what he felt for her, in this moment, was designed?

The train hurtled through the tunnel. Lu Hao watched his own reflection in the window, thinking of Shen Yao's fingers tracing the tin lid, thinking of her smile when she said "That's enough."

---

Back at the warehouse, Lu Hao switched on the light. The fluorescent tubes flickered, then lit the familiar space.

He walked to the computer and sat down. The screen came alive, showing the node monitoring interface. Seventy-two green dots. System running normally.

He opened a new document. The cursor blinked in the empty space.

A few seconds later, he closed it.

Some things didn't need to be written down.

Lu Hao stood and walked to the window, looking at the distant lights — soft, warm, as though the city were breathing.

*(End of Chapter Thirteen)*
