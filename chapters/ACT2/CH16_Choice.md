# Chapter Sixteen: Choice

His phone buzzed in his pocket.

Lu Hao was watching Zhou Xin debug a Firefly node. The waveform on screen held steady below the 9.8-watt dashed line, the occasional peak jumping but the overall trend flat. He didn't reach for the phone immediately — he waited until Zhou Xin had logged the final set of readings, then took it out.

Four characters on screen: *Tonight, eight.*

The same number. He replied with one word: *OK.*

"Liu Ming hasn't been in for three days," Zhou Xin said suddenly, eyes still on the screen.

Lu Hao slipped the phone back into his pocket. "I know."

"The heat dissipation design for Node 37 needs reworking. I can't finish it alone."

"I'll help you tomorrow."

Zhou Xin didn't respond. She went on adjusting parameters. Lu Hao picked up his jacket and pushed through the door.

---

Forty minutes later he stood outside Shen Yao's door and knocked twice.

The door opened. Shen Yao wore a loose, dark blue jumper. Her hair seemed freshly washed, still carrying a trace of damp.

Lu Hao stepped inside. The heating was on. He took off his jacket; Shen Yao took it and hung it on the rack by the door.

"Have a seat." She gestured towards the sofa.

Lu Hao sat. The room was arranged just as before — timber desk, metal bookshelf, off-white sofa, floor lamp in the corner. But something felt different today, something he couldn't quite place. Perhaps the scent in the air — tea mixed with something else, a woody fragrance, perhaps incense.

Shen Yao handed him a cup of tea.

"Pu'er," she said. "A few years old. Not too bitter."

Lu Hao took a sip. "It's good."

Shen Yao drank too, then rested the cup on her knee, both hands wrapped around it. Her fingertips were faintly flushed from the heat.

"I asked you here for two things," she said.

Lu Hao watched her, waiting.

"The first is work-related." Shen Yao stood and went to the bookshelf, pulling a manila folder from the middle shelf. She handed it to Lu Hao.

"That 1987 report I gave you last time — I've found its appendix."

Lu Hao opened the folder and drew out the slightly oxidised, yellowed pages.

The title read *On the Mathematical Modelling of "Non-Evolutionary Traits"*, dated June 1987.

He scanned the content quickly. The research group had attempted to use systems dynamics models to explain those human behaviours that "did not conform to evolutionary expectations" — why humans made choices clearly against their own interests, why certain statistical patterns were so stable. Their finding made Lu Hao pause:

"If humans are treated not as independent evolutionary individuals but as components of a larger system, model fit improves from 47% to 89%."

In the margin, someone had written in fountain pen, the handwriting scrawled but legible: "What is the objective function? What is the system trying to maximise?"

Further on, at the bottom of the last page, a line of small print — apparently added later:

"This line of inquiry is too dangerous. Recommend permanent archival. Project terminated."

Lu Hao closed the folder and looked up at Shen Yao. She had sat back down, cup in hand, watching him.

"They came so close to touching it," Shen Yao said, her voice very soft. "And then they stopped."

"Who made them stop?"

"I don't know." Shen Yao shook her head. "This appendix surfaced from a private collection. The originals were probably destroyed long ago. I only managed to obtain these few pages."

Lu Hao set the folder on the side table and leant back into the sofa.

"1987," he said. "Over thirty years ago, and people were already thinking about this."

"There were probably many." Shen Yao said. "But few left any record. Fewer still whose records survived."

The room was quiet for a moment.

"The second thing," Lu Hao said.

Shen Yao didn't answer immediately.

"The second..." she began, then stopped.

Outside, a car passed, tyres on wet road.

"What has Tianjun been working on lately?" she asked.

"Still running the feeling prediction experiment. Phase two."

"Last time you said behaviour prediction was at 94.7%, feeling prediction 31.2%. Any change?"

"About the same." Lu Hao raised his cup and drank. "Behaviour's actually gone up a touch. Feelings are still stuck."

"So it could predict you'd come tonight."

"Very likely."

"But it can't predict what you feel coming here."

"It can't."

Shen Yao nodded. Her gaze rested on Lu Hao's face, as though she were turning something over.

"Has Tianjun ever said," she asked, "why feelings can't be predicted?"

"It says feelings leave no data traces." Lu Hao put down the cup. "Behaviour leaves records — what key you pressed, what route you took, what you bought. But feelings are internal. No external signal. All it can do is guess."

"Guess." Shen Yao repeated the word. "A system that can predict 94% of human behaviour, and when it comes to feelings, all it can do is guess."

"What do you make of that?"

Shen Yao didn't answer directly. Her gaze drifted towards the window.

"I've been thinking about something recently," she said. "If humans really are designed — if your discoveries are real — then what exactly did the designer design?"

"Behaviour," Lu Hao said. "Based on the data so far, behaviour."

"Yes. Behaviour." Shen Yao turned back. "Striving, competition, perpetual dissatisfaction, the 62.7% failure rate — all of it sits at the behavioural level. Predictable. Quantifiable. Expressible in a formula."

"But not feelings."

"But not feelings." Shen Yao repeated. "Which makes me think of a possibility — that feelings are not part of the design."

Lu Hao watched her.

"You mean..."

"I mean that if there really is a designer, they designed human behavioural patterns — designed those statistical regularities precise to the first decimal place — but they didn't design feelings." Shen Yao said. "Feelings are... an accident. Something the system generated on its own during operation. Not in the original blueprint."

"A bug."

"Or a feature. Depends how you look at it."

The room fell quiet again. Lu Hao thought of the numbers in Tianjun's report. 94.7% and 31.2%. Over sixty percentage points between them, like a wide river separating behaviour and feeling on opposite banks.

"There's one more thing," Shen Yao said. Her voice had changed; something like concern moved in her eyes.

Lu Hao watched her.

"I've been hearing things," Shen Yao said. "About Tianjun."

"What things?"

"People are discussing it." Shen Yao returned to her chair and sat on its edge, leaning slightly forward. "Not publicly. A very small circle. Specialists."

"Specialists?"

"They're assessing risk," she said. "I don't know the specifics, but I know they're nervous."

"Why?"

"I'm not certain." Shen Yao's voice dropped further. "But from their tone... it doesn't sound like they're worried Tianjun will malfunction, or be misused. It sounds more like they're worried about what Tianjun might discover."

Lu Hao's fingers slowly curled against his knee.

Shen Yao looked at him. "They're nervous. Far more nervous than I expected."

"These people," he asked, "who are they?"

Shen Yao didn't answer. She simply looked at him, her expression complicated.

"Aren't you going to ask how I know all this?" she said.

Lu Hao looked at her. He did want to ask.

But he didn't.

Shen Yao pressed her lips together.

"The patterns Tianjun has discovered," she said, "62.7%, 11.3 weeks, 94.7% — they share something in common."

"What?"

"Extremely narrow tolerance margins." Shen Yao said. "Standard deviations of only a few percentage points. Data spanning decades and dozens of countries, with vanishingly small fluctuation ranges."

"I know," Lu Hao said. "Too neat."

"Not just neat." Shen Yao said. "Precise. Precise to the point of... looking calibrated."

She paused.

"If these patterns really are designed," she went on, "then whoever — or whatever — designed them knew exactly what they were doing. So precisely that no deviation was tolerated. So precisely that the tolerance margin was compressed to virtually zero."

"Do you think Tianjun will find them?" Lu Hao asked. "The designers?"

Shen Yao shook her head.

"I don't know," she said. "But I think that if designers do exist, they would not want to be found."

"And Tianjun is heading in that direction."

The room fell silent.

"Why are you telling me this?" he asked.

Shen Yao was quiet for a few seconds.

"Because I want you to know," she said, "that the path you're on may be more dangerous than you think."

"And you?" Lu Hao asked. "Are you on this path?"

Shen Yao didn't answer at once. She lowered her eyes to the tea in her cup.

"I'm watching the people on it," she said at last.

Lu Hao looked at her. He wanted to ask more.

He didn't speak. He simply breathed out softly, the faintest curve at the corner of his mouth.

Shen Yao's shoulders relaxed.

"It's getting late," she said.

Lu Hao stood.

Shen Yao brought his jacket over. As he put it on, she straightened the collar for him, her fingers lingering a second longer on his shoulder.

He opened the door, stepped out, and turned.

Shen Yao stood in the doorway, warm light behind her.

"Let me know when you're home," she said.

"I will."

*(End of Chapter Sixteen)*
