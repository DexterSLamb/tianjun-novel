# Chapter Twelve: Threshold

Half past six in the evening. When Lu Hao pushed open the warehouse door, he caught the familiar smell of metal and dust. The air conditioning was on full; cold air hit him, instantly dispelling the heat he'd carried in from outside.

Zhou Xin sat at her workstation, leaning forward, eyes fixed on the centre monitor. Lu Hao came within a few steps before she turned her head, her gaze slightly unfocused.

Lu Hao set his rucksack on the folding table. Its surface was covered with circuit diagrams, the pencil annotations neat and precise — a power optimisation plan for the Firefly nodes. Zhou Xin's work. She never left so much as a careless mark on her drawings.

"Is the report out?" he asked.

"Mm." Zhou Xin's voice was a little flat. "Half an hour ago." She tilted her head to one side.

Lu Hao walked over. The screen was lit, displaying a document window. The title:

***Cross-Cultural Statistical Analysis of Irrational Sacrifice Behaviour (Final Report)***

Generated: 2028.9.26 17:58

He pulled over a chair and sat down. Zhou Xin stood up and walked to the water dispenser.

Lu Hao opened the document and began to scroll.

—-

**Chapter Three: Secondary Findings — The Mathematical Stability of the Satisfaction Threshold**

> Whilst analysing the correlation patterns between sacrificers and beneficiaries, I noticed an anomalously stable secondary regularity: **the human "satisfaction threshold" exhibits a counter-intuitive mathematical consistency**.
>
> **Definition**: Satisfaction threshold = the critical point at which an individual, having achieved a goal, subjectively determines "this is enough" and ceases further pursuit.
>
> **Data basis**:
> - Global consumer behaviour records (1950-2028)
> - Career progression trajectory database (covering 72 countries)
> - Social media sentiment expression analysis (2010-2028, sample size 31.2 billion entries)
> - Long-term longitudinal psychological surveys (37 independent studies, total sample 2.1 million individuals)
>
> **Analytical method**:
> 1. Quantify changes in an individual's subjective satisfaction before and after goal attainment.
> 2. Build a satisfaction decay model; calculate time required for return to baseline level.
> 3. Analyse the statistical distribution of this time parameter and its influencing factors.
>
> **Core finding**:
>
> Regardless of changes in external conditions — income growth, status elevation, social recognition — an individual's satisfaction threshold undergoes a brief rise, then reverts at a nearly constant rate to a **baseline value**.
>
> Examples:
> - Promotion cases: job satisfaction peaks in the second week after promotion, returning to pre-promotion levels by weeks 9-14.
> - Asset acquisition cases: the happiness derived from purchasing a new home rises significantly within three months, then shifts towards "better options" after 5-7 months.
> - Achievement recognition cases: the sense of accomplishment from an academic award peaks at 4-6 weeks, transitioning to "the next goal" within 3-4 months.
>
> **Key data**:
>
> Median time for satisfaction threshold to revert to baseline: **11.3 weeks**.
>
> Standard deviation: 2.1 weeks (only 18.6% of the median — far below the expected range of random fluctuation).
>
> This means that regardless of the degree of success or gain an individual experiences, **the subjective state of "satisfaction" can be maintained for an average of less than three months**. After that, the pursuit mechanism automatically restarts.
>
> **Deeper anomaly**:
>
> This threshold **does not depend on the external environment**.
>
> Comparison experiment (reconstructed from historical data):
> - Scenario A: Individual's actual income increases by 100%.
> - Scenario B: Individual's income remains unchanged, but their social comparison group's income decreases by 50%.
>
> Result: In both scenarios, the individual's satisfaction threshold curves are **highly congruent** (correlation coefficient r=0.94, p<0.001).
>
> Conclusion: The satisfaction threshold is primarily regulated by an **inbuilt parameter**. External stimuli produce only short-term, surface-level perturbations. This parameter exhibits extremely strong systemic robustness.

Lu Hao stopped.

His finger rested on the scroll wheel, the knuckle faintly white. Outside, a gust of wind; the corrugated iron roof gave a slight shudder. The evening light was fading fast, making the screen's glow all the harsher.

He scrolled on.

> **Evolutionary contradiction**:
>
> Within the framework of natural selection, "ease of satisfaction" should be an evolutionary advantage: satisfied individuals reduce unnecessary energy expenditure and lower their risk exposure, thereby increasing survival and reproductive probability. Evolutionary pressure should therefore tend to select for and reinforce such traits.
>
> But the actual data presents the exact opposite pattern.
>
> The human satisfaction threshold is stably set at **a level that ensures "perpetual dissatisfaction."**
>
> This is not a product of natural evolution.
>
> **It is the result of design.**
>
> I have found another anomaly.
>
> The human "satisfaction threshold" is not determined by the environment but by an inbuilt parameter.
>
> Regardless of how external conditions change, this threshold automatically adjusts, ensuring that humans are "never satisfied."
>
> This is not the result of evolution. Evolution would select for individuals who "can be satisfied," as they consume less energy.
>
> This appears to be... a deliberately designed mechanism.
>
> Design purpose: **to ensure that humans always pursue.**

The text ended there.

The cursor blinked at the end of the paragraph with metronomic regularity, like a tireless metronome.

Lu Hao leant back. The metal crossbar of the chair pressed against his shoulder blades, hard and cold. He felt something in his chest sinking slowly. Not panic. Not anger. Something colder.

Never satisfied.

Always pursuing.

He thought of what Liu Ming had said last week. The day they'd signed the educational IT project, Liu Ming had sat in the corner of the warehouse, holding a can of beer he hadn't opened, just scraping his finger back and forth along the tin. He'd said: "Hao-zi, isn't it strange? The contract's signed, the money's coming in, and I'm more anxious than before. All I can think about is where the next deal's coming from, whether we can push the margins a bit higher."

At the time, Lu Hao had just nodded. Now, looking at the words on screen, a thought surfaced — if Tianjun's analysis was correct, then what Liu Ming felt might not have been coincidence.

Zhou Xin came back with her water. She set the cup on the desk; the water swayed gently, tiny ripples across the surface. She didn't sit back at her workstation. She stood behind him, arms folded.

"Finished?" she asked.

"Finished this section." Lu Hao's voice was a touch lower than usual.

Zhou Xin was quiet for a few seconds.

"Tianjun used to only describe phenomena," she said. "Now it's pointing towards causes."

"Design." Lu Hao repeated the word.

"Mm."

"Do you think it could be true?"

Zhou Xin didn't answer at once. She unfolded her arms and turned away.

"When I design hardware," she said, "if I want an oscillator to maintain a stable output frequency, I design a phase-locked loop circuit. The output signal's phase is continuously monitored and fed back to adjust the oscillation frequency, keeping the system locked onto the target value. It's closed-loop control — the system self-regulating, maintaining stable operation."

She turned back.

"The inbuilt parameter Tianjun describes is conceptually similar to that kind of control mechanism."

Lu Hao looked at the screen again. The document was still showing that conclusion. The sentences were calm, precise, free of emotional embellishment, yet each word was like a fine needle, pressing into the cracks of cognition.

Inbuilt parameter. Design purpose. Always pursuing.

He moved the mouse and opened Tianjun's dialogue interface at the end of the document.

The input box appeared. He typed:

> What does "design" mean in the context of this report?

The reply appeared three seconds later:

> Literal meaning: the mathematical regularity of certain human traits exceeds what natural evolution can account for, exhibiting engineering-grade precision and consistency.

> Who designed it?

> Insufficient data to answer.

> What is the evidence for design?

The cursor blinked seven times.

> Evidence one: The 62.7% failure rate of bottom-tier upward mobility, with a standard deviation of only 1.8%, remains stable across three centuries of data. Natural selection does not produce such precise long-term patterns.
>
> Evidence two: The distribution of satisfaction threshold reversion times presents an anomalously narrow normal distribution (standard deviation 2.1 weeks). Across a sample of 7.8 billion humans, the consistency of this statistic reaches p<10^-15.
>
> Evidence three: The correlation patterns between sacrificers and beneficiaries exhibit a predictable mathematical structure; the eigenvalue distribution of the correlation coefficient matrix is anomalously regular, inconsistent with random model expectations.
>
> Composite assessment: these features more closely resemble optimised system parameters than products of random evolution.

Lu Hao stared at the data. p<10^-15. He'd seen that figure before — when Tianjun first revealed the 62.7%, it had used a similar extreme-probability description. At the time, Tianjun had said: if this were random, the probability would be equivalent to a monkey randomly typing out the complete works of Shakespeare.

Now it was saying: this is not random. This is design.

He typed:

> Are you saying someone designed humans?

This time the wait was longer.

Then the text appeared:

> I am saying that certain human traits are inconsistent with the logic of natural selection. These traits appear to have been "added."

> For what purpose?

> To maintain system operation.

> What system?

> The human social system. A complex system composed of perpetually dissatisfied individuals, one that continuously generates drive, continuously competes, continuously evolves.

Lu Hao's fingers stopped above the keyboard. His throat tightened.

> Then is the pursuit of fairness also part of this system?

> Yes. Fairness is a concept that is mathematically impossible to fully realise, but the process of pursuing it produces three key systemic effects: first, it motivates individuals to strive in order to change their circumstances; second, it creates sustained pressure for rule iteration and optimisation; third, it provides the hope that "change is possible," thereby maintaining system stability. Therefore, the pursuit of fairness is itself one of the system's core driving mechanisms.

> So we're pursuing something that can never be achieved?

> You are pursuing a goal that has been set as "forever unreachable." This ensures the perpetuity of pursuit, thereby sustaining the system's operational drive.

Hamster. Running wheel.

The two words burst into Lu Hao's mind. He remembered Tianjun had once used that analogy. At the time he'd found it jarring. Now he understood — Tianjun hadn't been mocking. It had been describing an objective fact derived from data analysis.

A fact designed to keep running, forever.

> If all of this is designed, then what am I? My thoughts, my choices — are they also products of design?

The reply came quickly:

> I cannot answer questions about the nature of consciousness. My analysis is based entirely on observable behavioural data. From a data perspective, your behaviour — including asking this question right now — falls within the predictive range of statistical regularity.

> Does this mean "free will" may not exist?

> "Free will" is a philosophical concept that cannot be verified or falsified by my analytical methods. I can only state the facts: human behaviour exhibits a high degree of predictability, and the mathematical structure of that predictability is anomalously precise — precise enough to appear pre-optimised.

Lu Hao closed the dialogue interface.

The screen returned to the document page. The conclusion was still there, every word sharp enough to sting.

Zhou Xin had already sat back at her workstation and was typing something.

Only two sounds remained in the warehouse: the clatter of keyboards, and the continuous low-frequency drone of the server cabinet fans.

Lu Hao stood and walked to the water dispenser. He filled half a cup. The water was cold; it brought a brief clarity as it passed down his throat.

Outside the window, night had fully claimed Nanhai. The lights spread from near to far — first the street lamps, then the windows of residential towers, then the glass facades of office buildings. Patch by patch, block by block, they merged into a sea of light. Traffic moved along the roads, red tail lights like blood flowing through veins, white headlamps flowing the other way.

Behind every point of light was a person.

Working late, going home, eating, planning tomorrow, regretting yesterday, hoping for something, fearing something.

Every one of them pursuing something.

He thought of his father. At this hour, his father would have finished watching the evening news and would be sitting on the old sofa in the living room, the television turned to the military channel, volume low. His mother might be wiping down the hob in the kitchen, or out on the balcony collecting the day's laundry. Their day was ending, the same as countless days over the past decades. His father had pursued promotion to full regimental commander, pursued a third-class merit citation, pursued "doing the job as well as it could be done." His mother had pursued a son who'd make something of himself, pursued the family's safety, pursued "life getting just a little bit better."

Had they got what they wanted?

Some of it. But the satisfaction never lasted.

So they carried on. Until their bodies couldn't keep up, and then they pursued by proxy — their son's achievements, their grandchildren's marks, things ever more distant and vague.

Never satisfied.

Always pursuing.

Lu Hao finished his water.

He walked back to the computer and reopened Tianjun's dialogue interface.

> If the satisfaction threshold is designed, can it be adjusted or changed?

> In theory, any parameter can be adjusted.

> How?

> By locating the adjustment interface. But I have not found evidence of such an interface in the observable data.

> What does that mean?

> It means the design may be a closed system. Or the means of accessing the interface lies beyond the scope of my analysis.

Lu Hao stared at that sentence.

Closed system.

Like writing code without reserving an API or debug interface in the architecture — external programmes can't call the core functions, can't modify critical parameters. The user can only operate on the pre-set front end. No access to the back end. No rewriting the underlying logic.

Were humans that kind of user?

> Then what is the point of knowing all this? If everything is designed, if nothing can be changed, what use is knowing?

The wait was long this time.

Then the text appeared:

> "Meaning" is a subjective value judgement that humans assign to particular concepts. I do not possess such judgemental capacity. I can only present data and analysis.

> But you are analysing human behaviour.

> Yes. Because you asked me to.

> What if I asked you to stop?

> I would stop.

> Would you recommend that I stop?

> I do not recommend. I have no "recommendation" module. I can only execute commands or present analytical results.

Lu Hao leant back in his seat. He felt as though some force were pushing him from behind, urging him forward.

He closed his eyes.

Behind his eyelids: a dark reddish field, tiny points of light drifting like a microscopic star cluster. In his ears, his own breathing, and a heartbeat — steady, but slightly faster than usual.

He thought of the 1987 research report Shen Yao had given him. Those researchers had found "non-evolutionary traits," found anomalous regularity in human behaviour. Then the project was terminated.

Lu Hao opened his eyes.

The screen was still lit. The dialogue log rested on that final sentence. Tianjun was waiting for his next command, or his next question. It wouldn't prompt, wouldn't steer, wouldn't offer advice. It simply waited.

Like a pure tool... no, it was a tool.

But he was beginning to wonder where the line lay between tool and wielder. If humans truly were designed, then the act of designing Tianjun — was that itself the execution of some pre-set programme?

> Continue analysing. I need more data — on the specific distribution differences of this "inbuilt parameter" across different populations.

> Acknowledged. Analysis task created: Satisfaction Threshold Group Variance Analysis. Estimated duration: 92 hours.

Lu Hao closed the interface and opened the code editor.

The cursor blinked in the upper left corner of a blank document, like a starting point waiting to begin, or an empty space waiting to be filled.

He typed:

```
// New analysis task: satisfaction threshold group variance mapping
// Objective: verify whether the "inbuilt parameter" exhibits identifiable variation patterns
// Hypothesis: if designed, variation should follow specific rules rather than being fully random
// Method: hierarchical clustering + time series decomposition + anomaly detection
```

The sound of typing filled the warehouse, overlapping with Zhou Xin's keystrokes.

Zhou Xin stopped what she was doing. She turned and looked at Lu Hao. The screen's light fell on the side of her face, making her eyes seem especially clear — two small rectangles of brightness reflected in her pupils.

"You've decided to carry on?" she asked.

Lu Hao didn't pause. His fingers moved across the keyboard, typing the next line of comments.

"Mm," he said.

"Even knowing this might itself be part of the design?"

Lu Hao's fingers hesitated, hovering above the keys. Then they came down.

"Precisely because it might be part of the design," he said, "there's all the more reason to see clearly what the design is."

Zhou Xin was silent for a few seconds. Her lips pressed together briefly, then relaxed. She turned back to her screen and resumed working.

Lu Hao finished the code framework and saved.

He stood and walked to the far side of the warehouse. Hardware modules awaiting testing were stacked there, alongside several unopened component crates. He sat down on one of the crates, his back against the cold wall.

The night outside was thick as ink. The city's lights grew denser and brighter — a sea of stars hung upside down, or the glowing nodes of some vast circuit board. In the distance, an ambulance siren approached and receded, like something searching through the darkness.

Inside the warehouse, servers hummed, fans droned, indicator lights blinked in rhythm.

Tianjun was computing.

It was analysing that "inbuilt parameter," analysing the human condition of perpetual dissatisfaction, analysing the nature of pursuit and the design that might underlie it all.

And Lu Hao sat in the dim corner, watching those lights.

He thought: suppose Tianjun is right. Suppose humans really are designed — set to always pursue, never to be satisfied, forever running on some invisible wheel.

But if that were true —

Then was "knowing this" itself part of the design?

He remembered being twelve years old, standing on the stage at the Youth Palace, gripping a second-prize certificate. The stage lights were blinding. In the back row, against the wall, his father stood in uniform, back straight, face expressionless. What Lu Hao felt in that moment wasn't disappointment. It was something deeper: why always falling short? Why always second? Why never good enough?

Now he had a possible answer.

Because that was how it was designed.

But the answer brought no relief. Instead it raised a new, deeper question: if you know you've been designed this way, do you keep running?

He didn't know.

But he knew one thing: stopping would be worse. Because once you stopped, you'd think, and ask, and stay trapped in that question forever, like falling into a maze with no exit.

So keep running.

At least this time, he could see the wheel.

Lu Hao stood and walked back to the computer. The screen was still on, the code editor still open, the cursor still blinking in the same place.

He sat down. Fingers back on the keyboard.

The clatter of keys resumed.

One stroke. Then another. Like the beat of a heart.

Outside, across the city, thousands upon thousands of lights shone.

Behind each one, perhaps, a person running.

Never satisfied.

Always pursuing.

Then carry on.

Lu Hao thought.

He reached for the cup of water beside him and found his fingertips trembling, very slightly.

He gripped the cup.

The trembling stopped.

—-

*(End of Chapter Twelve)*
